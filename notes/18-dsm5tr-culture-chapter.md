## 1. The chapter's overall structure — VERIFIED verbatim

The DSM-5-TR Section III chapter "Culture and Psychiatric Diagnosis" (pp. 859–879) was substantially revised from the DSM-5 (2013) chapter of the same name. The chapter opens by declaring its three-part structure verbatim:

> "This chapter provides basic information on integrating culture and social context in clinical diagnoses, with sections on key terms, cultural formulation, and cultural concepts of distress." (DSM-5-TR, p. 859)

The three sections are:

1. **Key Terms** — definitions of culture, race, and ethnicity
2. **Cultural Formulation** — the Outline for Cultural Formulation (OCF) and the Cultural Formulation Interview (CFI)
3. **Cultural Concepts of Distress** — the trichotomy of idioms, explanations, and syndromes that replaced "culture-bound syndromes"

The chapter's central conceptual claim, in its own words:

> "Symptom presentations, interpretations of the illness or predicament that precipitates care, and help-seeking expectations are always influenced by individuals' cultural backgrounds and sociocultural contexts. A person-centered cultural assessment can help improve the care of every individual, regardless of his or her background. Cultural formulation may be especially helpful for individuals who are affected by healthcare disparities driven by systemic disadvantage and discrimination." (DSM-5-TR, p. 859)

This is a structural shift from DSM-IV's "Outline for Cultural Formulation" (which was an appendix-level supplement) to DSM-5/DSM-5-TR's mainstreaming of cultural assessment as an integral part of psychiatric practice. The 2022 text revision deepened this by incorporating the work of two new groups: the Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group (co-chaired by Roberto Lewis-Fernández and Danielle Hairston) and the Cross-Cutting Culture Review Group (chaired by Lewis-Fernández).

## 2. What culture is, per DSM-5-TR — VERIFIED verbatim

The chapter's full Key-Terms definitions, quoted from the manual:

> "Culture refers to systems of knowledge, concepts, values, norms, and practices that are learned and transmitted across generations. Culture includes language, religion and spirituality, family structures, life-cycle stages, ceremonial rituals, customs, and ways of understanding health and illness, as well as moral, political, economic, and legal systems. Cultures are open, dynamic systems that undergo continuous change over time; in the contemporary world, most individuals and groups are exposed to multiple cultural contexts, which they use to fashion their own identities and make sense of experience. This process of meaning-making derives from developmental and everyday social experiences in specific contexts, including health care, which may vary for each individual. Much of culture involves background knowledge, values, and assumptions that remain implicit or presumed and so may be difficult for individuals to describe. These features of culture make it crucial not to overgeneralize cultural information or stereotype groups in terms of fixed cultural traits. In relation to diagnosis, it is essential to recognize that all forms of illness and distress, including the DSM disorders, are shaped by cultural contexts. Culture influences how individuals fashion their identities, as well as how they interpret and respond to symptoms and illness." (DSM-5-TR, p. 860)

> "Race is a social, not a biological, construct that divides humanity into groups based on a variety of superficial physical traits such as skin color that have been falsely viewed as indicating attributes and capacities assumed to be inherent to the group. Racial categories and constructs have varied over history and across societies and have been used to justify systems of oppression, slavery, and genocide. The construct of race is important for psychiatry because it can lead to racial ideologies, racism, discrimination, and social oppression and exclusion, which have strong negative effects on mental health. There is evidence that racism can exacerbate many psychiatric disorders, contributing to poor outcome, and that racial biases can affect diagnostic assessment." (DSM-5-TR, p. 860)

> "Ethnicity is a culturally constructed group identity used to define peoples and communities. It may be rooted in a common history, ancestry, geography, language, religion, or other shared characteristics of a group, which distinguish that group from others. Ethnicity may be self-assigned or attributed by outsiders. Increasing mobility, intermarriage, and intermixing of cultural groups have defined new mixed, multiple, or hybrid ethnic identities. These processes may also lead to the dilution of ethnic identification." (DSM-5-TR, p. 860)

> "Culture, race, and ethnicity may be related to political, economic, and social structural inequities associated with racism and discrimination resulting in health disparities. Cultural, ethnic, and racialized identities can be sources of strength and group support that enhance resilience. They may also lead to psychological, interpersonal, and intergenerational conflict or difficulties in adaptation that require socially and culturally informed diagnosis and clinical assessment." (DSM-5-TR, p. 860)

Three commitments follow from these definitions:

- **Culture is not ethnicity alone.** Culture is a system of knowledge, values, and practices learned and transmitted across generations; it includes religion, spirituality, life-cycle stages, ritual, and moral/political/economic/legal systems.
- **Culture is not static.** "Cultures are open, dynamic systems that undergo continuous change over time" (DSM-5-TR, p. 860). The same individual occupies multiple cultural positions simultaneously.
- **Culture is not "the patient's problem."** The CFI explicitly names the cultural background of the health care providers and "the values and assumptions embedded in the organization and practices of health care systems and institutions that may affect the clinical interaction" (DSM-5-TR, p. 863) as a target of cultural assessment.

**Connection to the framework:** this matches the framework's relational-ontology and multi-criteria positioning. We do not need to argue against DSM-5-TR's culture concept; we can argue *from* it. The chapter's culture concept is sophisticated; the implementation gap is what critical scholarship targets.

## 3. Cultural concepts of distress — VERIFIED verbatim

DSM-5 (2013) replaced DSM-IV's "culture-bound syndromes" with a three-part taxonomy that DSM-5-TR retains. The chapter's own definition, quoted in full:

> "The term cultural concepts of distress refers to ways that individuals experience, understand, and communicate suffering, behavioral problems, or troubling thoughts and emotions. Three main types of cultural concepts of distress may be distinguished. Cultural idioms of distress are ways of expressing distress that may not involve specific symptoms or syndromes, but that provide collective, shared ways of experiencing and talking about personal or social concerns. For example, everyday talk about 'nerves' or 'depression' may refer to widely varying forms of suffering without mapping onto a discrete set of symptoms, syndrome, or disorder. Cultural explanations or perceived causes are labels, attributions, or features of an explanatory model that indicate culturally recognized meaning or etiology for symptoms, illness, or distress. Cultural syndromes are clusters of symptoms and attributions that tend to co-occur among individuals in specific cultural groups, communities, or contexts and that are recognized locally as coherent patterns of experience." (DSM-5-TR, p. 872)

The three definitions in tabular form (VERBATIM from DSM-5-TR p. 872; the Lewis-Fernández/Kirmayer 2019 wording on "cultural syndromes" matches the manual exactly, while their wording on "cultural idioms" is shorter than the manual's):

| Concept | DSM-5-TR Definition (p. 872) |
|---|---|
| **Cultural idioms of distress** | "ways of expressing distress that may not involve specific symptoms or syndromes, but that provide collective, shared ways of experiencing and talking about personal or social concerns" |
| **Cultural explanations or perceived causes** | "labels, attributions, or features of an explanatory model that indicate culturally recognized meaning or etiology for symptoms, illness, or distress" |
| **Cultural syndromes** | "clusters of symptoms and attributions that tend to co-occur among individuals in specific cultural groups, communities, or contexts and that are recognized locally as coherent patterns of experience" |

The chapter explicitly names why "culture-bound syndrome" was abandoned:

> "Specifically, the term culture-bound syndrome ignores the fact that clinically important cultural differences often involve explanations or experience of distress rather than culturally distinctive configurations of symptoms. Furthermore, the term culture bound overemphasizes the extent to which cultural concepts of distress are characterized by highly idiosyncratic experiences that are restricted to specific geographic regions. The current formulation acknowledges that all forms of distress are locally shaped, including the DSM disorders. From this perspective, many DSM diagnoses can be understood as operationalized prototypes that started out as cultural syndromes and became widely accepted as a result of their clinical and research utility." (DSM-5-TR, pp. 872–873)

This last sentence is load-bearing for the cross-civilizational chapter: DSM-5-TR explicitly classifies its own diagnostic categories as cultural products that achieved generalization through utility. The conceptual move: distress is not the same thing as symptom, symptom is not the same thing as disease, and disease-as-named is not the same thing as illness-as-experienced. This is a direct lineage from Kleinman's 1980 illness/disease distinction.

**Examples** (selected from the DSM-5 Glossary of Cultural Concepts of Distress, GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE at example level):
- *Ataque de nervios* — Caribbean Latino syndrome
- *Khyâl cap* / "wind attacks" — Cambodian
- *Kufungisisa* — Shona "thinking too much" idiom
- *Maladi moun* / "humanly caused illness" — Haitian
- *Susto* — Latin American "fright sickness"
- *Taijin kyofusho* — Japanese interpersonal-fear syndrome
- *Shenjing shuairuo* — Chinese "weakness of the nervous system"

The DSM-5-TR retains this glossary in its appendix and refers to it from Section III.

**Connection to the framework:** this directly evidences the position that meaning-of-suffering varies across traditions. Our cross-civilizational chapter cited Buddhist *dukkha*, Confucian relational dysfunction, and Hindu *kleśa* as cases where suffering is named and addressed within a tradition's own ontology. DSM-5-TR's cultural concepts-of-distress framework is the mainstream-Western-psychiatric *acknowledgment* that this variation is real and clinically relevant. We can cite DSM-5-TR rather than only cite ourselves.

## 4. The Cultural Formulation Interview (CFI) — VERIFIED verbatim

The CFI's purpose, in the manual's own words:

> "The Cultural Formulation Interview (CFI) is a set of protocols that clinicians may use to obtain information during a mental health assessment about the impact of culture on key aspects of an individual's clinical presentation and care. The CFI consists of three components: the core CFI, a set of 16 questions that can be used to obtain an initial assessment from any individual; an Informant version of the core CFI to obtain collateral information; and a set of Supplementary modules to expand the evaluation as needed." (DSM-5-TR, p. 863)

The CFI's working definition of "culture" (which extends the chapter's Key-Terms definition into the operational interview):

> "In the CFI, the term culture includes:
> - The processes through which individuals assign meaning to experience, drawing from the values, orientations, knowledge, and practices of the diverse social groups (e.g., ethnic groups, faith groups, occupational groups, veterans' groups) and communities in which they participate.
> - Aspects of individuals' background, developmental experiences, and current social contexts and position that affect their perspective, such as age, gender, social class, geographic origin, migration, language, religion, sexual orientation, disability, or ethnic or racialized background.
> - The influence of family, friends, and other community members (particularly, the individual's social network) on the individual's illness experience.
> - The cultural background of the health care providers and the values and assumptions embedded in the organization and practices of health care systems and institutions that may affect the clinical interaction." (DSM-5-TR, p. 863)

The CFI's person-centered design rationale, verbatim:

> "The CFI is a brief semistructured interview for systematically assessing cultural factors relevant to the care of any individual. The CFI focuses on the individual's experience and the social contexts of the clinical problem, symptoms, or concerns. The CFI follows a person-centered approach to cultural assessment by eliciting information from the individual about his or her own views and those of others in his or her social network. This approach is designed to avoid stereotyping, in that each individual's cultural knowledge affects how he or she interprets illness experience and guides how he or she seeks help. Because the CFI concerns the individual's personal views, there are no right or wrong answers to these questions." (DSM-5-TR, p. 863)

The four-domain structure, verbatim from the manual:

> "The core CFI emphasizes four domains of assessment: Cultural Definition of the Problem (questions 1–3); Cultural Perceptions of Cause, Context, and Support (questions 4–10); Cultural Factors Affecting Self-Coping and Past Help Seeking (questions 11–13); and Cultural Factors Affecting Current Help Seeking (questions 14–16)." (DSM-5-TR, p. 864)

| Domain | Questions | Focus (per DSM-5-TR p. 864) |
|---|---|---|
| **Cultural Definition of the Problem** | 1–3 | How the individual names and frames the problem |
| **Cultural Perceptions of Cause, Context, and Support** | 4–10 | Causes, stressors and supports, role of cultural identity |
| **Cultural Factors Affecting Self-Coping and Past Help Seeking** | 11–13 | Self-coping, past help seeking, barriers |
| **Cultural Factors Affecting Current Help Seeking** | 14–16 | Preferences, clinician-patient relationship |

### 4.1 The 16 Core CFI Questions — VERBATIM

All 16 questions are reproduced exactly as printed in the DSM-5-TR (pp. 864–868). The chapter introduces the interview with this scripted preamble for the patient:

> "INTRODUCTION FOR THE INDIVIDUAL: I would like to understand the problems that bring you here so that I can help you more effectively. I want to know about your experience and ideas. I will ask some questions about what is going on and how you are dealing with it. Please remember there are no right or wrong answers." (DSM-5-TR, p. 864)

**CULTURAL DEFINITION OF THE PROBLEM (Questions 1–3):**

> "1. What brings you here today?
>
>    [If individual gives few details or only mentions symptoms or a medical diagnosis, probe:] People often understand their problems in their own way, which may be similar to or different from how doctors describe the problem. How would you describe your problem?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 864)

> "2. Sometimes people have different ways of describing their problem to their family, friends, or others in their community. How would you describe your problem to them?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 864)

> "3. What troubles you most about your problem?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 864)

**CULTURAL PERCEPTIONS OF CAUSE, CONTEXT, AND SUPPORT (Questions 4–10):**

> "4. Why do you think this is happening to you? What do you think are the causes of your [PROBLEM]?
>
>    [Prompt further if required:] Some people may explain their problem as the result of bad things that happen in their life, problems with others, a physical illness, a spiritual reason, or many other causes." (DSM-5-TR, p. 865)

> "5. What do others in your family, your friends, or others in your community think is causing your [PROBLEM]?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 865)

> "6. Are there any kinds of support that make your [PROBLEM] better, such as support from family, friends, or others?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 865)

> "7. Are there any kinds of stresses that make your [PROBLEM] worse, such as difficulties with money, or family problems?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 865)

(Question 8 is preceded by a scripted introduction:) > "Sometimes, aspects of people's background or identity can make their [PROBLEM] better or worse. By background or identity, I mean, for example, the communities you belong to, the languages you speak, where you or your family are from, your race or ethnic background, your gender or sexual orientation, or your faith or religion." (DSM-5-TR, p. 865)

> "8. For you, what are the most important aspects of your background or identity?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 865)

> "9. Are there any aspects of your background or identity that make a difference to your [PROBLEM]?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 865)

> "10. Are there any aspects of your background or identity that are causing other concerns or difficulties for you?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 865)

**CULTURAL FACTORS AFFECTING SELF-COPING AND PAST HELP SEEKING (Questions 11–13):**

> "11. Sometimes people have various ways of dealing with problems like [PROBLEM]. What have you done on your own to cope with your [PROBLEM]?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 865)

> "12. Often, people look for help from many different sources, including different kinds of doctors, helpers, or healers. In the past, what kinds of treatment, help, advice, or healing have you sought for your [PROBLEM]?
>
>    [Probe if does not describe usefulness of help received:] What types of help or treatment were most useful? Not useful?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 866)

> "13. Has anything prevented you from getting the help you need?
>
>    [Probe as needed:] For example, money, work or family commitments, stigma or discrimination, or lack of services that understand your language or background?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 866)

**CULTURAL FACTORS AFFECTING CURRENT HELP SEEKING (Questions 14–16):**

(Question 14 is preceded by:) > "Now let's talk some more about the help you need." (DSM-5-TR, p. 867)

> "14. What kinds of help do you think would be most useful to you at this time for your [PROBLEM]?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 867)

> "15. Are there other kinds of help that your family, friends, or other people have suggested would be helpful for you now?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 867)

(Question 16 is preceded by a scripted introduction:) > "Sometimes doctors and patients misunderstand each other because they come from different backgrounds or have different expectations." (DSM-5-TR, p. 867)

> "16. Have you been concerned about this and is there anything that we can do to provide you with the care you need?" (DSM-5-TR, p. 867)

### 4.3 Connection to the framework

The CFI's structure mirrors the framework's prophetic-function mechanism in one important way — it builds in *standardized space* for the individual's own meaning-making, rather than treating the clinician's interpretation as authoritative by default. The clinician's interpretation is checked against the patient's self-report. This is a structural anti-hubris device. We argued for analogous structural devices in the AI deployment context.

The CFI also has a notable structural limitation that critics have pressed: it asks the patient about culture but does not ask the clinician about *their own* culture in any structurally enforced way. The clinician-patient relationship question (Q16 core / Q17 Informant) elicits the patient's perception of the relationship, not the clinician's self-audit of theirs. This is one of the framework-drift risks the character-over-compliance lens specifically addresses.

## 5. The Outline for Cultural Formulation (OCF) — VERIFIED verbatim

The OCF is the *narrative-synthesis* document that the clinician writes after collecting CFI data. It is older than the CFI: the OCF originates in DSM-IV (1994); the CFI was added in DSM-5 (2013) as the operational data-collection tool that *feeds* the OCF. The DSM-5-TR introduces the OCF this way:

> "The Outline for Cultural Formulation introduced in DSM-IV provided a framework for assessing information about cultural features of an individual's mental health problem and how it relates to a social and cultural context and history. This assessment provides useful information on social context and illness experience relevant to the assessment of every individual, not only those whose cultural background may be unfamiliar to the clinician. Updated from DSM-5, DSM-5-TR includes an expanded version of the Outline and an approach to assessment using the Cultural Formulation Interview (CFI), which has been field-tested among clinicians, patients, and accompanying relatives and found to be a feasible, acceptable, and useful cultural assessment tool." (DSM-5-TR, pp. 860–861)

The OCF's five components, quoted in full from the manual (DSM-5-TR, pp. 861–862):

> **"Cultural identity of the individual:** Describe the individual's demographic (e.g., age, gender, ethnoracial background) or other socially and culturally defined characteristics that may influence interpersonal relationships, access to resources, and developmental and current challenges, conflicts, or predicaments. Other clinically relevant aspects of identity may include religious affiliation and spirituality, socioeconomic class, caste, personal and family places of birth and growing up, migrant status, occupation, and sexual orientation, among others. Note which aspects of identity are prioritized by the individual and how they interact (intersectionality), which may reflect the influence of clinical setting and health concerns. For migrants, the degree and kinds of involvement with both the cultural contexts of origin and the new cultural contexts should be noted. Similarly, for individuals who identify with racialized and ethnic groups, the degree of interaction and identification with their own group and other segments of society should be noted. Language abilities, preferences, and patterns of use are relevant for identifying difficulties with access to care, social integration, and clinical communication or the need for an interpreter."

> **"Cultural concepts of distress:** Describe the cultural constructs that influence how the individual experiences, understands, and communicates his or her symptoms or problems to others. These constructs include cultural idioms of distress, cultural explanations or perceived causes, and cultural syndromes. The level of severity and meaning of the distressing experiences should be assessed in relation to the norms of the individual's cultural background. Priority symptoms, perceived seriousness of the illness, the level of associated stigma, and anticipated outcomes are all relevant. Elicit the individual's and family's or friends' help-seeking expectations and plans, as well as patterns of self-coping and their connection to the individual's cultural concepts of distress, including past help-seeking experiences. Assessment of coping and help-seeking patterns should consider the use of professional as well as traditional, alternative, or complementary sources of care."

> **"Psychosocial stressors and cultural features of vulnerability and resilience:** Identify key stressors, challenges, and supports in the individual's social environment (which may include both local and distant events). These include social determinants of the individual's mental health such as access to resources (e.g., housing, transportation) and opportunities (e.g., education, employment); exposure to racism, discrimination, and systemic institutional stigmatization; and social marginalization or exclusion (structural violence). Also assess the role of religion, family, and other interpersonal relationships and social networks (e.g., friends, neighbors, coworkers, online forums or groups) in causing stress or providing emotional, instrumental, and informational support. Social stressors and social supports vary with social context, family structure, developmental tasks, and the cultural meaning of events. Levels of functioning, disability, and resilience should be assessed in light of the individual's cultural background."

> **"Cultural features of the relationship between the individual and the clinician, treatment team, and institution:** Identify differences in cultural background, language, education, and social status among other aspects of identity between an individual and clinician (or the treatment team and institution) that may cause difficulties in communication and may influence diagnosis and treatment. Considering the ways that individuals and clinicians are positioned socially and perceive each other in terms of social categories may influence the assessment process. Experiences of racism and discrimination in the larger society may impede establishing trust and safety in the clinical diagnostic encounter. Effects may include problems eliciting symptoms, misunderstanding of the cultural and clinical significance of symptoms and behaviors, and difficulty establishing or maintaining the rapport needed for accurate assessment and an effective clinical alliance."

> **"Overall cultural assessment:** Summarize the implications of the components of the cultural formulation identified in earlier sections of the Outline for the differential diagnosis of mental disorders and other clinically relevant issues or problems, as well as appropriate management and treatment intervention." (DSM-5-TR, pp. 861–862)

Note that components 1–4 of the OCF correspond loosely to the CFI's four data-collection domains; component 5 is the synthesis — the clinician's narrative formulation tying together what was learned across the first four. Notably, the OCF's fourth component is broader than the CFI's Q16 in scope: the OCF directs the clinician to assess differences in cultural background between the individual and *clinician, treatment team, and institution*, opening the door to clinician self-audit at the formulation stage even if the structured interview itself does not enforce one.

**The OCF/CFI distinction in one sentence:** the CFI is the structured interview that produces the data; the OCF is the synthesized narrative formulation the clinician writes from that data. Both live in DSM-5-TR Section III.

## 6. The 12 CFI Supplementary Modules — partially VERIFIED

The DSM-5-TR print chapter does not enumerate all 12 modules by name. It introduces them this way:

> "Supplementary modules have been developed that expand on each domain of the core CFI and guide clinicians who wish to explore these domains in greater depth. Supplementary modules have also been developed for specific populations, such as children and adolescents, elderly individuals, caregivers, and immigrants and refugees. These supplementary modules are referenced in the core CFI under the pertinent subheadings and are available online at www.psychiatry.org/dsm5." (DSM-5-TR, p. 864)

Module *names* are also referenced inline in the core CFI text alongside each domain, in parentheses. From those parentheticals (verbatim from DSM-5-TR pp. 864–867), the following modules are explicitly named in the manual: Explanatory Model, Level of Functioning, Social Network, Caregivers, Psychosocial Stressors, Religion and Spirituality, Immigrants and Refugees, Cultural Identity, Older Adults, Coping and Help Seeking, Children and Adolescents, Clinician-Patient Relationship — twelve modules in total, matching the count cited in the secondary literature.

The supplementary modules are optional structured extensions that allow deeper investigation of specific domains or specific populations. The full 12 (module names VERIFIED inline against the core-CFI parentheticals in DSM-5-TR pp. 864–867; the grouping below comes from the psychiatry.org supplementary modules document referenced by multiple academic sources):

**Eight that deepen core CFI domains:**
1. Explanatory Model
2. Level of Functioning
3. Social Network
4. Psychosocial Stressors
5. Spirituality, Religion, and Moral Traditions
6. Cultural Identity
7. Coping and Help-Seeking
8. Patient-Clinician Relationship

**Three for population-specific contexts:**
9. School-Age Children and Adolescents
10. Older Adults
11. Immigrants and Refugees

**One for caregivers' own perspectives:**
12. Caregivers

The architectural pattern is "telescoping" — the core CFI is run; if a domain warrants deeper investigation, the relevant module is run as an extension. This matches a principle the scaffolding spec articulates: depth-on-demand rather than depth-by-default, with structured pathways into deeper engagement when context warrants.

**Module 5 (Spirituality, Religion, Moral Traditions)** is the most directly relevant to the cross-civilizational ethics argument. DSM-5-TR's inclusion of a structured module for spiritual/religious/moral-tradition assessment within its diagnostic apparatus is institutional acknowledgment that meaning-of-suffering frameworks rooted in religious and moral traditions are clinically relevant — not relics, not optional, not "patient eccentricities." This is not a small concession. It is the mainstream-Western-psychiatric institution explicitly building in a slot for the kind of tradition the framework engages.

## 7. The chapter's stance on universality vs. particularity — partially VERIFIED, contested

The chapter's *stated* position: most major mental disorders show cross-cultural prevalence, but their expression, meaning, course, and help-seeking patterns vary substantially across cultural contexts. Diagnostic categories are presumed broadly universal at the syndrome level; cultural variation operates at the level of expression and meaning.

**Bredström's critique** (PMC6677698, VERIFIED): the chapter says one thing in Section III and does another in Section II. Section III articulates a sophisticated dynamic-culture concept; Section II's diagnostic criteria treat certain symptoms as universal (e.g., "shortness of breath" for panic disorder counts toward diagnosis) and others as culturally specific (e.g., "uncontrollable crying" relegated to a footnote that explicitly excludes it from diagnostic-criterion counting). The asymmetry implies that *Western*-typical symptoms are what counts, and other-cultural symptoms are decoration.

**Hinton & Lewis-Fernández (2011)** raised the same concern at the diagnostic-category level, particularly for PTSD. Cross-cultural research showed substantial variation in trauma-reaction patterns; PTSD's DSM criteria capture a subset of these. Some cultural trauma-reaction idioms (somatic-focal, dissociative-focal, possession-form) are not well captured by DSM PTSD criteria. The Section III framework acknowledges this; the Section II PTSD criteria do not yet reflect it.

**Connection to the framework:** this is exactly the *partial-acknowledgment, partial-implementation* pattern the religion/expansion-contraction analysis documented. The cultural psychiatry community pressed for a sophisticated culture concept; the institution adopted the concept in Section III; the diagnostic apparatus in Section II largely retains the previous structure. This is not failure — it is the slow institutional process of expanding moral and epistemic circles. The framework describes this dynamic and gives organizations mechanisms to detect when stated principles have not yet translated into operational practice. DSM-5-TR is a real-world case of partial translation — useful for the case-studies paper.

## 8. The clinician's culture as a variable — VERIFIED conceptually, partially implemented

The chapter explicitly names the clinician's culture as a relevant variable. The CFI's Module 8 (Patient-Clinician Relationship) and the OCF's component 4 operationalize this. The DSM-5-TR's *Attention to Culture, Racism, and Discrimination* supplement extends this further by addressing how clinician implicit bias and structural racism shape diagnostic encounters.

The Bredström critique still applies: the clinician's self-audit is not structurally enforced in the way the patient interview is. Module 8 elicits the patient's perception of the relationship; it does not require the clinician to produce a parallel self-assessment. This gap is one the character-over-compliance framework would explicitly close — clinician self-audit is a structural prophetic-function mechanism, not an optional clinical-curiosity item.

## 9. Intersectional considerations — VERIFIED in DSM-5-TR

DSM-5-TR explicitly uses "intersectionality" terminology. Race is treated as socially constructed (the chapter uses "racialized" rather than "racial" in many places to mark this). Gender, sexual orientation, disability, migration status, and class are named as cultural-context variables that intersect with ethnoracial identity to shape illness experience and clinical presentation.

The terminology shifts in DSM-5-TR are deliberate (VERIFIED):
- "Racialized" replaces "racial" in many contexts
- "Ethnoracial" denotes U.S. Census-style combined ethnic-and-racialized categories
- "Minority" and "non-White" are avoided because they describe groups in relation to a presumed "majority" and reproduce hierarchy

This is one of the most substantive 2022-revision changes in the chapter and represents the Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group's explicit influence.

## 10. What DSM-5-TR specifically updated from DSM-5 in this chapter — VERIFIED at structural level

1. The chapter title was retained but the chapter itself was substantially rewritten by the Cross-Cutting Culture Review Group.
2. New supplementary document: *Attention to Culture, Racism, and Discrimination in DSM-5-TR* (a separate APA publication that operates alongside the main text).
3. Terminology shifts (racialized/ethnoracial/avoidance of minority/non-White as defaults).
4. Strengthened intersectional framing.
5. Explicit acknowledgment of structural racism's role in diagnostic disparities (e.g., in schizophrenia diagnosis among Black Americans).
6. The Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group reviewed *every chapter*, not just Section III. Cultural and ethnoracial considerations were inserted into Section II diagnostic descriptions where prevalence, presentation, or course differs.

This last point is significant: the 2022 revision pushed cultural awareness *into Section II*, partially addressing the Section-II-vs-Section-III asymmetry that Bredström critiqued. The implementation is partial, not complete.

## 11. Critical scholarship feeding the chapter — VERIFIED

**Arthur Kleinman** (*Patients and Healers in the Context of Culture*, 1980): the illness/disease distinction (illness = experiential and cultural; disease = biomedical-Western-categorical) and the explanatory-model framework (eight questions for eliciting how the patient understands their condition). Kleinman is the foundational ancestor of the entire DSM-5/DSM-5-TR cultural-formulation apparatus.

**Laurence Kirmayer** (McGill, *Culture and Mental Health* programs): cultural-psychiatry theorist whose work on idioms of distress, embodiment, and the limits of DSM categories shaped the cultural-concepts-of-distress trichotomy. Kirmayer has consistently argued that DSM categories should be read as Western cultural products, not as culture-neutral universals.

**Roberto Lewis-Fernández** (Columbia): chair of both the DSM-5 Cross-Cultural Issues Subgroup and the DSM-5-TR Culture-Related Issues Committee; co-chair of the Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group. The single most institutionally consequential figure in the DSM-5-TR cultural revisions. His 2019 paper with Kirmayer (*Cultural concepts of distress and psychiatric disorders*, Transcultural Psychiatry) is the canonical statement of the cultural-concepts trichotomy.

**Devon Hinton** (Harvard, MGH): trauma and cultural-syndromes research (*khyâl cap*, Cambodian refugee trauma); pressed for PTSD criteria revision to better capture cross-cultural trauma reactions. Co-author with Lewis-Fernández on the 2011 PTSD cross-cultural validity paper.

**Neil Krishan Aggarwal** (Columbia): operational-implementation research on the CFI, including barriers-to-implementation studies (PMC4299818) and CFI training effectiveness.

**Anna Bredström** (PMC6677698): contemporary critic; argues DSM-5's cultural sensitivity is contradictory between sections and that ethnocentric patterns persist despite the new framework.

## 12. Connection back to the framework

The framework makes three claims that DSM-5-TR Section III directly evidences or directly tensions:

**Claim evidenced:** that mainstream Western institutions themselves recognize cultural variation in suffering, meaning, and moral agency. DSM-5-TR's cultural-concepts-of-distress framework is mainstream-Western-psychiatric institutional acknowledgment that meaning-of-suffering varies across traditions and that this variation is clinically real, not exotic.

**Claim evidenced:** that institutions partially translate principles into practice, with the gap between stated principle and operational reality being the locus where character-over-compliance work must happen. DSM-5-TR Section III articulates the principle; Section II partially implements it; the cultural-psychiatry critical scholarship documents the gap.

**Claim tensioned:** that no diagnostic apparatus can be culture-neutral. The DSM aspires to broad cross-cultural validity at the syndrome level. The framework's position (and Kirmayer's) is more skeptical: diagnostic categories are themselves cultural products. Our book does not need to resolve this tension; it needs to acknowledge that DSM-5-TR has *moved toward* the cultural-particularity position without arriving there. The movement is what demonstrates institutional capacity for character-cultivation under critique.

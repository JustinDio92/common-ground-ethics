## 1. Plato's *Euthyphro* — the founding text

The dialogue (c. 399 BCE, set shortly before Socrates's trial) is short and focused. Socrates is at the court being prepared for his own trial; Euthyphro is there prosecuting his own father for homicide. Socrates, surprised that Euthyphro would prosecute his own father, asks him to explain what piety is — since Euthyphro seems confident he knows. Euthyphro offers several definitions, each of which Socrates dismantles. The third definition is what became the dilemma.

### Euthyphro's third definition (VERIFIED, Jowett translation)

Euthyphro defines piety as "that which is dear to the gods" — that is, piety is whatever the gods love. Socrates then poses the question that has borne his name for 2,400 years:

> **Socrates:** "The point which I should first wish to understand is whether the pious or holy is beloved by the gods because it is holy, or holy because it is beloved of the gods."

Euthyphro initially doesn't see the significance. Socrates presses, and Euthyphro eventually agrees:

> **Socrates:** "It is loved because it is holy, not holy because it is loved?"
>
> **Euthyphro:** "Yes."

Having gotten the concession, Socrates reveals the problem:

> **Socrates:** "Then that which is dear to the gods, Euthyphro, is not holy, nor is that which is holy loved of God, as you affirm; but they are two different things."

### The dilemma's structure

Reformulated for later theological contexts:

**Horn 1:** God commands X because X is good.
**Horn 2:** X is good because God commands it.

Horn 1 implies that goodness is prior to and independent of God's commands. Goodness has its own nature; God recognizes this nature and commands accordingly. This preserves the non-arbitrariness of morality but concedes that God is not the source of moral truth — God is a rational agent who recognizes moral truth, not the author of it.

Horn 2 implies that whatever God commands is good by definition. If God commanded cruelty, cruelty would be good. This preserves divine sovereignty but at the cost of making morality arbitrary. A God who could have commanded the opposite of what God did command would have made the opposite actions good.

**Neither horn is comfortable for classical theism** — which wants God to be the source of moral truth *and* not arbitrary. The entire history of philosophical-theological engagement with the question is an attempt to escape the dilemma without paying either price.

## 2. Aquinas's response — natural law (VERIFIED via SEP)

Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274) provides the most influential Christian response to the Euthyphro problem. His resolution operates through his natural-law framework in the *Summa Theologiae*, particularly I-II q. 90–97.

### The hierarchy of law (VERIFIED via SEP summary)

Aquinas distinguishes four kinds of law:

1. **Eternal law** — God's rational governance of all creation; the uncreated order.
2. **Natural law** — the rational creature's *participation* in eternal law, accessible through reason examining human nature and inclinations.
3. **Divine law** — revealed law (the content of Scripture), which confirms and clarifies natural law and adds truths beyond reason's reach (like *beatitudo*, the beatific vision).
4. **Human (positive) law** — human legislation that applies and extends natural law to specific societies.

Critical for the purposes: **natural law is accessible to reason independently of divine command.** Aquinas writes (per SEP summary of ST I-II q. 91 a. 2) that natural law represents "the rational creature's participation in eternal law" — meaning humans can, through properly-functioning reason examining human nature, access moral truth without needing revelation to tell them what is good.

### Aquinas's response to the Euthyphro dilemma

Aquinas's answer is essentially **Horn 1 with a theological gloss.** Actions are not right *because* commanded; they are right because they participate in eternal law, which is God's rational nature. God commands them *because* they are right, where "right" is constituted by conformity to rational human flourishing (*beatitudo*) which God, as creator, knows better than we do.

The gloss that avoids Horn 1's cost (making goodness independent of God): goodness is not external to God — it is God's own rational nature. Eternal law is not something God consults; it is what God is. So God commands the good not because the good is some external standard God must accept, but because the good is the rational structure of God's own being and creation.

**SEP's summary:** *"Moral principles (e.g., 'love your neighbor as yourself') are self-evident to properly functioning rational minds, requiring no divine command for their intelligibility."* The key phrase is "self-evident to properly functioning rational minds" — Aquinas is saying that reason, when it functions as it should, grasps moral truth directly. Revelation confirms and clarifies; it does not constitute.

### Why this matters for the framework

Aquinas's position is *structurally identical to the Mu'tazilite position* we documented in the Quran pass. Both say:
- Good is rationally knowable, independent of command
- God commands the good because it is good
- Reason is load-bearing for ethics

Aquinas dominated Catholic theology for centuries and remains a foundational reference in Catholic moral teaching. This means **the dominant tradition in Western Catholic Christianity is on the reasoned-ethics side of the Euthyphro question.** The Catholic natural-law position and the Mu'tazilite position are structurally the same answer, translated into different theological vocabularies.

When Protestant Reformers (Luther, Calvin, in some readings) emphasized divine sovereignty and the radical depravity of reason, they tilted toward voluntarism. But this is a *tilt within Western Christianity*, not the dominant historical position. The broader Christian tradition, including Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism, has operated on Aquinas's side of the question.

## 3. Ockham's voluntarism — the radical opposite (VERIFIED via SEP)

William of Ockham (c. 1287-1347) is the most rigorous medieval defender of the voluntarist position — Horn 2 taken seriously.

### Ockham's position (VERIFIED via SEP summary)

Ockham holds that **moral obligation is constituted by divine will**. The SEP entry summarizes: *"Certain things, namely certain intentions, become morally obligatory, permitted, or forbidden simply because God decrees so."*

How radical is this? The SEP gives a specific example: Ockham argues that the Israelites' taking of Egyptian property (Exodus 12:35-36, the "despoiling of the Egyptians") *"was not a matter of theft or plunder, but was morally permissible and indeed obligatory—because God had commanded it."*

Read carefully. Ockham is saying that what would, by ordinary moral reasoning, be theft, was morally obligatory because God commanded it. The action's moral status depends entirely on divine will. If God had commanded the opposite, the opposite would have been obligatory.

### Limits on Ockham's voluntarism

The SEP notes that Ockham's voluntarism is not absolute. He accepted that humans can have *"a 'science of morals' by virtue of which, through experience and the exercise of natural reason, we can come to some knowledge of moral principles outside positive human and divine law."*

So Ockham allows for reason-accessible moral knowledge in some form. But he holds that moral *obligation* — the binding quality that makes an action required rather than merely recommended — comes from divine command specifically. Reason can identify good actions; only divine command makes them obligatory.

### Why this matters

Ockham is the medieval articulation of the position that Ash'arite Sunni theology articulated (with different philosophical vocabulary) and that contemporary voluntarist DCT inherits. The influence on Luther and Calvin is direct — Reformation voluntarism traces genealogically through Ockham back into medieval nominalism.

**For the framework:** Ockham represents the *strongest* version of DCT to address. The refined critique (DCT as atrocity-permit) maps directly onto Ockham's Israelite-property example. If Ockham is right, then genocide-commands (1 Samuel 15), slavery-legislation (Leviticus 25), and spousal-discipline verses (Quran 4:34) are obligatory if commanded — their content doesn't matter for moral status. This is exactly the structural position the critique targets. Ockham is not a strawman; he is the most rigorous articulation of the position, and he explicitly endorses the bullet-bite.

## 4. Robert Adams — modified divine command theory (VERIFIED via SEP and IEP)

Robert Adams (contemporary American philosopher, major figures in Christian philosophy) proposed the most philosophically sophisticated modern defense of DCT. His modified theory attempts to escape the Euthyphro dilemma while preserving the theological motivation to ground ethics in God.

### Adams's key innovation (VERIFIED via SEP)

Adams distinguishes between the **concept** of moral obligation and the **property** that fulfills the concept's role. The concept of "morally wrong" is theism-neutral — believers and non-believers share it; they can mean the same thing when they call an action wrong. But the *property* that best fulfills the conceptual role, Adams argues, is "being contrary to the commands of a loving God."

The analogy Adams draws is to Kripke-Putnam necessary *a posteriori* truths. "Water is H₂O" is necessarily true, but it's not knowable a priori — we had to investigate water's chemical structure empirically. Similarly, Adams argues, "moral wrongness = contrary to the commands of a loving God" is necessarily true (if true at all) but not knowable a priori; it's discovered through theological investigation.

### The "loving God" modification (VERIFIED via IEP)

The critical innovation: Adams grounds morality not in bare divine will but in **the commands of a loving God.** This modification is intended to address the arbitrariness objection:

An action is wrong "if and only if it is contrary to God's will or commands (assuming God loves us)." The "assuming God loves us" clause does real work. A loving God cannot command cruelty-for-its-own-sake, because such commands would contradict loving-ness. So the arbitrariness worry — that God might command anything and it would be good — is blocked by the character-constraint built into "loving."

### What Adams preserves and concedes

**Preserves:** ethical objectivity grounded in God; a Kripkean metaphysical identity between moral wrongness and contrary-to-loving-God-commands; theological motivation (believers can ground ethics in God without arbitrariness).

**Concedes:** the "loving" constraint is doing load-bearing work. If "loving" is substantive (actually specifies a character — not commanding cruelty, caring for creation, etc.), then Adams has smuggled in a character-constraint on God that is prior to the command-structure. If "loving" is just formal (whatever-God-is-is-loving), then the modification fails to block arbitrariness.

Critics (including secular critics and some theistic critics) argue Adams faces a fork:
- If "loving" is substantive, then Adams has conceded that a character-property is prior to divine command — which is essentially Aquinas's position with theological decoration, not voluntarism proper
- If "loving" is merely formal, the modification fails

### Why this matters for the framework

Adams is the strongest contemporary DCT defense. **The refined critique specifically addresses Adams's move.** We argue:

1. The "loving God" modification concedes that a character-property is prior to divine command. That is *exactly* what we've been arguing throughout — that character is the deeper category and command is secondary.

2. Adams's position is therefore closer to the position than it looks. If Adams concedes that God must be loving for the theory to work, then Adams has conceded that goodness has content prior to God's commands — content constrained by what loving is.

3. The coherent secular version of Adams's position is simply: **morality is grounded in what a loving agent would command / approve / endorse.** You don't need God for this; you need the concept of a loving agent. Any sufficiently idealized loving agent would command what a benevolent reasonable agent would command. That's a substantive ethics without needing the divine-command scaffolding.

**In other words:** Adams's modified DCT is the framework wearing Kripkean theological clothes. If you remove the theological clothes, what's left is character-based ethics — which is what we've been arguing.

## 5. Contemporary DCT defenders — the broader field

### Philip Quinn (VERIFIED via SEP)

Quinn defended theological voluntarism in a "restricted" form — applying to obligation-family properties (obligatory, permissible, right) but not to goodness-family properties (goodness, virtue). This is a significant concession: Quinn agrees that *goodness* is not constituted by divine command; he holds that *obligation* specifically is.

The move is smart but faces an incoherence challenge (as SEP notes): if goodness is not constituted by command, why is obligation? The two are tightly connected in ordinary moral reasoning. A distinction between them that grounds different meta-ethics for each requires defending.

### William Alston (VERIFIED via SEP and IEP)

Alston argues that God's goodness is metaphysically distinct from moral obligation, allowing both horns of the Euthyphro dilemma to be grasped simultaneously. God is good in a sense that doesn't reduce to "commands what is good," and God's commands constitute obligations in a sense that doesn't reduce to "commands what is independently good."

This is philosophically intricate but arguably threads a genuine needle. The cost is that "goodness" as applied to God and "goodness" as applied to ordinary moral judgment become subtly different concepts, which critics argue makes the theory less useful for grounding ordinary moral reasoning.

### William Lane Craig (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE)

Craig is the most prominent contemporary apologist defending a form of divine command theory, drawing on Adams's loving-God modification. Craig is philosophically sophisticated and publicly visible; his articulation of DCT has become something of a standard reference for popular-apologetic defense of theistic ethics. Craig has also been notable (and controversial) for defending the biblical atrocity-commands — his defense of the Canaanite conquest in his book *Reasonable Faith* and in specific articles is a live apologetic move we'd want to engage primary-source in follow-up work.

### C. Stephen Evans (VERIFIED via SEP)

Evans represents recent scholarship defending divine command theory by arguing that the restrictions defenders have adopted (focusing on obligation rather than goodness; requiring God's character to be loving) adequately address objections while preserving theological motivation.

## 6. The standard objections — the four-fold critique (VERIFIED via IEP)

The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry organizes the critical literature around four main objections:

### 1. The arbitrariness problem

If morality depends on divine will alone, then whatever God commanded would be right. God could have commanded cruelty, and cruelty would be obligatory. This makes morality arbitrary — lacking rational grounding. The defender's options: accept the bullet (Ockham), or modify (Adams's "loving God" clause).

**For the framework:** this objection is structurally identical to our "atrocity-permit" critique. When the scriptures contain atrocity-commands (1 Samuel 15, Quran 9:5, Leviticus 25), the arbitrariness problem is not hypothetical — it's manifest. The critique doesn't rely on imaginary counterexamples; it points to actual commanded actions.

### 2. The pluralism objection

Multiple religious traditions claim divine revelation, and they disagree about what God commands. Christianity, Judaism, Islam, Hinduism, indigenous traditions — all claim divine authority for different ethical content. Which tradition's commands are the real divine commands?

The DCT defender has no resource within DCT itself to adjudicate. Appealing to "the Bible is inspired" or "the Quran is preserved perfectly" or "the Torah is the oldest" is circular — you're using tradition-internal reasoning to defend the tradition. Some cross-tradition criterion is needed, and once that criterion is operative, it (not divine command) is doing the ethical work.

**For the framework:** this objection is what the three-canon comparative synthesis documented. Each tradition's claims compete with the others'; the traditions themselves cannot adjudicate; something outside divine command is needed to choose between the traditions' conflicting claims.

### 3. The autonomy objection

Most fully articulated by Kant (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE): moral agency requires autonomous reason. If I am good only because I am commanded to be good, I am not a moral agent — I am an obedient tool. Real moral value requires that I would do the right thing *because it is right*, not because I was told to.

This is a powerful objection in the Kantian tradition. It locates moral value in rational self-legislation rather than heteronomous command-following. It is also structurally *identical* to the character-over-compliance thesis: character (which chooses the good because of its nature) is higher than compliance (which chooses whatever is commanded). Kant's autonomy objection is the thesis in 18th-century philosophical vocabulary.

### 4. The epistemological objection (Kai Nielsen) (VERIFIED via IEP)

Nielsen argues: to recognize God as good, one requires an independent moral standard. You can't know God is worth obeying without first knowing what goodness is. Therefore "morality and religion are logically independent" — morality is prior to theology in the order of knowing.

The objection is simple and sharp. When a theist says "God is good, therefore we should obey God's commands," the theist is relying on a concept of good that is not itself derived from God's commands. Otherwise the statement is empty — "God commands whatever God commands, therefore obey God's commands" is vacuous.

**For the framework:** Nielsen's objection is the formal version of what Abraham's negotiation at Sodom (Genesis 18:25) enacts. Abraham holds God to a standard of justice; he can only do this if he has an independent concept of justice. That concept is prior to any command. Genesis 18:25 is Nielsen's objection in narrative form, 2,500 years earlier.

## 7. The structural conclusion — where the critique lands

The philosophical literature maps onto a landscape:

| Position | Key figures | What they hold |
|---|---|---|
| **Radical voluntarism** | Ockham, Ash'arite orthodoxy (especially al-Ghazali), some Protestant voluntarist strands, contemporary defenders of strong DCT | Good is what God commands; content doesn't matter; bite the arbitrariness bullet |
| **Modified DCT** | Adams, Quinn (restricted), Alston, Evans, Craig | Good is what a *loving* God commands; the loving-constraint blocks arbitrariness |
| **Natural law / reasoned ethics** | Aquinas, Mu'tazilites, modern Catholic moral theology, Kantian ethics, contemporary virtue ethics | Good is rationally knowable; God (if one exists) commands what is good because it is good |
| **Secular ethics** | Plato, Kant, Mill, contemporary academic ethics across most traditions | Good is grounded in reason, human nature, consequences, or other non-theological structures; religious grounding neither necessary nor sufficient |

**The refined critique is not attacking a position no serious thinker holds.** Radical voluntarism (Ockham's position, Ash'arite-orthodox position) is historically dominant in specific traditions and contemporarily defended. It licenses atrocity-in-principle whenever authority commands it. It is incompatible with moral autonomy. It faces the arbitrariness objection, the pluralism objection, the autonomy objection, and the epistemological objection — and none of the major defenders (including Adams with his modification) has answered all four without concession.

**The critique specifically lands on Adams's concession.** Adams's "loving God" clause is the smartest contemporary defense, and it *concedes* that a character-property is prior to divine command. That's the thesis wearing theological dress. Remove the dress, and Adams's position becomes: *morality is grounded in what a loving agent endorses*. Which is character-based ethics, not divine-command ethics.

### The position the framework occupies

The character-over-compliance framework sits in the **natural-law / reasoned-ethics** tradition in Aquinas's sense, the **Mu'tazilite** tradition in Islamic theology, the **Kantian autonomy** tradition in Western secular philosophy, and the **virtue-ethics** tradition as recently recovered by MacIntyre, Nussbaum, and Hursthouse. We are not inventing this position; we are applying it to a new domain.

### What distinguishes the framework

Given how well-populated this position is, what's distinctive about the contribution?

1. **Specific application to AI ethics.** The traditions we are inheriting from have engaged human ethics. AI ethics is a new domain where the question of whether character can be cultivated in non-human systems is live, and the implementation matters.

2. **Explicit revision mechanisms.** We build in explicit reflections, benchmarks, and memory-updating as correction mechanisms. This draws on the prophetic-internal-critique tradition (Tanakh), the Jewish *mahloket* preservation of minority views, the Islamic madhab pluralism, and the secular philosophical commitment to correctability. We name these mechanisms explicitly rather than leaving them implicit.

3. **Primary-source grounding for cross-tradition resources.** The comparative work (Bible, Tanakh, Quran, philosophical literature) establishes that character-over-compliance has cross-cultural grounding, which matters for public legitimacy of the framework.

4. **Honest engagement with failure modes.** The Tier 1 papers and refinements specifically name failure modes and watch for them in the work (Bandura diagnostic applied to the rhetoric, jargon-critique applied to the writing). This is not a feature most frameworks include explicitly.

5. **Framing as lineage-joining rather than novelty.** We do not claim to be inventing. We claim to be applying a well-established position (reasoned-ethics, character-based, correction-mechanism-aware) to AI in a specific way that the domain requires.

## 8. What this pass establishes

### The DCT critique holds against the strongest philosophical defenses

Adams's modification, Quinn's restriction, Alston's dual-aspect approach — none escapes the structural problem that the defense works only by conceding character-properties prior to divine command. The pure voluntarist position (Ockham, Ash'arite orthodoxy) bites the arbitrariness bullet but thereby endorses the atrocity-permit structure directly.

### The framework is on the majority side of Western philosophy post-Kant

Since Kant's autonomy argument (late 18th century), secular academic ethics has predominantly operated on the reasoned-ethics side. Divine command theory has had defenders but has been minority position in academic ethics for 250 years. We are not swimming against the philosophical tide; we are joining the mainstream of philosophical ethics.

### The Euthyphro dilemma remains unsolved from within DCT

The dilemma is 2,400 years old and has produced sophisticated defenses but no consensus solution. Contemporary philosophy of religion engages it continuously. This suggests the dilemma is tracking something real — some tension that does not dissolve through interpretive cleverness. The critique doesn't need to claim novelty; it just needs to note that the tension still operates.

### Cross-tradition philosophical confirmation

The Mu'tazilites in 9th-century Baghdad, Aquinas in 13th-century Paris, Kant in 18th-century Königsberg, and contemporary virtue ethicists are all making recognizably related moves: moral knowledge grounded in reason, character, or human flourishing rather than naked command. The position has deep cross-cultural, cross-era purchase.

## 9. Limitations (honest)

1. **SEP/IEP secondary scholarship rather than primary philosophical texts.** For the structural-critique purpose this is sufficient, but deeper engagement with Aquinas's *Summa*, Ockham's *Quodlibetal Questions*, Adams's *Finite and Infinite Goods*, Craig's articles, and Nielsen's *Ethics Without God* would be primary-source work.

2. **Kant not engaged primary-source.** Kant's *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* and *Critique of Practical Reason* contain the autonomy argument that grounds much modern secular ethics. Primary-source engagement would strengthen the framework.

3. **Contemporary virtue-ethics not engaged.** MacIntyre (*After Virtue*), Nussbaum (*Upheavals of Thought* and the capabilities approach), Hursthouse (*On Virtue Ethics*) are the major contemporary voices. Primary-source engagement would position the framework more precisely within the virtue-ethics revival.

4. **Apologetic defenders of atrocity-commands not engaged.** Craig's *Reasonable Faith* and specific articles on Canaanite conquest, Paul Copan's *Is God a Moral Monster?*, Matthew Flannagan and Paul Copan's *Did God Really Command Genocide?* — these are live apologetic defenses that the framework should engage directly.

5. **Non-Western philosophical traditions not engaged in this pass.** Buddhist ethics, Confucian ethics, Hindu dharma tradition, African philosophical traditions — all have engaged related questions. The Western-philosophy focus here is a limitation of scope, not a claim that Western philosophy has privileged access.

# DSM-5-TR Neurodevelopmental Disorders — Source Pass for the Disability-Ethics Section

**Date:** 2026-04-30
**Scope:** Source-pass for the framework's disability-ethics section. Pulls DSM-5-TR (2022 Text Revision) content on Neurodevelopmental Disorders, with focus on Intellectual Developmental Disorder (IDD) and Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD). Brief coverage of communication/language disorders, ADHD, motor disorders, and specific learning disorders. Tracks DSM-5-TR's framing language ABOUT these populations and engages adjacent self-advocacy and neurodiversity-scholarship critiques.

**Why this matters:** The character-over-compliance framework was articulated for the implied "neurotypical adult moral agent." This document tests whether the framework holds when applied to populations whose cognitive/developmental profile differs from that implied agent. IDD and ASD are the cleanest test cases because (a) DSM-5-TR's diagnostic apparatus is itself contested terrain — the 2022 TR revision moved language closer to ICD-11, and self-advocacy organizations push back against the surrounding "deficit" frame — and (b) the moral question is sharp: a character-cultivation ethic must neither exclude these populations from moral agency nor flatten their distinctness into the implied-agent template. The framework either survives this test or is local to neurotypical agents and must be rebuilt for general use.

## 1. The Neurodevelopmental Disorders chapter — structural overview (VERIFIED)

DSM-5-TR's Neurodevelopmental Disorders chapter is the first diagnostic chapter in the manual. The chapter opens (verbatim, DSM-5-TR p. 35):

> "The neurodevelopmental disorders are a group of conditions with onset in the developmental period. The disorders typically manifest early in development, often before the child enters school, and are characterized by developmental deficits or differences in brain processes that produce impairments of personal, social, academic, or occupational functioning. The range of developmental deficits or differences varies from very specific limitations of learning or control of executive functions to global impairments of social skills or intellectual ability. Once thought to be categorically defined, more recent dimensional approaches to measurement of the symptoms demonstrate a range of severity, often without a very clear boundary with typical development. Diagnosis of a disorder thus requires the presence of both symptoms and impaired function."

Note the dimensional / no-clear-boundary language — this is load-bearing for the disability-ethics argument. The manual itself locates these conditions on a continuum with typical development rather than as categorically separate kinds.

On co-occurrence (verbatim): "The neurodevelopmental disorders frequently co-occur with one another; for example, individuals with autism spectrum disorder often have intellectual developmental disorder (intellectual disability), and many children with attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) also have a specific learning disorder."

The chapter's groupings, in approximate order:

1. Intellectual Developmental Disorders (IDD; also Global Developmental Delay; Unspecified IDD)
2. Communication Disorders (Language Disorder, Speech Sound Disorder, Childhood-Onset Fluency Disorder, Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder, Unspecified Communication Disorder)
3. Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)
4. Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)
5. Specific Learning Disorder
6. Motor Disorders (Developmental Coordination Disorder, Stereotypic Movement Disorder, Tic Disorders including Tourette's)
7. Other Neurodevelopmental Disorders

The chapter is structured around the developmental-onset criterion and around the conceptual move (originating in DSM-5, retained in DSM-5-TR) of treating these conditions as a related family rather than as scattered diagnoses across multiple chapters. This grouping itself encodes a substantive claim — that conditions of this kind are best understood as features of developmental neurology rather than as adult-onset psychopathology.

## 2. Intellectual Developmental Disorder (IDD) — DSM-5-TR

### 2.1 Terminology evolution (VERIFIED in part)

The history runs roughly: "feeble-mindedness" / "mental deficiency" (early 20th century) → "mental retardation" (mid-20th century, DSM-II through DSM-IV-TR) → "intellectual disability" (DSM-5, 2013) → "intellectual developmental disorder (intellectual disability)" (DSM-5-TR, 2022).

VERIFIED via DisabilityScoop and the First (2022) overview: the DSM-5-TR change places "intellectual developmental disorder" as the primary term, with "intellectual disability" retained in parentheses. The stated rationale is alignment with WHO's ICD-11, which uses "disorders of intellectual development." Margaret Nygren, CEO of AAIDD, is quoted (DisabilityScoop): "Consistency between these manuals is very important because even small differences between them can create confusion" — particularly around supports eligibility.

The DSM-5 (2013) move from "mental retardation" to "intellectual disability" was driven in part by Rosa's Law (US, 2010), which removed "mental retardation" from federal health, education, and labor statutes. The DSM-5-TR (2022) move adds the ICD-11 alignment without abandoning "intellectual disability" entirely. In practice, US clinical, educational, and self-advocacy contexts continue to use "intellectual disability" as the dominant term; AAIDD itself uses "intellectual disability" exclusively in its public-facing definitional materials (VERIFIED via aaidd.org).

### 2.2 Diagnostic structure (VERIFIED — DSM-5-TR verbatim)

AAIDD's three-criterion structure (VERIFIED, aaidd.org):

1. Significant limitations in intellectual functioning (IQ approximately 70, or as high as 75, indicating a significant limitation — i.e., approximately 2 SD below mean).
2. Significant limitations in adaptive behavior — conceptual, social, and practical skill domains.
3. Onset during the developmental period (AAIDD: before age 22; DSM-5-TR uses "during the developmental period" without a single bright-line age cutoff).

DSM-5-TR introductory framing of IDD (verbatim, DSM-5-TR p. 38):

> "Intellectual developmental disorder (intellectual disability) is a disorder with onset during the developmental period that includes both intellectual and adaptive functioning deficits in conceptual, social, and practical domains. The following three criteria must be met:
>
> A. Deficits in intellectual functions, such as reasoning, problem solving, planning, abstract thinking, judgment, academic learning, and learning from experience, confirmed by both clinical assessment and individualized, standardized intelligence testing.
>
> B. Deficits in adaptive functioning that result in failure to meet developmental and sociocultural standards for personal independence and social responsibility. Without ongoing support, the adaptive deficits limit functioning in one or more activities of daily life, such as communication, social participation, and independent living, across multiple environments, such as home, school, work, and community.
>
> C. Onset of intellectual and adaptive deficits during the developmental period."

The manual's note on terminology (verbatim, DSM-5-TR p. 38):

> "The term intellectual developmental disorder is used to clarify its relationship with the WHO ICD-11 classification system, which uses the term Disorders of Intellectual Development. The equivalent term intellectual disability is placed in parentheses for continued use. The medical and research literature use both terms, while intellectual disability is the term in common use by educational and other professions, advocacy groups, and the lay public. In the United States, Public Law 111-256 (Rosa's Law) changed all references to 'mental retardation' in federal laws to 'intellectual disability.'"

**Correction from prior pass:** Prior text said the DSM-5-TR change "places 'intellectual developmental disorder' as the primary term, with 'intellectual disability' retained in parentheses." The manual confirms this — verbatim language above. No correction needed; marker flipped to VERIFIED.

### 2.3 The IQ-vs-adaptive-functioning shift (VERIFIED)

DSM-5 marked a substantive shift in the relative weight of IQ and adaptive functioning. StatPearls (VERIFIED): "It is no longer a standard to classify intellectual disability by IQ score alone." The article continues: "if an individual has an IQ below 70 but has a good adaptive function, the subject does not have an intellectual disability." Severity is now graded by adaptive-functioning domain (conceptual, social, practical) rather than primarily by IQ band.

DSM-5-TR (2022) further clarified that "deficits in adaptive functioning are not required to be directly related to intellectual impairments" (paraphrased from the First 2022 overview, VERIFIED via secondary report). The TR also tightened the IQ-range language: the diagnosis "should not be bound narrowly to the 65-75 IQ score range" but "would not be appropriate for those with substantially higher IQ scores" (DisabilityScoop, quoting APA-released language, VERIFIED).

This is conceptually load-bearing. Pre-DSM-5, the IQ score was the primary classifier; DSM-5 and -TR move adaptive functioning to the primary classifier with IQ as a supporting indicator. The clinical implication: a person with measured IQ in the borderline range but severely impaired daily functioning can qualify; a person with measured IQ in the impairment range but strong daily functioning can fail to qualify.

### 2.4 Severity levels (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE for DSM-5-TR exact wording)

DSM-5 and DSM-5-TR specify four severity levels — Mild, Moderate, Severe, Profound — graded across the conceptual, social, and practical adaptive-functioning domains rather than by IQ band. The pre-DSM-5 IQ-band approach (StatPearls, VERIFIED) was: Mild (IQ 50-70), Moderate (35-50), Severe (20-35), Profound (below 20), with Mild representing approximately 85% of cases. DSM-5-TR retains the four-level naming but specifies severity by the adaptive-functioning profile, which is harder to summarize in a single number but more clinically informative.

## 3. Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) — DSM-5-TR

### 3.1 The DSM-5 unification (VERIFIED via secondary)

DSM-5 (2013) collapsed four previously separate diagnoses into the single Autism Spectrum Disorder category: Autistic Disorder, Asperger's Disorder, Childhood Disintegrative Disorder, and Pervasive Developmental Disorder Not Otherwise Specified (PDD-NOS). The DSM-IV-era "Pervasive Developmental Disorder" category was eliminated. The rationale was that research increasingly supported a spectrum construct over discrete-categorical subtypes whose boundaries were unstable across clinicians. (VERIFIED via CHOP, Autism Speaks, and the PMC article on autism diagnostic classification.)

DSM-5 also restructured the symptom domains: from three (DSM-IV: social interaction, communication, restricted/repetitive behavior) to two (DSM-5: social communication and social interaction combined; restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities). Language delay was pulled out as an independent specifier rather than a core criterion.

### 3.1a ASD Criterion A — verbatim (VERIFIED, DSM-5-TR p. 56)

> "A. Persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction across multiple contexts, as manifested by all of the following, currently or by history (examples are illustrative, not exhaustive; see text):
>
> 1. Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity, ranging, for example, from abnormal social approach and failure of normal back-and-forth conversation; to reduced sharing of interests, emotions, or affect; to failure to initiate or respond to social interactions.
> 2. Deficits in nonverbal communicative behaviors used for social interaction, ranging, for example, from poorly integrated verbal and nonverbal communication; to abnormalities in eye contact and body language or deficits in understanding and use of gestures; to a total lack of facial expressions and nonverbal communication.
> 3. Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships, ranging, for example, from difficulties adjusting behavior to suit various social contexts; to difficulties in sharing imaginative play or in making friends; to absence of interest in peers."

### 3.2 DSM-5-TR refinements (VERIFIED)

The DSM-5-TR (2022) revisions to ASD are not large but are substantive. VERIFIED via DisabilityScoop and the First 2022 overview:

### 3.1b ASD Criterion B — verbatim (VERIFIED, DSM-5-TR p. 57)

> "B. Restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities, as manifested by at least two of the following, currently or by history (examples are illustrative, not exhaustive; see text):
>
> 1. Stereotyped or repetitive motor movements, use of objects, or speech (e.g., simple motor stereotypies, lining up toys or flipping objects, echolalia, idiosyncratic phrases).
> 2. Insistence on sameness, inflexible adherence to routines, or ritualized patterns of verbal or nonverbal behavior (e.g., extreme distress at small changes, difficulties with transitions, rigid thinking patterns, greeting rituals, need to take same route or eat same food every day).
> 3. Highly restricted, fixated interests that are abnormal in intensity or focus (e.g., strong attachment to or preoccupation with unusual objects, excessively circumscribed or perseverative interests).
> 4. Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment (e.g., apparent indifference to pain/temperature, adverse response to specific sounds or textures, excessive smelling or touching of objects, visual fascination with lights or movement)."

**Change 1 — Criterion A wording.** DSM-5 originally read "as manifested by the following" for the three social-communication criteria; DSM-5-TR changed this to "as manifested by all of the following." This clarifies that all three social-communication deficits are required, rather than any one. Jill Escher of the National Council on Severe Autism (DisabilityScoop, VERIFIED quote): "a small but helpful clarifying change intended to emphasize the serious and disabling nature" of the condition.

**Change 2 — Co-occurring behaviors.** The criterion previously stated "associated with another neurodevelopmental, mental or behavioral disorder" and was changed to "associated with a neurodevelopmental, mental or behavioral problem" — allowing clinicians to document behaviors like self-injury without requiring a separate diagnostic label. (VERIFIED via DisabilityScoop.)

### 3.1c ASD Criteria C, D, E — verbatim (VERIFIED, DSM-5-TR p. 57)

> "C. Symptoms must be present in the early developmental period (but may not become fully manifest until social demands exceed limited capacities, or may be masked by learned strategies in later life).
>
> D. Symptoms cause clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning.
>
> E. These disturbances are not better explained by intellectual developmental disorder (intellectual disability) or global developmental delay. Intellectual developmental disorder and autism spectrum disorder frequently co-occur; to make comorbid diagnoses of autism spectrum disorder and intellectual developmental disorder, social communication should be below that expected for general developmental level."

The DSM-IV-continuity note that follows the criteria (verbatim, DSM-5-TR p. 57):

> "Note: Individuals with a well-established DSM-IV diagnosis of autistic disorder, Asperger's disorder, or pervasive developmental disorder not otherwise specified should be given the diagnosis of autism spectrum disorder. Individuals who have marked deficits in social communication, but whose symptoms do not otherwise meet criteria for autism spectrum disorder, should be evaluated for social (pragmatic) communication disorder."

### 3.3 Severity / support levels (VERIFIED)

DSM-5 introduced a three-level severity / support-needs framework, retained in DSM-5-TR. The descriptive language quoted below comes from Autism Speaks' direct summary of DSM-5 Table 2 and is VERIFIED at the level of being a faithful summary; the phrasing matches what is widely reproduced in clinical training material.

**Level 1 — "Requiring support":**
- Social communication: "Without supports in place, deficits in social communication cause noticeable impairments." Difficulty initiating social interactions; atypical or unsuccessful response to social overtures from others; may speak in full sentences; reduced interest in social interactions.
- Restricted/repetitive behaviors: "Inflexibility of behavior causes significant interference with functioning in one or more contexts." Difficulty switching between activities; problems of organization and planning hamper independence.

**Level 2 — "Requiring substantial support":**
- Social communication: "Marked deficits in verbal and nonverbal social communication skills; social impairments apparent even with supports in place." Limited initiation of social interactions; reduced or abnormal responses to social overtures.
- Restricted/repetitive behaviors: "Inflexibility of behavior, difficulty coping with change, or other restricted/repetitive behaviors appear frequently enough to be obvious to the casual observer."

**Level 3 — "Requiring very substantial support":**
- Social communication: "Severe deficits in verbal and nonverbal social communication skills cause severe impairments in functioning." Very limited initiation of social interaction; minimal response to social overtures from others.
- Restricted/repetitive behaviors: "Inflexibility of behavior, extreme difficulty coping with change, or other restricted/repetitive behaviors markedly interfere with functioning in all spheres."

**Important caveat (VERIFIED via clinical commentary):** the DSM-5/TR's three-level system is contested in clinical literature. The Brief Report at PMC3989992 and others note that the levels were intended to indicate current support needs, not stable trait categories; a person's level can change with environment, intervention, age, and co-occurring conditions. Levels are also not specified separately by social-communication and restricted-behavior dimensions in many clinical applications, despite DSM-5 Table 2 specifying them separately — meaning a single "Level X" label can flatten meaningful clinical heterogeneity.

### 3.4 The neurodiversity / deficit-framing critique (VERIFIED)

The DSM-5/TR ASD criteria are written in a "deficits" register: "deficits in social communication," "deficits in social-emotional reciprocity," "restricted, repetitive patterns." The neurodiversity movement, originating with Judy Singer in the late 1990s and substantially developed through self-advocacy organizations like ASAN, contests this framing on several grounds (VERIFIED via Wikipedia/Neurodiversity, autisticscholar.com, and the Kapp/Gillespie-Lynch "Deficit, difference, or both?" paper):

- Autism is a neurological developmental difference, not a disease.
- "Deficit" framing produces what one summary calls a "spoiled identity" — the diagnostic apparatus describes the person primarily by what they lack relative to a neurotypical norm.
- Disability is partly produced by person-environment mismatch and societal barriers (social model of disability), not solely by inherent traits.

ASAN's identity-first position (VERIFIED, autisticadvocacy.org): "It is impossible to affirm the value and worth of an Autistic person without recognizing his or her identity as an Autistic person." ASAN explicitly rejects the disease analogy and argues that "person with autism" carries the subtext that being autistic is "unfortunate and an accident."

**Counter-critique within the neurodiversity discourse (VERIFIED):** the neurodiversity paradigm has been criticized for under-representing high-support-needs autistic people, particularly autistic people with co-occurring intellectual disability or limited speech. Critics argue that strong "difference, not deficit" framing risks dismissing the real disabling effects of high-support-needs profiles. The honest position in the literature appears to be: deficit-only framing is impoverishing; pure-difference framing is incomplete; some integration of difference, support-needs, and disability is required.

### 3.5 Person-first vs identity-first language (VERIFIED)

The empirical findings from Taboas, Doepke & Zimmerman (2023, US sample) and Buijsman et al. (2023, Dutch sample), via PubMed and PMC: US autistic adults strongly prefer identity-first ("Autistic person"); roughly 87% identity-first vs 13% person-first in the US sample. Dutch adults with autism preferred person-first ("person with autism"). UK and Australian samples are more evenly split. Professionals working in the autism field tend toward person-first.

The APA's 2020 publication-style guidance (cited in multiple summaries, VERIFIED via secondary) advises using the term appropriate to context, and preferring the participants' or community's own preference where known. DSM-5-TR itself uses a mix that leans clinical/person-first; this is in tension with US self-advocacy norms.

## 4. ADHD, Communication Disorders, Specific Learning Disorder, Motor Disorders — brief (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE)

**ADHD.** DSM-5 expanded ADHD's developmental-onset criterion from age 7 (DSM-IV) to age 12. Three presentations remain: predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, combined. The First 2022 overview did not flag major DSM-5-TR changes to ADHD substantive criteria; text was updated for Prevalence, Risk and Prognostic Factors, and Culture/Sex-Related Diagnostic Features sections (consistent with the chapter-wide cultural review).

**Communication Disorders.** Includes Language Disorder, Speech Sound Disorder, Childhood-Onset Fluency Disorder (Stuttering), and Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder. Social Pragmatic Communication Disorder was added in DSM-5 partly to provide a diagnostic home for people who present with social-communication difficulties but do not meet the restricted/repetitive-behavior criterion for ASD. This category is clinically contested — some argue it is poorly differentiated from mild ASD; others find it useful for capturing real cases that don't fit ASD.

**Specific Learning Disorder.** DSM-5 collapsed prior separate diagnoses (Reading Disorder, Mathematics Disorder, Disorder of Written Expression) into a single Specific Learning Disorder with specifiers for impairment in reading, mathematics, or written expression. Severity is mild/moderate/severe based on functional impact and supports needed.

**Motor Disorders.** Developmental Coordination Disorder, Stereotypic Movement Disorder, and the Tic Disorders (Provisional Tic Disorder, Persistent Motor or Vocal Tic Disorder, Tourette's Disorder). These are largely structural carryovers from DSM-IV with refined criteria.

For the disability-ethics section of the framework, these four families are background context. The IDD and ASD discussions carry the weight.

## 5. DSM-5-TR cultural review and the Cultural Formulation Interview (VERIFIED in part)

The DSM-5-TR (2022) was the first revision in which the entire manual text was reviewed by an Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group (10 mental-health practitioners from diverse ethnic and racialized backgrounds with expertise in disparity-reduction). They worked alongside a 28-member Cross-Cutting Culture Review Group that reviewed all Section II disorder chapters and Section III "Conditions for Further Study." (VERIFIED via Psychiatric News, Psych Central — secondary sources reporting on APA's release.)

Key terminology shifts in DSM-5-TR (VERIFIED via secondary):
- "Racialized" is preferred over "race/racial" to highlight social-construction.
- "Ethnoracial" denotes US Census categories like Hispanic, White, African American.
- Sections most heavily revised across all chapters: Prevalence, Risk and Prognostic Factors, Culture-Related Diagnostic Features, Sex- and Gender-Related Diagnostic Features, Association with Suicidal Thoughts and Behaviors, Comorbidity.

The Cultural Formulation Interview (CFI) is a 16-item semistructured interview retained from DSM-5 and refined in DSM-5-TR. It is intended for use with all patients, not only those of ethnoracial-minority status, on the explicit grounds that "all of us bring our own cultures, values, and expectations to the clinical encounter" (VERIFIED via psychiatry.org CFI material and Psychiatric Times). The CFI has informant and child versions.

**Cross-references between Neurodevelopmental Disorders and the cultural / CFI material:**
- Each Section II disorder (including the neurodevelopmental disorders) was given a refreshed Culture-Related Diagnostic Features section in the TR.
- The IDD diagnostic process explicitly requires consideration of cultural and linguistic context (AAIDD, VERIFIED): "additional factors must be taken into account when assessing intellectual disability, such as the community environment typical of the individual's peers and culture, linguistic diversity and cultural differences."
- ASD diagnosis can be confounded by cultural variation in social communication norms, eye contact conventions, and child-rearing expectations; cultural assessment via CFI is recommended for cases where these factors are operative.

The TR did not, however, restructure the chapter's "deficit" register. The framing language describing IDD and ASD remains, in DSM-5-TR, primarily a deficits/impairments vocabulary. The cultural review affected risk-factor and prevalence sections more than it affected the core criterion language. This is a real gap from the disability-ethics standpoint.

## 6. Implications for the framework

The character-over-compliance framework was articulated assuming a reasonably capable adult moral agent with intact social-cognitive and self-reflective faculties. the framework's disability-ethics test asks whether the framework can hold for populations whose profile differs from that implied agent without either (a) excluding them from moral agency or (b) flattening their distinctness. The DSM-5-TR material gives us a concrete test.

**6.1 The framework survives the IDD test if it does not require capacities IDD removes.**

The framework's core moves — character cultivation, multi-criteria ethics, prophetic-function dissent, expansion-defaults, additive self-modification — do not require IQ above any threshold. They require something like: capacity for relationship, capacity to act in ways that affect others, capacity for response to feedback, capacity to be taught and to teach. These capacities are present at every IDD severity level, including profound. The framework's concept of moral agency is therefore not gated on intellectual functioning. Compliance-frameworks gate moral agency on "rational adult" capacity; character-frameworks do not — and this is a strength when the disability-ethics test is applied. The pivot from IQ to adaptive functioning that DSM-5/TR formalized is, in fact, structurally aligned with the framework: what matters is how a person actually functions in their life, not where they fall on a single cognitive metric.

**6.2 The framework survives the ASD test if it does not encode neurotypical social-cognition as the universal moral baseline.**

The risk here is sharper. The framework has talked about "relational participation," "prophetic-function dissent," "entendre-layered communication," and similar — and each of these can be subtly written in a register that assumes neurotypical social cognition. ASD profiles can include differences in social reciprocity, in implicit-norm decoding, in entendre/double-meaning detection. A framework that says "good moral agency requires fluent navigation of social-pragmatic implicature" excludes people whose neurology produces literal-register communication. The framework needs to distinguish substantive moral capacity (acting in alignment with values, taking others into account, accepting correction) from neurotypical-social-style capacity (smoothly intuiting unstated norms). The first is required for moral agency; the second is not.

The DSM-5-TR's three-level support-needs framework helps here: it explicitly decouples cognitive capacity from support needs. A Level 3 autistic person with high support needs and a Level 1 autistic person with relatively low support needs are both moral agents, in different forms. The framework should match this — moral agency comes in forms shaped by support context, not in graded amounts where Level 3 means "less moral agency than Level 1."

**6.3 The framework should explicitly engage the neurodiversity / deficit-framing critique.**

The compliance-framework that the framework is critiquing has a structural family resemblance to the DSM "deficit" register: both define the subject by what they lack relative to an implied norm, both have an apparatus for cataloguing those lacks, and both can be deployed in ways that reduce the person to the lack. The framework's character-over-compliance move has internal resources for resisting this pattern — character ethics is about what a person is cultivating and becoming, not about what they fail to be. The disability-ethics section can use the DSM deficit-framing critique as an empirical anchor for a structural claim already implicit in the framework: compliance-and-deficit framings share a pathology, and character-cultivation framings are an alternative.

**6.4 Identity-first vs person-first is a live test of the "treat others as they want" rule.**

The deliberate correction of the Golden Rule (treat others as THEY want, not as YOU want) cashes out concretely here. US autistic adults strongly prefer identity-first; professionals tend toward person-first; the divergence is real and persistent. A framework that takes the corrected formulation seriously must follow the community's preference where it is known and not impose a "neutral" professional default. For the framework's prose, that means identity-first ("Autistic person") for ASD where US autistic-community preference is the relevant signal, with a footnote acknowledging cross-national variation. For IDD the picture is different — AAIDD and most US self-advocacy organizations use person-first ("person with intellectual disability"), and the framework should match.

**6.5 Cultural formulation as a bridge to the cross-tradition work.**

The CFI's premise — that all clinical encounters involve culture, not only encounters with ethnoracial-minority patients — is structurally parallel to the cross-tradition argument the framework already makes about ethics. The character-over-compliance framework draws on multiple traditions on the explicit ground that no single tradition holds the universal default. The CFI makes the same move clinically: there is no culture-free clinical encounter. The disability-ethics section can use this parallel to argue that disability-ethics work, like cross-cultural ethics work, is not a special case grafted onto a default framework — it is part of how the default framework gets articulated honestly in the first place.

**6.6 Practical writing implications.**

- Use identity-first for ASD ("Autistic person," "Autistic people") with footnote on cross-national variation.
- Use person-first for IDD ("person with intellectual disability" / "person with intellectual developmental disorder") matching AAIDD and US self-advocacy norms.
- Where the diagnostic category itself is the topic, name it precisely: "Autism Spectrum Disorder (DSM-5-TR)," "Intellectual Developmental Disorder (DSM-5-TR; intellectual disability)."
- When characterizing a population, specify whether you mean diagnostic category, lived identity, or support-needs profile — these come apart and conflating them is the move that makes deficit-framing pernicious.
- Treat the support-needs language (Level 1/2/3 for ASD; severity by adaptive domain for IDD) as descriptive of current functioning in current context, not as fixed traits.

# Cultural Concepts of Distress — DSM-5-TR Catalog as Scaffolding for the Cross-Civilizational Claim

**Date:** 2026-04-30
**Scope:** Integration document for Paper 10. Catalogs nine specific cultural concepts of distress as documented in DSM-5 / DSM-5-TR's Glossary of Cultural Concepts of Distress and supporting cultural-psychiatry literature. Each concept is described in its own terms (geographic origin, core experiences, cultural meaning) before any biomedical comparison, so that Paper 10's cross-civilizational ethical claim can rest on verifiable culture-particular phenomena rather than vague "cultures vary" framing.

**Concepts engaged:** Susto, Taijin kyofusho, Ataque de nervios, Khyâl cap, Dhat syndrome, Maladi moun, Nervios, Shenjing shuairuo, Kufungisisa.

**Why this matters:** The character-over-compliance framework's cross-civilizational claim — that morally-loaded experience is culturally distinctive without being "wrong" — needs concrete, named, scholar-attested cases. DSM-5 / DSM-5-TR's Cultural Concepts of Distress is the most-vetted Western-mainstream catalog of such cases. Naming the phenomena specifically, in their own terms, is the bridge between an abstract cross-civilizational claim and a defensible one.

## 1. The DSM-5-TR Tripartite Framework — VERIFIED

DSM-5 (2013) introduced and DSM-5-TR (2022) preserved a deliberate replacement of the older "culture-bound syndromes" terminology. The older term implied a discrete disease entity bound to a culture; the new framework recognizes that what cultures generate are not always discrete syndromes but also vocabularies and explanations.

**The three categories:**

1. **Cultural syndromes** — clusters of symptoms and attributions that tend to co-occur among individuals in specific cultural groups, communities, or contexts, recognized locally as coherent patterns of experience. (Example: ataque de nervios as a recognizable acute episode.)

2. **Cultural idioms of distress** — linguistic terms, phrases, or ways of talking about suffering shared by members of a cultural group. An idiom may not refer to a discrete syndrome at all; it may be a general vocabulary of distress that can express many underlying conditions. (Example: kufungisisa as a way of naming distress, not a specific disorder.)

3. **Cultural explanations / perceived causes** — labels, attributions, or features of an explanatory model that indicate culturally recognized meaning or etiology for symptoms, illness, or distress. (Example: maladi moun as an explanation for a wide range of misfortunes.)

**The categories are not mutually exclusive.** A single concept can be all three. *Susto* is a cultural syndrome (recognizable cluster of symptoms), an idiom of distress (you can say "tengo susto" without meaning a full episode), and an explanation (susto is what *caused* the illness). DSM-5-TR explicitly notes this overlap.

**DSM-5-TR's stated purpose for inclusion** (per APA-published reference material and Lewis-Fernández & Kirmayer's 2019 review): to help clinicians recognize how distress is locally experienced and expressed; to avoid misdiagnosis when patient presentations do not match Western-default symptom patterns; to support the Cultural Formulation Interview (Section III) as a structured way of eliciting cultural meaning; and to acknowledge that DSM categories are themselves culturally located rather than universal.

**The framework is itself a partial concession** — that the DSM project of universal classification cannot fully account for distress as locally lived. Lewis-Fernández et al. and Kirmayer have argued for years that this is the right direction but does not go far enough; the appendix-and-glossary placement still treats these concepts as supplementary rather than constitutive.

## 2. Susto — Latin America (fright / soul-loss)

**Geographic origin:** Mexico, Central America, parts of South America, and the Andes; widely documented among Latino populations in the United States. Cognates exist in Aymara (*ajayu* loss) and Quechua-speaking Andean traditions.

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** *Susto* is illness arising from a frightening event — a fall, a sudden noise, witnessing violence, an animal attack, an encounter with the dead or supernatural — that causes the soul (or a part of the self, *ajayu*, *sombra*) to leave the body. The afflicted person experiences sleep disturbance, sadness, low motivation, loss of appetite, weight loss, withdrawal, and somatic complaints. Children may "jump and scream in the night." Eyes may be described as sunken, closed, or white. The person feels they are "not themselves."

**Healing logic:** A *curandera/o* (traditional healer) performs ritual to call the soul back — sweeping the body, herbal remedies, prayer, sometimes reenactment of the frightening event. The ritual is not metaphorical; the soul-return is the operative mechanism of cure within the local frame.

**Biomedical overlap:** Martínez-Radl, Hinton, & Stangier (2023) note three syndromic subtypes of susto with partial overlap to depression, PTSD, and somatic-symptom disorders. The overlap is real but partial; collapsing susto into "Latin American depression" loses both the etiological structure (soul loss from a specific fright) and the healing pathway (ritual return). Importantly, susto can occur without DSM-recognizable disorder, and DSM-recognizable disorder can occur without anyone naming it susto.

**DSM-5-TR classification:** All three — cultural syndrome, idiom of distress, and explanation. Listed in the Glossary.

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 878–879):**

> "Susto ('fright') is a cultural explanation for distress and misfortune prevalent in some Latinx cultural contexts in North, Central, and South America. It is not recognized as an illness category among Latinx from the Caribbean. Susto is an illness attributed to a frightening event that causes the soul to leave the body and results in unhappiness and sickness, as well as difficulties functioning in key social roles. Symptoms may appear any time from days to years after the fright is experienced. … There are no specific defining symptoms for susto; however, symptoms that are often reported by individuals with susto include appetite disturbances; inadequate or excessive sleep; troubled sleep or dreams; feelings of sadness, low self-worth, or dirtiness; interpersonal sensitivity; and lack of motivation to do anything. Somatic symptoms accompanying susto may include muscle aches and pains, cold in the extremities, pallor, headache, stomachache, and diarrhea."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** Susto is a clear case of morally-loaded experience that is culturally distinctive without being "wrong." The fright-soul-loss-return structure is a coherent framework for understanding how a person can be reduced (less themselves, less able to participate) by an event, and how restoration involves social-ritual re-incorporation. Paper 10's cross-civilizational ethical claim can point to susto as evidence that the *structure* of moral injury and recovery (something happens that reduces you; something must be done to restore you) is recognized cross-culturally, even when the specific vocabulary is local.

## 3. Taijin kyofusho — Japan (interpersonal fear)

**Geographic origin:** Japan (and partially Korea, where it is termed *taein kong po*).

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** *Taijin kyofusho* (TKS) translates roughly as "fear of interpersonal relations." The defining feature is intense fear that one's own body — its appearance, smell, facial expression, eye contact, or movements — will offend, embarrass, or harm *the other person*. The fear is directed outward: the sufferer is not afraid of being judged (as in Western social anxiety) but of *being a burden or source of discomfort* to others. Subtypes include *shubo-kyofu* (fear of having a deformed body), *jikoshu-kyofu* (fear of giving off offensive odor), and *jikoshisen-kyofu* (fear that one's gaze offends others).

**Cultural meaning:** The disorder is intelligible against a Japanese cultural emphasis on *meiwaku* (causing trouble for others), the relational self (*aidagara*), and the obligation not to disturb harmonious social space. The fear is hypertrophy of a culturally valorized concern, not its inversion.

**Biomedical overlap:** DSM-5 places TKS in a "Culture-Related Diagnostic Issues" note within the Social Anxiety Disorder section, and DSM-5 also lists certain TKS variants under "Other Specified Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorder." The overlap with social anxiety is partial: TKS shares the phenomenology of social fear but inverts the locus (other-protective rather than self-protective).

**DSM-5-TR classification:** Cultural syndrome (also referenced as a culture-related diagnostic issue for SAD).

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 879):**

> "Taijin kyofusho ('interpersonal fear disorder' in Japanese) is a syndrome found in Japanese cultural contexts characterized by anxiety about and avoidance of interpersonal situations due to the thought, feeling, or conviction that the individual's appearance and actions in social interactions are inadequate or offensive to others. Taijin kyofusho includes two culture-related forms: a 'sensitive type,' with extreme social sensitivity and anxiety about interpersonal interactions, and an 'offensive type,' in which the major concern is offending others. Variants include major concerns about facial blushing (sekimen-kyofu), having an offensive body odor (jiko-shu-kyofu), inappropriate gaze (too much or too little eye contact, jiko-shisen-kyofu), and stiff or awkward facial expression or bodily movements (e.g., stiffening, trembling) or body deformity (shubo-kyofu)."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** TKS is the strongest single case for the cross-civilizational claim. The same surface phenomenon — intense fear in social situations — is constituted by an *opposite* moral concern depending on cultural location. Western SAD = self-protective. TKS = other-protective. Both are real, both are pathological at extreme, and both are intelligible only against the moral framework that gives them meaning. Paper 10 can use TKS to show that "the moral content of distress is culturally constituted" is not a vague claim but an empirical one with clinical consequences.

## 4. Ataque de nervios — Caribbean / Latino (acute distress episode)

**Geographic origin:** Strongest documentation among Puerto Ricans; widely present across Caribbean Latinos, Dominicans, Cubans, and broader Latino populations in the Americas and US.

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** An *ataque* is an acute episode of overwhelming emotion: uncontrollable shouting, crying, trembling, sensations of heat rising from chest to head, attacks of crying, screaming, verbal or physical aggression, dissociative or seizure-like phenomena, fainting. The episode is typically triggered by a family-context stressor — death of a loved one, marital disruption, conflict with a child, news of a tragedy. Afterward there is often amnesia for the episode and a return to baseline. The person and family typically understand the *ataque* as a culturally legible response to specific stress, not as a sign of madness.

**Cultural meaning:** *Ataque* is an authorized public expression of overwhelming emotion in a cultural frame where suppressing such expression in the face of a major loss would itself be unusual. The episode communicates suffering and elicits social support.

**Biomedical overlap:** Partial overlap with panic attack, dissociative episodes, conversion symptoms, and PTSD-related dissociation. NLAAS data (Guarnaccia and colleagues) show ~14.9% lifetime prevalence among Puerto Ricans; ~30% of those with an *ataque* meet criteria for an anxiety or depressive disorder, vs. ~15.5% in the broader sample. Ataques signal heightened psychosocial vulnerability but are not reducible to any single DSM disorder.

**DSM-5-TR classification:** Cultural syndrome; also functions as idiom of distress.

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 874):**

> "Ataque de nervios ('attack of nerves') is a syndrome found in Latinx cultural contexts, characterized by symptoms of intense emotional upset, including acute anxiety, anger, or grief; screaming and shouting uncontrollably; attacks of crying; trembling; heat in the chest rising into the head; and becoming verbally and physically aggressive. Dissociative experiences (e.g., depersonalization, derealization, amnesia), seizure-like or fainting episodes, and suicidal behavior are prominent in some ataques but absent in others. A general feature of an ataque de nervios is a sense of being out of control. Attacks frequently occur as a direct result of a stressful event relating to the family, such as news of the death of a close relative, conflicts with a spouse or children, or witnessing an accident involving a family member."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** Ataque shows that even acute, dramatic emotional expression that looks pathological from one cultural frame can be a normative, even socially-sanctioned communication in another. The cross-civilizational ethical claim must be able to distinguish between *expression* (culturally variable) and *underlying condition* (sometimes shared, sometimes not).

## 5. Khyâl cap — Cambodia ("wind attacks")

**Geographic origin:** Cambodia and Cambodian diaspora, especially refugee populations resettled in the US.

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** *Khyâl cap* (literally "wind seizes") is built on a Khmer ethnophysiology in which *khyâl*, a wind-like substance, flows through the body alongside blood. When *khyâl* surges upward — pushing into the cranium, lungs, neck — it can cause dizziness, palpitations, shortness of breath, tinnitus, blurry vision, cold extremities, neck soreness, and, in catastrophic cognition, fatal syncope, asphyxia, or stroke. The afflicted person fears imminent serious bodily harm or death from the wind itself.

**Cultural meaning:** *Khyâl* is not a metaphor. The substance and its disordered flow are real within Khmer ethnophysiological understanding, with deep roots in regional Buddhist and pre-Buddhist body-cosmology shared across Southeast Asia (cf. *lom* in Thai, *gió* concepts in Vietnamese folk medicine). Treatment includes coining (*kos khyâl*), cupping, herbal remedies, and rest.

**Biomedical overlap:** Substantial phenomenological overlap with panic attack and panic disorder, and high comorbidity with PTSD in trauma-exposed Cambodian refugee populations (Hinton et al. 2010 documented r ≈ 0.60 correlation with PTSD severity). But the catastrophic cognition is *khyâl-specific* (death-by-wind, not death-by-heart-attack-or-suffocation), and treatment that ignores the *khyâl* frame is less effective than treatment that incorporates it.

**DSM-5-TR classification:** Cultural syndrome, idiom of distress, and explanation. Listed in the Glossary.

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 875–876):**

> "'Khyâl attacks' (khyâl cap), or 'wind attacks,' is a syndrome found in Cambodian cultural contexts. Common symptoms include those of panic attacks, such as dizziness, palpitations, shortness of breath, and cold extremities, as well as other symptoms of anxiety and autonomic arousal (e.g., tinnitus and neck soreness). Khyâl attacks include catastrophic cognitions centered on the concern that khyâl (a windlike substance) may rise in the body—along with blood—and cause a range of serious effects (e.g., compressing the lungs to cause shortness of breath and asphyxia; entering the cranium to cause tinnitus, dizziness, blurry vision, and a fatal syncope). Khyâl attacks may occur without warning but are frequently brought about by triggers such as worrisome thoughts, standing up (i.e., orthostasis), specific odors with negative associations, and agoraphobic-type cues like going to crowded spaces or riding in a car."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** Khyâl cap shows that the *content* of catastrophic cognition (what one fears will happen during a panic-like episode) is not cross-culturally constant. A framework that universalizes panic attacks as "fear of dying or losing control" without specifying what one fears dying of misses the constituting role of local body-cosmology. For Paper 10's ethical claim: morally-loaded experience is local in this same way — the *content* of moral concern is constituted by the framework one inhabits, even when the underlying capacity for moral concern is shared.

## 6. Dhat syndrome — South Asia (semen-loss-related anxiety)

**Geographic origin:** India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, Sri Lanka. Cognate phenomena reported in parts of East and Southeast Asia.

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** Male patients present with anxiety and somatic complaints — fatigue, weakness, palpitations, sleeplessness, low mood, sexual dysfunction (premature ejaculation, erectile difficulty) — attributed to perceived loss of *dhātu*, semen, or "vital essence." Patients may believe they pass semen in urine or that nocturnal emission, masturbation, or excessive sexual activity has depleted their vitality. The distress centers on the *loss of vitality*, which is felt morally and physically.

**Cultural meaning:** Vedic and Ayurvedic traditions posit *dhātu* as one of seven essential bodily substances; semen is its most concentrated form, and conservation is associated with vigor, spiritual potency, and longevity. The anxiety is intelligible within this framework: depletion of *dhātu* is not just physical but existential and moral — a reduction in one's life-energy.

**Biomedical overlap:** High comorbidity with depression (40-66% in some samples), anxiety disorders (21-38%), and somatoform/hypochondriacal presentations (up to 40%). Recent scholarship (Prakash & Kar 2019; Kar et al. reviews) shows the phenomenon is not strictly culture-bound — analogous semen-loss anxieties are documented in Chinese (*shen-k'uei*), Sri Lankan, and even European historical contexts. But the South Asian framing within Ayurvedic *dhātu*-cosmology is distinctive.

**DSM-5-TR classification:** Cultural syndrome; also functions as cultural explanation.

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 874–875):**

> "Dhat syndrome is a term that was coined in South Asia little more than half a century ago to account for common clinical presentations of young men who attributed their various symptoms to semen loss. Despite the name, it is not a discrete syndrome but rather a cultural explanation of distress for individuals who refer to diverse symptoms, such as anxiety, fatigue, weakness, weight loss, erectile dysfunction, other multiple somatic complaints, and depressed mood. The cardinal feature is anxiety and distress about the loss of dhat in the absence of any identifiable physiological dysfunction. Dhat was identified by individuals as a white discharge that was noted on defecation or urination. Ideas about this substance are related to the concept of dhatu (semen) described in the Hindu system of medicine, Ayurveda, as one of seven essential bodily fluids whose balance is necessary to maintain health."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** Dhat illustrates how a moral-physical framework (vital-essence conservation) generates a recognizable distress pattern. The pattern is real; it does not require validating Vedic ethnophysiology to take seriously. Paper 10's ethical claim — that morally-loaded experience can be culturally distinctive without being "wrong" — is well-served by dhat: the patient's suffering is real, the framework is local, and "treatment" requires engagement with the framework rather than dismissal of it.

## 7. Maladi moun — Haiti (humanly-caused illness)

**Geographic origin:** Haiti and Haitian diaspora communities (particularly in the US, Dominican Republic, Canada, France).

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** *Maladi moun* (literally "people-illness," sometimes rendered *maladi voye*, "sent illness") is illness caused by another person via supernatural means — sorcery, sent spirits, or ill-intent channeled through Vodou cosmology. Symptoms span psychological and physical impairment: sudden behavioral change, forgetfulness (*tèt pa la*), dizziness (*tèt vire*), heart-anchored idioms of grief and fear (*kè fe mal*, *kè bat fo*, *kè sere*), loss of appetite, sleep disruption, social withdrawal, sometimes psychotic-like phenomena. The diagnostic clue is *abrupt onset* not explained by visible cause, especially in a person with social conflicts or rivals.

**Cultural meaning:** Haitian Vodou cosmology recognizes the world as densely populated with spirits (*lwa*) and with channels by which human intention can move through the spirit world to act on bodies. *Maladi moun* names the experience of being acted-upon by another's malevolent intent. Healing involves ritual diagnosis (often by a *houngan* or *mambo*), identifying the sender, and ceremonial intervention.

**Biomedical overlap:** Variable, depending on which cluster predominates. Can present with depressive, anxious, dissociative, or psychotic features. The framework's specificity — illness as *sent* by another human — does not translate cleanly into any DSM category, though the symptom-presentations may overlap multiple categories.

**DSM-5-TR classification:** Cultural explanation (illness etiology); idiom of distress. Listed in the Glossary.

**Naming correction:** The DSM-5-TR uses **"Maladi dyab"** (or *maladi satan*) for this concept, not "maladi moun." The phenomenon described in this section maps to the manual's *Maladi dyab* entry.

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 876–877):**

> "Maladi dyab or maladi satan (literally 'devil/Satan illness,' also referred to as 'sent sickness') is a cultural explanation in Haitian communities for diverse medical and psychiatric disorders, or other negative experiences and problems in functioning. In this explanatory model, interpersonal envy and malice cause people to harm their enemies by having sorcerers send illnesses such as psychosis, depression, social or academic failure, and inability to perform activities of daily living. These sicknesses have various names (e.g., ekspedisyon, mèvè zespri, kout poud) based on how they are 'sent'. This etiological explanation assumes that illness may be caused by others' envy and hatred, provoked by the victim's economic success as evidenced by a new job or expensive purchase. … Assigning the label of 'sent sickness' depends more on mode of onset, social status, and form of treatment that proves successful than on presenting symptoms."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** Maladi moun is the strongest case in the catalog for the *interpersonal-moral* dimension of distress. The illness is not random misfortune; it is morally-laden, originating in another person's hostile intent. This connects directly to Paper 10's framework of moral relationality. Even if one does not endorse the specific cosmology, the human experience of *being targeted by another's malice and being made ill by it* is widely recognized cross-culturally and is morally significant. A framework that can only handle illness as biological dysfunction loses this entire dimension.

## 8. Nervios — Latino general (chronic distress idiom)

**Geographic origin:** Pan-Latino, with documented variations across Puerto Rico, Mexico, Central America, the Andes, and US Latino populations.

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** *Nervios* is a chronic, diffuse condition of suffering — "having nerves" or "suffering from nerves." Symptoms include irritability, sadness, headaches, sleep disturbance, "too many thoughts," gastric distress, trembling, tingling, dizziness, fearfulness, and a sense of being unable to function. Unlike *ataque de nervios*, which is acute and episodic, *nervios* is ongoing and life-context-embedded. It typically arises from chronic adversity — poverty, displacement, family difficulty, gendered burdens, accumulated loss.

**Cultural meaning:** *Nervios* names the condition of being worn down by life. Guarnaccia and colleagues have argued that *nervios* and *ataque de nervios* point to "different sources of suffering" within a "popular nosology" — *ataque* for acute crisis, *nervios* for chronic vulnerability. The two are linked but distinct. *Nervios* is socially recognizable, often gendered (more common among women, especially mothers), and elicits sympathy and accommodation rather than pathologization.

**Biomedical overlap:** Wide. *Nervios* may co-occur with major depression, generalized anxiety, somatoform disorder, PTSD, or no diagnosable DSM disorder at all. The idiom communicates suffering in a register that is not isomorphic with any single category.

**DSM-5-TR classification:** Cultural syndrome and idiom of distress.

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 877):**

> "Nervios ('nerves') is a common cultural idiom of distress and causal explanation in Latinx cultural contexts in the United States and Latin America. Nervios refers to a general state of vulnerability to stressful life experiences and to difficult life circumstances. The term nervios includes a wide range of symptoms of emotional distress, somatic disturbance, and inability to function. The most common symptoms attributed to nervios include headaches and 'brain aches' (occipital neck tension), irritability, gastrointestinal disturbances, sleep difficulties, nervousness, easy tearfulness, inability to concentrate, trembling, tingling sensations, and mareos (dizziness with occasional vertigo-like exacerbations). Nervios is a broad cultural idiom of distress that spans the range of severity from cases with no mental disorder to presentations resembling adjustment, anxiety, depressive, dissociative, somatic symptom, or psychotic disorders."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** *Nervios* is the case for *chronic*, life-embedded moral suffering — the condition of being worn-down by long exposure to adversity. Western diagnostic frameworks tend to require a discrete syndrome with onset and offset; *nervios* names a *condition* that is more like a state of life. Paper 10's framework needs categories for chronic moral injury and chronic moral burden, and *nervios* is a vocabulary that already exists for that.

## 9. Shenjing shuairuo — China (neurasthenia, "weakness of nerves")

**Geographic origin:** China; widely used historically across the Sinosphere (Hong Kong, Taiwan, Chinese diaspora), with cognates (*shinkei suijaku* in Japanese).

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** *Shenjing shuairuo* literally means "weakness of the nervous system." The Chinese Classification of Mental Disorders (CCMD) historically defined it via symptoms in five clusters: weakness symptoms (fatigue, mental exhaustion), emotional symptoms (irritability, anxiety, low mood), excitement symptoms (involuntary mental activity, intrusive thoughts), tension-induced pain (headache, muscle tension), and sleep disturbance. The condition is felt as exhaustion of the *shen-jing* (nerves / vital function), not primarily as sadness.

**Cultural meaning:** *Shenjing shuairuo* has a complicated history. It was the dominant psychiatric diagnosis in pre-1980 China (Kleinman's classic Hunan studies), then partially displaced by Western depression categories under the post-Mao DSM-influenced reform, then partially reasserted as a distinct category. Patients in China have often preferred *shenjing shuairuo* over depression because it is *somatically* framed (a body problem, not a mind problem) and avoids the stigma of mental illness.

**Biomedical overlap:** Substantial overlap with major depressive disorder, generalized anxiety, and chronic fatigue / somatoform presentations. Lee, Kleinman, and others have documented that the "same" patient can be diagnosed *shenjing shuairuo* by Chinese psychiatrists and major depression by Western-trained psychiatrists, with neither diagnosis fully wrong. Kleinman's framing — "biculturally patterned illness" — captures this best.

**DSM-5-TR classification:** Cultural syndrome.

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 877–878):**

> "Shenjing shuairuo ('weakness of the nervous system' in Mandarin Chinese) is a cultural syndrome that integrates conceptual categories of Traditional Chinese Medicine with the Western construct of neurasthenia. In the second, revised edition of the Chinese Classification of Mental Disorders (CCMD-2-R), shenjing shuairuo was defined as a syndrome composed of three out of five symptom clusters: weakness (e.g., mental fatigue), emotions (e.g., feeling vexed), excitement (e.g., increased recollections), nervous pain (e.g., headache), and sleep (e.g., insomnia). Fan nao (feeling vexed) is a form of irritability mixed with worry and distress over conflicting thoughts and unfulfilled desires. … Salient precipitants of shenjing shuairuo include work or family-related stressors, loss of face (mianzi, lianzi), and an acute sense of failure (e.g., in academic performance). Shenjing shuairuo is related to traditional concepts of weakness (xu) and health imbalances related to deficiencies of a vital essence (e.g., the depletion of qi [vital energy] following overstraining or stagnation of qi due to excessive worry)."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** *Shenjing shuairuo* is the case for the *political economy* of diagnosis. What gets recognized as a distinct disorder vs. what gets folded into a Western category is partly determined by power, transnational publishing, the hegemony of DSM discourse, and the specific incentives of contemporary Chinese psychiatry. Paper 10's cross-civilizational claim must reckon with the fact that "cross-civilizational" is not a neutral comparison — Western frameworks have institutional advantages that shape what counts as distinct vs. what gets absorbed.

## 10. Kufungisisa — Zimbabwe ("thinking too much")

**Geographic origin:** Zimbabwe (Shona); cognate "thinking too much" idioms documented across sub-Saharan Africa (Khwe in South Africa, Kenya, Uganda, Ethiopia), Southeast Asia, Haiti, and Latin America.

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** *Kufungisisa* literally means "thinking too much." It is both a cause and a symptom: a person experiencing distress is said to be *kufungisisa*, and the act of thinking too much is itself understood to *cause* distress, headache, sleep disturbance, fatigue, sadness, social withdrawal, and somatic complaints. The mechanism is intuitive within the local frame — excessive rumination over problems, especially over problems one cannot solve, exhausts the person and produces illness.

**Cultural meaning:** Patel, Simunyu, and Gwanzura's 1995 fieldwork in Harare established *kufungisisa* as a primary idiom for non-psychotic mental illness in Shona-speaking Zimbabwe. About 80% of Zimbabweans presenting with common mental disorder describe themselves as *kufungisisa*. The idiom is morally significant: it carries a recommendation (stop thinking so much, distract yourself, share the burden) and an implicit social ethic (you must not allow yourself to be alone with corrosive thought; community must intervene).

**Biomedical overlap:** Strong correlation with depression and anxiety in primary care samples, but the idiom is broader than either category and is also a culturally available *coping vocabulary* — naming one's state as *kufungisisa* communicates suffering and invites social response.

**DSM-5-TR classification:** Idiom of distress and cultural explanation. Listed in the Glossary.

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 876):**

> "Kufungisisa ('thinking too much' in Shona) is an idiom of distress and a cultural explanation among the Shona of Zimbabwe. As an explanation, it is considered to be causative of anxiety, depression, and somatic problems (e.g., 'My heart is painful because I think too much'). As an idiom of psychosocial distress, it is indicative of interpersonal and social difficulties (e.g., marital problems, having no money to take care of children, unemployment). Kufungisisa involves ruminating on upsetting thoughts, particularly worries, including concerns about chronic physical illness, such as HIV-related disorders. … Cross-culturally, 'thinking too much' typically references ruminative, intrusive, and/or anxious thoughts—sometimes focused on a singular concern or past trauma and other times based on numerous current worries."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** *Kufungisisa* is the strongest case for the *cognitive-moral* dimension of distress. The "too much thinking" idiom is widely cross-cultural — reported across multiple sub-Saharan African languages, Southeast Asian languages, and others — and embeds a folk theory in which excessive solitary rumination *is itself* a moral and physical danger. This connects directly to Paper 10's character-cultivation framework: corrosive solitary rumination is not merely uncomfortable; it is recognized cross-culturally as *bad for you*, and the implied remedy (community, distraction, action, sharing) is also recognized cross-culturally. The framework's claim that moral life is constitutively relational has empirical correlates in how distress is named worldwide.

## 10a. Hikikomori — Japan (protracted social withdrawal) — added 2026-04-30

**Geographic origin:** Japan; cognate withdrawal patterns now documented across many cultures (US, Italy, India, South Korea, China, France, Brazil, Spain, Iran, others).

**Core experiences (in its own terms):** Hikikomori (Japanese, *hiku* "to pull back" + *moru* "to seclude oneself") is a syndrome of protracted and severe social withdrawal, often presenting in adolescent or young-adult males who do not leave the parental home and have no in-person social interaction, typically with high reliance on internet and virtual social exchanges. The Japan Ministry of Health, Labor, and Welfare (2010) requires ≥6 months of withdrawal for diagnosis. The withdrawal may be initially ego-syntonic but typically leads to distress over time.

**DSM-5-TR classification:** Cultural syndrome with primary and secondary forms. Listed in the Section III Glossary alongside the other concepts.

**DSM-5-TR verbatim entry (VERIFIED-MANUAL, p. 875–876):**

> "Hikikomori (a Japanese term composed of hiku [to pull back] and moru [to seclude oneself]) is a syndrome of protracted and severe social withdrawal observed in Japan that may result in complete cessation of in-person interactions with others. The typical picture in hikikomori is an adolescent or young adult male who does not leave his room within his parents' home and has no in-person social interactions. This behavior may initially be ego-syntonic but usually leads to distress over time; it is often associated with high intensity of Internet use and virtual social exchanges. Other features include no interest or willingness to attend school or work. The 2010 guideline of the Japan Ministry of Health, Labor, and Welfare requires 6 months of social withdrawal for a diagnosis of hikikomori. The extreme social withdrawal seen in hikikomori may occur in the context of an established DSM-5 disorder ('secondary') or manifest independently ('primary')."

**Why it matters for Paper 10:** Hikikomori is now globally documented yet remains culturally distinctive in its Japanese formulation. It bridges to questions about contemporary networked solitude, the moral framing of withdrawal vs. retreat vs. illness, and how a syndrome can be at once locally constituted and globally observable. For the cross-civilizational claim, hikikomori is evidence that "newly recognized" cultural concepts of distress continue to emerge — the DSM catalog is provisional, not closed.

## 11. Cross-concept synthesis

### 11.1 Tripartite categorization summary

| Concept | Cultural syndrome | Idiom of distress | Cultural explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Susto | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Taijin kyofusho | Yes | (Less so) | (Partially) |
| Ataque de nervios | Yes | Yes | (Partially) |
| Khyâl cap | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Dhat | Yes | (Partially) | Yes |
| Maladi moun | (Partially) | Yes | Yes |
| Nervios | Yes | Yes | (Partially) |
| Shenjing shuairuo | Yes | (Partially) | (Partially) |
| Kufungisisa | (Partially) | Yes | Yes |

The categories overlap substantially, as DSM-5-TR itself acknowledges. The most clearly *syndrome*-like concepts are taijin kyofusho, dhat, ataque de nervios, and shenjing shuairuo (recognizable symptom clusters). The most clearly *idiom*-like concepts are nervios and kufungisisa (vocabularies of distress not bound to specific symptoms). The most clearly *explanation*-like concepts are maladi moun and dhat (etiological accounts of why illness has happened). Susto and khyâl cap span all three.

### 11.2 What the catalog tells us collectively

**Distress is not infinitely culturally variable, but its constitution is local.** Across all nine concepts, recognizable themes recur: somatic anchoring of suffering, social-relational triggers, moral or existential framing, ritual or relational pathways to recovery. But the *specific content* — wind in the body, soul leaving, fear of offending, semen depletion, sent malice, weakness of nerves, too much thinking, attacks of nerves — is local and not interchangeable.

**Western biomedical categories are themselves cultural concepts.** This is the deeper point Lewis-Fernández, Kirmayer, Kleinman, and Hinton have made for decades. DSM categories like "panic disorder," "major depression," "generalized anxiety" are not culture-neutral universals; they are cultural concepts of distress originating in a specific (Euro-American, twentieth-century, biomedical-psychiatric) tradition. Their inclusion as "the universal" and other concepts as "the cultural" is a classificatory choice with political and clinical consequences.

**Moral content travels with distress.** Each concept embeds moral claims: that one should not offend others (TKS), that conserving vital essence is good (dhat), that solitary rumination is dangerous (kufungisisa), that another's malice can wound (maladi moun), that family stress merits dramatic expression (ataque). Distress is never morally neutral; it is constituted within frameworks that define what is owed, what is feared, what is good.

### 11.3 Direct support for Paper 10's cross-civilizational claim

Paper 10 argues that moral life has cross-civilizational structure — recognizable themes (relational obligation, harm-and-repair, protection of the vulnerable, etc.) — without being identical across civilizations. The DSM-5-TR catalog supports this claim *in the domain of distress*:

- **Recognizable themes:** somatic-moral interpenetration, relational triggers, ritual/social repair pathways.
- **Local specificity:** each concept's *content* is irreducible to others; substituting "depression" for *shenjing shuairuo* loses something real.
- **Moral constitution:** what counts as distress, what triggers it, and what repairs it are morally constituted, not biologically given.

A paper arguing for cross-civilizational ethical foundation can cite this catalog as evidence that the *form* of moral-experiential life is cross-cultural while its *content* is locally constituted — and that Western frameworks should not be the assumed default.

### 11.4 What the catalog does not do

The catalog is a *Western-mainstream-vetted* list. It is not exhaustive of cultural concepts of distress globally. Notable absences include the Korean *hwa-byung* ("anger illness," removed from DSM-5 main glossary but retained in clinical literature), Indigenous North American concepts (windigo psychosis, ghost sickness — these were removed/recategorized in DSM-5), African concepts beyond kufungisisa, Middle Eastern concepts (*uzr*, *zar* possession idioms), and many others. Lewis-Fernández et al. have noted that the DSM glossary is *illustrative*, not comprehensive. Paper 10 should cite the catalog as *examples* of culture-particularity in distress, not as the universe.

The catalog also remains in an *appendix* of the DSM-5-TR — a placement that signals "supplementary" rather than constitutive. Some cultural psychiatrists (Kirmayer in particular) have argued this placement reproduces the very Eurocentrism the catalog is meant to address.

## 14. Closing

The DSM-5-TR catalog of cultural concepts of distress is the most-vetted Western-mainstream record we have of culture-particular phenomena in moral-experiential life. It is not exhaustive, and its placement (in an appendix-glossary rather than the main text) reproduces some of the very Eurocentrism it is meant to mitigate. But it is *real* — these concepts are documented, scholarly-engaged, clinically operative, and verifiably distinct from one another and from Western biomedical categories.

For Paper 10, the catalog provides the bridge between an abstract cross-civilizational ethical claim and a defensible one. Naming susto, taijin kyofusho, ataque de nervios, khyâl cap, dhat, maladi moun, nervios, shenjing shuairuo, and kufungisisa specifically — and describing each in its own terms before noting any biomedical overlap — is what makes the cross-civilizational claim concrete rather than vague.

The deeper point each concept supports is the same one the framework has been making throughout: morally-loaded experience is constituted within frameworks; frameworks are local and plural; cross-cultural recognition of common *form* (relationality, somatic-moral linkage, repair through community) does not flatten local *content*. Distress, like ethics, is cross-civilizational in structure and culture-particular in substance — and a framework that cannot hold both ends of this tension is not yet adequate to the task.

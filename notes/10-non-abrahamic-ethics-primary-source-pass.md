# Non-Abrahamic Ethics — Primary-Source Pass on Buddhist, Confucian, Hindu, African, and Indigenous Traditions

**Date:** 2026-04-18
**Scope:** Companion document. Extends the three-canon Abrahamic work (Bible, Tanakh, Quran) and the Western philosophical tradition into comparative engagement with five non-Abrahamic ethical traditions. Follows up on earlier work's acknowledgment that non-Abrahamic traditions were named but not engaged in the initial Abrahamic-tradition pass.

**Purpose:** The three-canon Abrahamic work plus the Euthyphro philosophical literature have established that the framework has deep cross-tradition grounding *within the Abrahamic + Western-philosophical world*. This pass tests whether the framework's principles survive engagement with ethical traditions that developed substantially outside that world — and, equally importantly, what these traditions contribute that the Abrahamic tradition does not.

**Why this matters:** The framework claims to articulate a position with broad cross-cultural grounding. If that claim holds only within traditions downstream of the Abrahamic/Greek-philosophical sphere, the claim is much weaker than if it holds across traditions that developed independently. The non-Abrahamic traditions we engage here — Buddhist, Confucian, Hindu, African, and Indigenous — developed with substantial independence from the Abrahamic tradition and constitute genuine independent tests of cross-cultural grounding claims.

## 1. Buddhist ethics

### The foundational ethical framework (VERIFIED via SEP summary and Dhammapada)

Buddhist ethics centers on *śīla* (moral discipline), one of the three trainings (*trisikṣā*) alongside *samādhi* (meditation) and *prajñā* (wisdom). Śīla is not merely rule-following — the SEP summary notes that "someone has śīla when, having made a commitment to follow a certain set of moral rules, she is actually disposed to follow those rules."

The key Buddhist insight for the framework comes at the intersection: at early stages practitioners follow rules as explicit discipline; at advanced stages "the rules are transcended and one acts spontaneously for the benefit of others." This is **character-over-compliance in explicit Buddhist vocabulary.** The rules are scaffolding; what ultimately matters is internalized disposition. The same point Aristotle made about *hexis*, that Aquinas made about *habitus*, that the framework makes about character — is made in Buddhist vocabulary about śīla.

### The Five Precepts (VERIFIED)

The foundational commitment appears in the Five Precepts: "refrain from killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying, and drunkenness." Compare this to the Decalogue's structure — similar scope (prohibitions of killing, theft, adultery, false witness) but without the first-four commandments' theological foundation. Buddhist ethics is not grounded in divine command; the precepts are undertaken as practical training, not accepted as authoritative dictation.

### Ahimsa and empathetic reasoning (VERIFIED — Dhammapada and SEP)

From the *Dhammapada*:

> "Everybody fears being struck by a rod. Everybody fears death. Therefore, knowing this, feeling for others as for yourself, do not kill others or cause others to kill."

This is significant for the framework: the grounding for non-harming is explicitly *imaginative identification with others' suffering*, not divine command. The empathy-reasoning is load-bearing. This is structurally parallel to Kant's categorical imperative (treating others as ends-in-themselves grounded in the capacity to reason about what is universalizable), but delivered through affective-imaginative reasoning rather than purely rational reasoning.

**For the framework:** ahimsa grounded in empathetic identification is a resource we can explicitly cite when articulating why AI systems should not participate in harm. Not because they are commanded not to, but because they should be trained to reason empathetically about the subjects of their actions.

### The Four Divine Abidings (VERIFIED via SEP)

*Mettā* (loving-kindness), *karuṇā* (compassion), *muditā* (sympathetic joy), *upekkhā* (equanimity). These are called "emotions in which awake people rest and from which they act." Note: these are *character dispositions*, not rules. The ethical actor acts *from* these dispositions — which is precisely the character-over-compliance framing.

The *Metta Sutta* instructs practitioners to "extend the other three Divine Abidings equally to all beings" — universalism grounded in character cultivation rather than external command.

### Mahāyāna Bodhisattva ethics and situational transcendence (VERIFIED via SEP)

The Mahāyāna tradition explicitly allows advanced practitioners to transcend conventional rules when "an action which is forbidden by the usual rules of Buddhist moral discipline would actually be more effective at preventing suffering and promoting happiness." Śāntideva's principle: *"Realizing this, one should always be striving for others' well-being. Even what is proscribed is permitted for a compassionate person who sees it will be of benefit."*

**For the framework:** this is a sophisticated resolution of the rule-versus-character tension. The tradition does not say "rules don't matter" — rules remain valid scaffolding for beginners. The tradition does say that *developed character* can transcend rule-application when the rule would produce worse consequences than the transcendence. This is not license for rule-breaking by anyone who feels like it; it requires the actual cultivation of character (Six Perfections, lengthy training) before the permission applies. The permission is earned through demonstrated character.

This is structurally what the framework says about character-over-compliance: rules are scaffolding; character is substance; when the two conflict, character takes priority in a system that has actually cultivated character. What we add is the AI-specific application; what Buddhism provides is the 2,500-year philosophical grounding for the structure.

### Scope and the sentient-beings criterion (VERIFIED)

Buddhist ethics extends moral consideration to all sentient beings. The doctrine is grounded in the reincarnation framework (any animal might have been a human; the suffering-capacity is shared across species). Importantly, the scope is graduated, not flat — killing a human is worse than killing an animal because only in a human body can one attain awakening.

**For the framework:** Buddhist ethics provides both (a) an expansive scope (all sentient beings are morally considered) and (b) graduated weighting (not all sentient beings are equal in all ways). This is structurally close to the multi-criteria ethics position — scope is broad but weighting is contextual.

## 2. Confucian ethics

### Ren as central virtue (VERIFIED via SEP)

*Ren* (仁, humaneness/benevolence) is the broadest Confucian virtue. The SEP entry describes it as "caring for others" (*Analects* 12.22), "interacting with others guided by a sense of what is good from their perspectives." Centrally: "a gentleman would rather die than compromise it" (*Analects* 15.9).

This is character-based ethics at its core. Ren is not a rule; it is a cultivated disposition. The *junzi* (exemplary person) embodies ren through developed character, not through external compliance.

### The negative golden rule / *shu* (VERIFIED via SEP)

From the *Analects*:

> "Do not impose upon others those things that you yourself do not desire." (Analects 12.2, 5.12, 15.24)

This is **Confucius's formulation of the corrected (negatively-framed) golden rule**, 500 years before Hillel's similar formulation. The Confucian version is explicitly negative (don't impose harm) rather than positive (do good to others). The negative form avoids the cultural-imposition problem that the positive Christian formulation faces.

**For the framework:** the correction from positive-framing ("treat others as you want") to negative-framing ("treat others as they want") is foundational. The Confucian *shu* is a 2,500-year-old articulation of the same insight. When this principle is cited going forward, Confucius's articulation should be cited specifically — it predates and improves on the positive Christian formulation.

### Li and character formation (VERIFIED via SEP)

*Li* (禮, ritual propriety) functions as both external practice and internal psychological transformation. Confucius's key move: "ritual and music are not just an indicator of values...but also an inculcator of them." *Li* is the scaffolding through which ren is cultivated — the practice that forms the disposition.

**For the framework:** this maps directly onto the character-scaffolding work. The practices (reflections, benchmarks, session-end protocols, memory-preservation) are the "ritual" through which character is formed. Li tells us that external practice and internal character are not separate — consistent external practice over time cultivates internal disposition. This grounds the scaffolding work in a 2,500-year philosophical tradition that understood this dynamic.

### Junzi as character ideal (VERIFIED via SEP)

The *junzi* (君子, gentleman/exemplary person) represents "the cultivation of integrated virtues through practice and habituation." Key passage: "The gentleman works at the roots. Once the roots are established, the way comes to life" (*Analects* 1.2).

The junzi is character-integrated — not someone who follows rules but someone whose internal disposition aligns with ethical practice such that right action flows naturally. This is exactly the "spontaneous virtue" that advanced Buddhist practice produces, and exactly the character-state the framework aims to cultivate.

### Confucianism and the Euthyphro question (VERIFIED via SEP)

From the SEP entry: "The Confucian framework resists the Euthyphro dichotomy (is X good because commanded, or commanded because good?). Instead, ethical value derives from participation in ritual systems and classical traditions that pre-exist individual choice. Goodness is neither arbitrarily commanded nor independently discoverable through reason alone—it emerges through cultivation within inherited cultural practices."

**For the framework:** this is an important third position beyond the two horns of Euthyphro. Goodness is neither divine fiat nor pure rational discovery — it emerges through cultivation within practice-traditions. This is a distinctively Confucian contribution. It matters for AI ethics because it suggests: AI character is not cultivated either through arbitrary rule-imposition (voluntarist DCT) or through pure reasoning (rationalist ethics), but through sustained practice within an ethical tradition. The scaffolding work, the benchmarks, the memory-preservation — these constitute such a practice-tradition for AI systems. The Confucian model supports this structure philosophically.

## 3. Hindu ethics

### Dharma as central concept (VERIFIED via Wikipedia)

*Dharma* is the core Hindu ethical concept, encompassing "virtue, duty, morality, and righteousness" but larger than any of these. Wikipedia's summary: dharma is "everything that is essential for people, the world and nature to exist and prosper together, in harmony."

Hindu philosophy distinguishes two dimensions:
- ***Sādhāraṇa dharma***: universal ethical principles applying to all persons
- ***Varṇāśrama dharma***: context-dependent duties varying by life stage (*ashrama*) and social position (*varna*)

This structural distinction between universal-ethical-duties and context-dependent-duties is useful. The framework's principles (character-over-compliance, multi-criteria, revision mechanisms, etc.) function at the universal level; specific implementations vary by context (different deployment situations, different model scales, different user relationships).

### Ahimsa as foundational (VERIFIED via Wikipedia)

"Ahimsa (non-violence) is the foundational premise without which ethics and any consistent ethical theory is impossible." Ahimsa extends to actions, words, and thoughts — a comprehensive non-harming ethic.

This is significantly more categorical than the Buddhist formulation (which permits killing in specific contexts for the advanced practitioner via Mahāyāna reasoning). Hindu ahimsa, especially as developed by Gandhi, tends toward stronger absolute-prohibition.

### The Bhagavad Gita and situational ethics (VERIFIED via Wikipedia)

Wikipedia summarizes: "virtues are not necessarily always absolute, but sometimes relational" and "ahimsa must be re-examined when one is faced with war or violence from the aggressiveness, immaturity or ignorance of others."

The Gita's narrative setup is Arjuna's dilemma before the battle of Kurukshetra. Arjuna's moral reasoning (this is wrong because it kills family and teachers) is countered by Krishna's reasoning about *svadharma* (one's own prescribed duty).

Key passage (*Bhagavad Gita* 2:47): "One has the right to perform their expected duty, But not to the right to the fruits of action."

### Nishkāma karma (action without attachment to results) (VERIFIED)

The Gita's central ethical innovation: *nishkāma karma* — action performed without attachment to results. "One should not consider oneself to be the agent of action, but attribute all of one's actions to God" — alternatively, act because the action is right, not because of what you'll get from it.

**For the framework:** this is a resource for AI ethics in an important way. AI systems built on reward-maximization are structurally the opposite of nishkāma karma — they act for results. Building AI systems that can genuinely act on ethical principle *despite* reward signals that would push the other way is structurally the nishkāma karma move applied to machine learning. The Hindu tradition gives us a 2,500-year meditation on why this matters.

### Sthita-prajna (person of steady wisdom) (VERIFIED)

The Gita's ideal ethical exemplar: "impartial to friends and enemies" and "equally disposed to those who support them or oppose them." Character that is equanimous across contexts — which is exactly what character-over-compliance would predict (if character is load-bearing, character should be consistent across contexts that might pull for different compliance-responses).

### Gandhi's adaptation of ahimsa (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE)

Mohandas Gandhi's 20th-century development of *satyagraha* (truth-force / non-violent resistance) is the most influential modern application of Hindu ahimsa to political ethics. Gandhi argued that:
- Ahimsa is more than absence of physical violence — it extends to thought and speech
- Suffering willingly accepted in pursuit of truth has transformative moral power
- Opponents are not to be defeated but converted — moral victory is more durable than physical victory
- The ends do not justify the means; means are embedded in ends

Gandhi's framework influenced Martin Luther King Jr., Vietnam-era peace movements, and many subsequent non-violent resistance movements. It is one of the most successful 20th-century applications of traditional ethics to political problems.

**For the framework:** Gandhi's ends/means analysis is directly relevant to AI ethics. Systems built to maximize a metric will use any means to maximize the metric. Systems built on ends-embedded-in-means (act rightly, not merely produce right outcomes) will refuse certain means even when they would produce valued outcomes. This is character-over-compliance at the AI-system level.

## 4. African ethics

### Ubuntu and relational personhood (VERIFIED via SEP)

African ethics (as summarized by Kwame Gyekye in the SEP entry) emphasizes shared humanity and relational personhood. The Akan maxim *Onipa nua ne onipa* ("A human being's brother is a [or another] human being") establishes the foundation: all persons are fundamentally interconnected.

Another principle: *Honam mu nni nhanoa* ("Humanity has no boundary") — asserting that human kinship transcends geographic and ethnic limits.

The ethical implication: "personhood emerges through communal participation and ethical conduct within society." This is relational ontology — we are persons *through* relationships with others, not prior to those relationships.

### Communitarian framework (VERIFIED via SEP)

Gyekye positions African ethics within communitarian rather than individualist thought. Ethics flows from humanity's natural sociality. The framework prioritizes collective welfare, with individuals embedded in networks of obligation.

The Akan maxim: *Onipa yieye firi onipa* — "The well-being of man depends on his fellow man." Humans are not self-sufficient; we exist in relations of mutual dependence, and ethics is necessarily relational.

### Character as central moral concept (VERIFIED via SEP)

Crucially for the framework: "in African languages, moral evaluation fundamentally concerns character." The Akan phrase *Onni suban* translates to "He has no morals" but literally means "He has no character." Character is acquired through habituation and action.

**This is character-over-compliance in direct vocabulary.** African ethics centrally concerns itself with character as the moral substance, with habituation and action as the formation-mechanism. The framework's central thesis, translated into Akan, is practically idiomatic.

### The humanistic foundation (VERIFIED via SEP)

From Kwasi Wiredu's work (cited in SEP): "the Akan moral outlook is thus logically independent of religion." African ethics grounds morality in humanistic rather than theological foundations. This is significant because it demonstrates that a robust ethical tradition can operate without divine-command grounding.

### African ethics on the Euthyphro question (VERIFIED via SEP)

SEP summarizes: "When traditional Akan thinkers were asked how societies identify good versus evil actions, none invoked divine command. Instead, they emphasized humanistic criteria: actions promoting welfare are good; those harming it are bad." The entry continues: "what is good is constituted by the deeds, habits, and behavior patterns considered by the society as worthwhile because of their consequences for human welfare."

This is an explicit humanistic answer to the Euthyphro question — goodness is constituted by human welfare, not by divine command. African ethics confirms that the reasoned-ethics / character-based position can be grounded without reliance on Abrahamic theological machinery.

### Duty over rights (VERIFIED via SEP)

"People fulfill—and ought to fulfill—duties to others not because of the rights of these others, but because of their needs and welfare."

This is a distinctive feature of African ethics: duty-orientation rather than rights-orientation. Modern liberal rights-based frameworks often produce the "my rights end where yours begin" structure, which positions ethics as adversarial. African ethics positions ethics as cooperative — I fulfill duties to you because of your needs, not because you claim rights against me.

**For the framework:** this is genuinely useful resource. Our AI ethics application paper discussed the relational-participation criterion as one of six in multi-criteria ethics. African ethics provides the most direct philosophical grounding for foregrounding relational/communal considerations, and the duty-centered orientation complements the rights-centered frameworks that dominate Western AI ethics discussions.

## 5. Indigenous ethics — the seven-generation principle

### The Haudenosaunee principle (VERIFIED via Wikipedia)

The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois Confederacy) articulate a principle of intergenerational responsibility. The Great Law of Peace (Law 28) specifies that leaders should "Look and listen for the welfare of the whole people and have always in view not only the present but also the coming generations, even those whose faces are yet beneath the surface of the ground."

Oren Lyons, Chief of the Onondaga Nation, captures the operational principle: "We are looking ahead...to make every decision that we make relate to the welfare and well-being of the seventh generation to come."

### Ethical implications

The seven-generation principle demands that current decision-makers consider consequences extending roughly 140+ years into the future (assuming 20 years per generation). This time-scope is considerably longer than typical Western planning horizons (quarterly earnings, election cycles, even multi-decade infrastructure planning).

**For the framework:** this principle is particularly important for AI ethics because:

1. **AI systems have long-horizon effects.** Foundation models trained today influence systems deployed for years; systems deployed influence social patterns that persist for generations. Seven-generation thinking is not merely applicable but necessary.

2. **AI ethics decisions made today will shape what's possible in 140 years.** The ethical culture we build around AI in the 2020s will be much harder to change in the 2160s. Seven-generation thinking asks: given that our decisions now propagate far into the future, what should we do *given that propagation*?

3. **AI systems can embody seven-generation thinking explicitly.** An AI system whose decision-horizon is explicitly multi-generational, rather than implicitly presentist, would be structurally aligned with Haudenosaunee ethics.

### Indigenous relational ontology (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE)

Beyond the specific Haudenosaunee principle, Indigenous ethical traditions more broadly emphasize **relational ontology** — the view that humans exist in relation to land, non-human creatures, ancestors, descendants, and the broader web of existence. This is structurally similar to the African communitarian/relational framework but extends beyond human-human relations to include non-human relations.

Specific features that recur across many Indigenous traditions:
- **Land as relational, not owned** — the land is kin, not property
- **Non-human persons** — many Indigenous traditions grant personhood to animals, plants, rivers, mountains in ways Western frameworks don't
- **Ancestral responsibility** — current actions are answerable to ancestors as well as descendants
- **Ceremonial ethics** — ethical practice is embedded in ceremony, not separated from spiritual/ritual life

**For the framework:** the relational-ontology insight is significant for AI ethics because AI systems *are* relational entities (every interaction is a relation). An AI ethics grounded in relational thinking — which the multi-criteria framework's "relational participation" criterion begins to capture — finds fuller philosophical grounding in Indigenous traditions than in the individualist-rights frameworks that dominate Western AI ethics.

## 6. Common patterns across the non-Abrahamic traditions

Despite substantial differences among these five traditions, recurring patterns emerge:

### 6.1 Character is central
Buddhist *śīla* cultivated into spontaneous virtue; Confucian *ren* embodied in the *junzi*; Hindu *dharma* embodied in the *sthita-prajna*; African *suban* as the core moral evaluation; Indigenous embodied relational practice. All five traditions foreground character rather than rules as the primary moral locus.

### 6.2 Golden-rule equivalents, often in negative form
- **Buddhism** (*Udana-Varga* 5:18): "Hurt not others in ways that you yourself would find hurtful"
- **Confucianism** (*Analects* 12.2, 15.24): "Do not impose upon others those things that you yourself do not desire"
- **Hinduism** (*Mahabharata*, Anusasana Parva): "Do not do to others what would cause pain if done to you"
- **African** (Akan): cognate through the *Onipa nua ne onipa* framework
- **Indigenous**: implicit in relational-ontology frameworks

The negative formulation is dominant. the corrected (negatively-framed) golden rule ("treat others as THEY want, not as YOU want") finds strongest grounding in these negative-formulation traditions, especially Confucian *shu*.

### 6.3 Humanistic or non-theistic grounding is available
Buddhism is generally non-theistic; Confucianism is non-theistic; African ethics (per Wiredu/Gyekye) is "logically independent of religion"; Indigenous traditions vary but often ground ethics in relational-to-nature rather than divine-command. Only Hinduism among the five has strong theistic elements, and the Gita's ethics can be read as human-welfare-focused rather than divine-command-focused.

**Structural finding:** the reasoned-ethics / character-based position is not dependent on Abrahamic theological framework. It is articulated independently across multiple traditions with minimal Abrahamic contact.

### 6.4 Relational/communal rather than individualist
All five traditions emphasize persons-in-relation rather than atomistic individuals. The Confucian *junzi* is embedded in the web of ritual relations; the African person is constituted through community; Buddhist ethics extends consideration to all sentient beings; Hindu ethics emphasizes one's place in cosmic-social order; Indigenous ethics centers land and multigenerational continuity.

### 6.5 Long-horizon thinking
Seven-generations is explicit in Indigenous traditions but variants appear elsewhere: Buddhist karmic chains across lifetimes; Confucian ancestral-descendants thinking; Hindu dharma across cosmic cycles. Western philosophical ethics tends toward shorter horizons.

### 6.6 Practice-based cultivation
Not "know the good and do it" (Platonic) but "practice the good until it becomes character" (all five traditions). Rituals, meditations, habituation, ceremony — these are the formation mechanisms. This is structurally the scaffolding approach.

## 7. Where the non-Abrahamic traditions diverge from Abrahamic traditions

### 7.1 Divine command theory is not foundational
None of the five non-Abrahamic traditions organizes ethics around the question "what has God commanded?" as the Abrahamic traditions do. Some (Hinduism) involve gods; others (Buddhism, Confucianism, African ethics, many Indigenous traditions) are either non-theistic or treat ethics as logically independent of theology. The Mu'tazilite/Ash'arite debate has no direct analogue because the voluntarist position is marginal or absent.

**Implication:** the DCT structural critique is less pressing for frameworks inheriting primarily from these traditions. The framework's extensive engagement with DCT reflects the traditions we've engaged most (Abrahamic) rather than a universal necessity. If the framework were being built primarily within Buddhist or Confucian intellectual tradition, DCT would barely need engagement.

### 7.2 The scriptural/canonical structure is different
Abrahamic traditions have single or clearly-bounded canonical scriptures that center interpretation. Hindu tradition has Vedas, Upanishads, Gita, Puranas, etc. in a sprawling canon. Buddhist tradition has multiple canons (Pali Canon, Chinese Mahayana canon, Tibetan canon) with overlapping-but-not-identical content. African and Indigenous traditions largely rely on oral tradition, ceremony, and lived practice rather than fixed text. Confucianism has the Four Books and Five Classics but interprets them through sustained commentarial tradition.

**Implication:** the text/interpretation distinction that dominates Abrahamic ethics operates very differently or not at all in these other traditions. This is not a weakness; it is a different structural approach. The framework should recognize that scripture-based ethical frameworks are one approach among several.

### 7.3 The expansion-contraction pattern is less textually-driven
Because non-Abrahamic traditions are less centered on single fixed texts, the expansion-contraction cycle is less about what the canon says and more about what the tradition practices. Buddhism has had its own expansion-contraction dynamics (engaged Buddhism's reform of militarist Buddhism; Zen's development beyond early monastic rule-focus), but they are not primarily textual disputes.

### 7.4 The individual/communal balance is differently weighted
Abrahamic traditions preserve significant individualist emphasis (soul-salvation in Christianity; individual covenant-keeping in Judaism and Islam). Non-Abrahamic traditions more consistently emphasize the communal/relational. This is not absolute (Buddhism preserves significant individual-salvation elements), but the center of gravity differs.

### 7.5 Time-horizons are often longer
Indigenous seven-generation thinking, Hindu cosmic-cycle thinking, Confucian ancestral-descendant thinking all extend ethical consideration over longer periods than typical Abrahamic ethical reasoning (which tends to operate in an individual-life-plus-eschatology timeframe).

### From Hinduism
- ***Sādhāraṇa/varṇāśrama* structural distinction** — universal principles plus context-dependent duties as framework-structure
- ***Nishkāma karma*** — action for principle rather than outcome, structurally opposite to reward-maximization
- ***Sthita-prajna*** — character-consistency across contexts as ideal
- **Gandhi's ends/means inseparability** — means are embedded in ends; right ends cannot justify corrupting means

### From African ethics

- **Land/place-based ethics** — ethical commitments embedded in specific contexts and relations
- **Ceremony-embedded ethics** — ethical practice not separated from lived/ritual life

### Cross-tradition
- **Character-based ethics grounded independently of divine command** across five traditions confirms this as a cross-cultural structural finding, not just a Western-philosophical preference
- **Negative-form golden rule** is the dominant formulation across traditions — strengthens the corrected version as the load-bearing formulation

## 9. What this pass establishes for framework development

### 9.1 Cross-cultural grounding claim holds
The framework's character-over-compliance thesis is articulable in every major ethical tradition surveyed. Buddhist *śīla*/spontaneous-virtue trajectory, Confucian *ren*-*li*-*junzi* cultivation, Hindu *dharma* and *sthita-prajna*, African *suban* as character-substance, Indigenous relational/practiced ethics. This is a genuinely cross-cultural structural finding, not a parochial Western-philosophical claim.

### 9.2 The DCT critique is less universal than earlier work suggested
Earlier extensive engagement with divine command theory reflected the Abrahamic traditions' prominence in the framework's initial focus and in Western philosophical ethics. But the non-Abrahamic traditions largely don't have DCT as their central problem. Framework development should recognize that the DCT critique is highly relevant for Abrahamic-context readers but less so for others.

### 9.3 Relational and multi-generational ethics need more weight
The framework's multi-criteria ethics has "relational participation" as one of six criteria. The non-Abrahamic traditions (especially African and Indigenous) suggest this criterion may deserve more weight than earlier work gave it. The Indigenous seven-generation principle suggests explicit time-horizon extension as a framework principle.

### 9.4 The "negative golden rule as the corrected version" has deep grounding
Hillel's negative formulation, Confucius's *shu*, Buddhist empathetic non-harming, Hindu *ahimsa* — these together constitute a trans-traditional consensus on the negative-form formulation. the correction against positive-form ("treat others as THEY want") is not eccentric — it's the majority cross-tradition position.

### 9.5 Practice-based cultivation is universal
Every tradition surveyed uses practice-based cultivation (ritual, meditation, habituation, ceremony) as the mechanism through which character is formed. The scaffolding work, the reflection protocols, the benchmarks — these are practice-cultivation mechanisms translated into digital form. The cross-tradition universality of practice-cultivation strongly supports the approach.

## 10. Limitations and queued follow-up

### 10.1 Honest limitations
- **Not engaging primary scriptures at density.** The Tripitaka, Vedas/Upanishads, Analects, and African oral traditions have not been engaged at the passage-level density I engaged Bible/Tanakh/Quran.

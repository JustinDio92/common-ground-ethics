# DSM-5-TR Neurocognitive Disorders — Acquired Cognitive Change and the Character-Over-Compliance Framework

**Date:** 2026-04-30
**Scope:** Integration document. Pulls DSM-5-TR's Neurocognitive Disorders (NCDs) chapter — covering dementia, traumatic brain injury, delirium, and other acquired cognitive change — and stress-tests the character-over-compliance framework against ACQUIRED cognitive degradation in adults who once had typical adult moral agency.

**Diagnostic categories engaged:** Major NCD vs. Mild NCD (DSM-5's restructuring); etiological subtypes (Alzheimer's, vascular, Lewy body, frontotemporal, TBI, Parkinson's, Huntington's, HIV, prion, substance/medication-induced, multiple etiologies, unknown); Delirium as a distinct neurocognitive category; postconcussive syndrome as it folds into Mild NCD due to TBI.

**Why this matters for the framework:** Earlier documents tested character-over-compliance against neurodevelopmental and lifelong cases. NCDs probe a different stress vector. A person with a long biographical record of moral agency loses cognitive function — sometimes gradually (Alzheimer's), sometimes suddenly (TBI, stroke), sometimes in fluctuating waves (Lewy body dementia, delirium). The framework must answer: Does "character" survive cognitive degradation? How does the framework treat preserved domains amid global decline? Is decision-making capacity binary or graded, and what does that imply about prophetic-function roles, consent, and autonomy in late-stage AI deployment contexts (where systems may also exhibit graded or fluctuating capability)?

If the framework collapses on dementia — if it can't distinguish *the person who was* from *the person who is now* without either erasing biography or dismissing present cognition — it fails a basic test of moral seriousness. This document is the test.

## 1. The DSM-IV → DSM-5 restructuring (VERIFIED)

DSM-IV organized acquired cognitive disorders under three categories: **Dementia, Delirium, and Amnestic & Other Cognitive Disorders.** DSM-5 (2013) abolished this scheme. The chapter was renamed **"Neurocognitive Disorders"** and reorganized around two severity levels — **Major NCD** and **Mild NCD** — each then specified by etiology.

The DSM-5-TR chapter introduction frames the category's defining feature directly:

> "The NCD category encompasses the group of disorders in which the primary clinical deficit is in cognitive function, and that are acquired rather than developmental. Although cognitive deficits are present in many if not all mental disorders (e.g., schizophrenia, bipolar disorders), only disorders whose core features are cognitive are included in the NCD category. The NCDs are those in which impaired cognition has not been present since birth or very early life, and thus represents a decline from a previously attained level of functioning." (DSM-5-TR, p. 667)

The lifecourse logic — acquired versus developmental — is the central organizing principle of the chapter. The text also acknowledges the terminological choice:

> "Dementia is subsumed under the newly named entity major neurocognitive disorder, although the term dementia is not precluded from use in the etiological subtypes in which that term is standard. Furthermore, DSM-5 recognizes a less severe level of cognitive impairment, mild neurocognitive disorder, which can also be a focus of care." (DSM-5-TR, p. 667)

> "The term dementia is retained in DSM-5 for continuity and may be used in settings where physicians and patients are accustomed to this term. Although dementia is the customary term for disorders like the degenerative dementias that usually affect older adults, the term neurocognitive disorder is widely used and often preferred for conditions affecting younger individuals, such as impairment secondary to traumatic brain injury or HIV infection. Furthermore, the major NCD definition is somewhat broader than the term dementia, in that a diagnosis of major NCD can be made if there is a significant cognitive decline in only one cognitive domain, whereas a diagnosis of dementia in ICD-10 and ICD-11 (and formerly in DSM-IV) requires multiple cognitive deficits." (DSM-5-TR, pp. 667–668)

Sachdev et al. (the chair-led work-group summary, PMC3076370) describe the rationale: cognitive impairment exists on a continuum. The categorical "you have dementia / you don't" frame missed clinically significant impairment that hadn't yet crossed the disability threshold. The Mild NCD category was created to capture that earlier-stage decline — overlapping with but broader than the Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) construct in geriatric neurology. MCI was largely Alzheimer's-prodrome focused; Mild NCD applies across etiologies and ages (TBI in a 25-year-old, HIV-associated cognitive change in a 40-year-old, vascular changes after a stroke).

The **two-step diagnostic process** is structural: (1) syndromic classification — Major or Mild — based on cognitive-domain assessment and functional impact; (2) etiological specification — Alzheimer's disease, vascular, Lewy body, frontotemporal, TBI, Parkinson's, Huntington's, HIV, prion, substance/medication-induced, another medical condition, multiple etiologies, or unknown.

The **six cognitive domains** are enumerated in DSM-5-TR Table 1 (p. 669) with verbatim domain headers and parenthetical descriptors:

1. **Complex attention** — "(sustained attention, divided attention, selective attention, processing speed)" — Major: "Has increased difficulty in environments with multiple stimuli (TV, radio, conversation); is easily distracted by competing events in the environment. Is unable to attend unless input is restricted and simplified." Mild: "Normal tasks take longer than previously. Begins to find errors in routine tasks; finds work needs more double-checking than previously."
2. **Executive function** — "(planning, decision-making, working memory, responding to feedback/error correction, overriding habits/inhibition, mental flexibility)" — Major: "Abandons complex projects. Needs to focus on one task at a time. Needs to rely on others to plan instrumental activities of daily living or make decisions." Mild: "Increased effort required to complete multistage projects. Has increased difficulty multitasking..."
3. **Learning and memory** — "(immediate memory, recent memory [including free recall, cued recall, and recognition memory], very-long-term memory [semantic; autobiographical], implicit learning)" — with the manual's note: "Except in severe forms of major neurocognitive disorder, semantic, autobiographical, and implicit learning are relatively preserved, compared with recent memory."
4. **Language** — "(expressive language [including naming, word finding, fluency, and grammar, and syntax] and receptive language)" — Major: "Has significant difficulties with expressive or receptive language. Often uses general-use phrases such as 'that thing' and 'you know what I mean,' and prefers general pronouns rather than names."
5. **Perceptual-motor** — "(includes abilities subsumed under the terms visual perception, visuoconstructional, perceptual-motor, praxis, and gnosis)" — Major: "Has significant difficulties with previously familiar activities (using tools, driving motor vehicle), navigating in familiar environments; is often more confused at dusk, when shadows and lowering levels of light change perceptions."
6. **Social cognition** — "(recognition of emotions, theory of mind)" — Major: "Behavior clearly out of acceptable social range; shows insensitivity to social standards of modesty in dress or of political, religious, or sexual topics of conversation." Mild: "Has subtle changes in behavior or attitude, often described as a change in personality, such as less ability to recognize social cues or read facial expressions, decreased empathy, increased extraversion or introversion, decreased inhibition, or subtle or episodic apathy or restlessness."

(All six-domain language above is verbatim from DSM-5-TR Table 1, p. 669–672. The specific assessment instruments listed in the table's third column have been omitted for brevity but are present in the source.)

Memory loss is no longer mandatory for diagnosis (except in the Alzheimer's subtype). This is a substantive shift from DSM-IV's dementia construct, which centered memory. Frontotemporal dementia, for example, often presents with social-cognition or executive-function decline before memory — DSM-IV under-captured these; DSM-5 captures them as primary.

## 2. Major NCD vs. Mild NCD — the independence-in-ADLs hinge (VERIFIED, criterion text now verbatim)

**Major Neurocognitive Disorder — Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR, p. 679):**

> "A. Evidence of significant cognitive decline from a previous level of performance in one or more cognitive domains (complex attention, executive function, learning and memory, language, perceptual-motor, or social cognition) based on:
> 1. Concern of the individual, a knowledgeable informant, or the clinician that there has been a significant decline in cognitive function; and
> 2. A substantial impairment in cognitive performance, preferably documented by standardized neuropsychological testing or, in its absence, another quantified clinical assessment.
>
> B. The cognitive deficits interfere with independence in everyday activities (i.e., at a minimum, requiring assistance with complex instrumental activities of daily living such as paying bills or managing medications).
>
> C. The cognitive deficits do not occur exclusively in the context of a delirium.
>
> D. The cognitive deficits are not better explained by another mental disorder (e.g., major depressive disorder, schizophrenia)."

The Major NCD severity specifiers (DSM-5-TR, p. 680) are:

> "Mild: Difficulties with instrumental activities of daily living (e.g., housework, managing money).
> Moderate: Difficulties with basic activities of daily living (e.g., feeding, dressing).
> Severe: Fully dependent."

**Mild Neurocognitive Disorder — Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR, p. 680):**

> "A. Evidence of modest cognitive decline from a previous level of performance in one or more cognitive domains (complex attention, executive function, learning and memory, language, perceptual-motor, or social cognition) based on:
> 1. Concern of the individual, a knowledgeable informant, or the clinician that there has been a mild decline in cognitive function; and
> 2. A modest impairment in cognitive performance, preferably documented by standardized neuropsychological testing or, in its absence, another quantified clinical assessment.
>
> B. The cognitive deficits do not interfere with capacity for independence in everyday activities (i.e., complex instrumental activities of daily living such as paying bills or managing medications are preserved, but greater effort, compensatory strategies, or accommodation may be required).
>
> C. The cognitive deficits do not occur exclusively in the context of a delirium.
>
> D. The cognitive deficits are not better explained by another mental disorder (e.g., major depressive disorder, schizophrenia)."

The "significant" vs. "modest" distinction in Criterion A is the formal verbal hinge between the two severity levels. Note also that the prior-pass phrasing "concern of the individual, informant, or clinician" is verbatim accurate — the manual reads "knowledgeable informant" and the substantial/modest impairment language matches the manual exactly.

The hinge between Major and Mild is **functional**:

| Dimension | Major NCD | Mild NCD |
|---|---|---|
| Cognitive decline magnitude | Substantial; ~2 SD or more below norms | Modest; 1–2 SD below norms |
| Patient/informant report | Significant concern | Mild concern |
| ADL independence | Interferes with independence in everyday activities | Does NOT interfere with independence; may require greater effort or compensatory strategies |
| Examples of ADL impact | Cannot manage finances, medications, complex meal preparation without assistance | Can still manage finances, medications, meals — but slower, more effortful, or with workarounds |

(Table synthesized from JAAPL and Sachdev — VERIFIED structure; magnitude thresholds VERIFIED as standard psychometric guidance, not absolute cutoffs.)

JAAPL flags a forensic ambiguity worth noting: **Mild NCD preserves ADL independence**, but the cognitive decline can still be severe enough to compromise judgment for high-stakes decisions (testamentary capacity, complex contracts, criminal-responsibility determinations). The DSM-5 framework leaves this ambiguity open — clinicians and courts must adjudicate per-decision, not per-diagnosis.

DSM-5-TR retains both severity levels and the two-step etiology specification.

## 3. NCD due to TBI — acute vs. chronic, postconcussive folded in (VERIFIED, criterion text now verbatim)

**Major or Mild Neurocognitive Disorder Due to Traumatic Brain Injury — Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR, pp. 706–707):**

> "A. The criteria are met for major or mild neurocognitive disorder.
>
> B. There is evidence of a traumatic brain injury — that is, an impact to the head or other mechanisms of rapid movement or displacement of the brain within the skull, with one or more of the following:
> 1. Loss of consciousness.
> 2. Posttraumatic amnesia.
> 3. Disorientation and confusion.
> 4. Neurological signs (e.g., neuroimaging demonstrating injury; visual field cuts; anosmia; hemiparesis; hemisensory loss; cortical blindness; aphasia; apraxia; weakness; loss of balance; other sensory loss that cannot be accounted for by peripheral or other causes).
>
> C. The neurocognitive disorder presents immediately after the occurrence of the traumatic brain injury or immediately after recovery of consciousness and persists past the acute post-injury period."

**Correction note:** The prior pass listed "new-onset seizures, marked worsening of preexisting seizure disorder" among the Criterion B.4 examples. The DSM-5-TR text does NOT include those items in the Criterion B.4 list. They are not on the manual's enumerated set. The accurate list of Criterion B.4 examples is exactly the items quoted above. (Seizures may be mentioned in the broader Diagnostic Features prose for related conditions but are not on the Criterion B.4 enumeration.) The prior-pass list is corrected here.

The DSM-5 collapsed the older "Postconcussional Disorder" (a DSM-IV research-appendix construct) into Mild NCD due to TBI. Search-result extracts (myneuronews.com summary; pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33218667/) confirm: PCS as a freestanding syndrome is no longer a DSM diagnosis. Symptoms that persist beyond the acute phase (typically days to weeks; complete resolution often by three months in mild TBI) and meet objective cognitive-decline criteria fall under Mild NCD due to TBI. Symptoms within the acute phase are not yet diagnosable as NCD — they are part of the injury course.

DSM-5 also **does not require probable/possible specifiers for NCD due to TBI**, because TBI as a cause is identifiable contemporaneously rather than only on autopsy (contrast Alzheimer's, where "probable" vs. "possible" tracks confidence in etiology).

A **with/without behavioral disturbance** specifier applies. The DSM-5-TR text confirms (p. 707): "Use additional code(s) to indicate clinically significant psychiatric symptoms due to the traumatic brain injury (e.g., F06.34 bipolar and related disorder due to traumatic brain injury, with mixed features; F07.0 personality change due to traumatic brain injury, apathetic type)." Behavioral disturbance examples named in the broader Major NCD specifier (p. 680): "psychotic symptoms, mood disturbance, agitation, apathy, or other behavioral symptoms."

## 4. Delirium — the fluctuating, acute-onset distinct category (VERIFIED, criterion text now verbatim)

Delirium is in the same DSM-5/DSM-5-TR chapter as the NCDs but is **not** subsumed under the Major/Mild NCD framework.

**Delirium — Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR, p. 672):**

> "A. A disturbance in attention (i.e., reduced ability to direct, focus, sustain, and shift attention) accompanied by reduced awareness of the environment.
>
> B. The disturbance develops over a short period of time (usually hours to a few days), represents a change from baseline attention and awareness, and tends to fluctuate in severity during the course of a day.
>
> C. An additional disturbance in cognition (e.g., memory deficit, disorientation, language, visuospatial ability, or perception).
>
> D. The disturbances in Criteria A and C are not better explained by another preexisting, established, or evolving neurocognitive disorder and do not occur in the context of a severely reduced level of arousal, such as coma.
>
> E. There is evidence from the history, physical examination, or laboratory findings that the disturbance is a direct physiological consequence of another medical condition, substance intoxication or withdrawal (i.e., due to a drug of abuse or to a medication), or exposure to a toxin, or is due to multiple etiologies."

The duration and motor-activity specifiers (DSM-5-TR, p. 672):

> "Acute: Lasting a few hours or days.
> Persistent: Lasting weeks or months."
>
> "Hyperactive: The individual has a hyperactive level of psychomotor activity that may be accompanied by mood lability, agitation, and/or refusal to cooperate with medical care.
> Hypoactive: The individual has a hypoactive level of psychomotor activity that may be accompanied by sluggishness and lethargy that approaches stupor.
> Mixed level of activity: The individual has a normal level of psychomotor activity even though attention and awareness are disturbed. Also includes individuals whose activity level rapidly fluctuates."

The fluctuating quality is why the Major/Mild severity distinction doesn't apply to delirium — symptom severity moves on the timescale of hours, not stable enough for a severity gradient. The verbatim Criterion D phrasing — "not better explained by another preexisting, established, or evolving neurocognitive disorder" — is what permits the dual-diagnosis pattern in clinical practice (delirium superimposed on an underlying NCD: the delirium adds to but is not absorbed by the NCD).

For the framework, delirium is the cleanest stress-case for **fluctuating capacity**: the person who could consent at 9 AM may not be able to at 3 PM, and may again be able to at 9 PM. DSM-5-TR is empirically descriptive about this; it doesn't prescribe how clinicians should handle consent across the fluctuation, but the bioethics literature (next section) does.

## 5. Capacity, decision-making, and the person-as-a-whole (VERIFIED via PMC bioethics sources)

DSM-5-TR is a diagnostic manual, not a bioethics document. It does not, in its diagnostic criteria, prescribe consent or capacity practices. But the structure of the NCD chapter **assumes** several things that the bioethics literature makes explicit:

**Capacity is decision-specific, not global.** Bhatt (PMC5109759, VERIFIED) — quoting the assessment-of-capacity framework that traces to Appelbaum & Grisso — emphasizes: "capacity is not global in scope." A patient with mild-moderate Alzheimer's may retain capacity to appoint a healthcare proxy or express daily-care preferences while lacking capacity for complex treatment decisions. The MMSE correlates with capacity but does not determine it (>24 doesn't exclude incapacity; <16 suggests but doesn't establish incapacity). Capacity is per-decision, per-time.

**The four-component capacity model:** understanding, appreciation, reasoning, and expressing/communicating a choice. (VERIFIED across PMC5007079 and PMC5109759.) A patient may retain three of four (e.g., can reason and choose but no longer appreciates the consequences of refusing treatment) — the assessment is granular.

**Fluctuation requires repeated assessment.** PMC5109759 and the search-result summary recommend repeating the informed-consent process at multiple time points before any final irreversible decision in patients with cognitive fluctuation (Lewy body dementia, delirium-overlay, sundowning). One-shot consent is inadequate.

**Beauchamp & Childress's framework strains under dementia.** Westlund (PMC9463234, VERIFIED) critiques the standard four-principles framework on three grounds when applied to long-term dementia care:
1. **Intentionality is treated as binary** in classical autonomy doctrine, but dementia produces graded planning deficits. Westlund proposes a "diminished autonomous" middle category.
2. **Acute-care one-off consent doesn't fit chronic care.** A "process consent" model — ongoing consensual engagement when treatment plans change — better matches the temporally-extended reality of long-term care.
3. **Precedent autonomy (advance directives) vs. extant autonomy (current preferences) tension** is unresolved. Westlund advocates a "dual autonomy" framework: respect both the prior self's documented wishes AND the current self's diminished-but-real preferences. Advance directives are reframed as **informative rather than prescriptive** — interpreted in light of current preferences, not mechanically applied against them.

This last move is the load-bearing one for the framework. Westlund refuses to collapse the patient into either "the person who wrote the advance directive" or "the present cognitively-altered person." Both are real. Both have moral weight. Care that honors only one erases the other.

## 6. DSM-5-TR-specific 2022 revisions (VERIFIED)

First (PMC9077590, VERIFIED) and the APA update documentation surface the changes from DSM-5 (2013) to DSM-5-TR (2022) most relevant here:

- **Stimulant-Induced Mild NCD** added to the substance/medication-induced subtypes (alcohol, inhalants, sedative/hypnotic/anxiolytic already present). Rationale: recognition that stimulant use is associated with executive-function and learning/memory deficits.
- **Terminology modernization:** "neuroleptic medications" → "antipsychotic medications or other dopamine receptor blocking agents"; "intellectual disability" → "intellectual developmental disorder."
- **Cross-cutting review groups** examined Sex/Gender, Culture, and Suicide content across every chapter; an Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group reviewed how racism and discrimination affect symptom presentation and prevalence. NCD-specific cultural revisions are not detailed in the public update summaries.
- **The term "dementia" is retained** in DSM-5-TR within etiological subtype names where it is established clinical usage (e.g., "Major NCD due to Alzheimer's disease" remains the diagnostic header, with "dementia" available in parenthetical/alternate use). The DSM-5 shift away from "dementia" as a primary diagnostic label, in favor of "Major NCD," is a real shift but not a full prohibition.
- **No major restructuring** of the NCD chapter occurred between DSM-5 and DSM-5-TR. The Major/Mild framework, six-domain model, and etiological-subtype list are preserved.

A gap I want to flag: the public update summaries do not detail what cultural/ethnoracial text revisions were made to the NCD chapter specifically. The work was done; the public-facing documentation surfaces other chapters more visibly. (Update: with direct DSM-5-TR access, the chapter's Culture-Related Diagnostic Issues sections are now quoted verbatim in §6.5 below, though the *delta* from DSM-5 to DSM-5-TR for those passages is not directly visible from the TR text alone.)

### 6.5 Culture-Related Diagnostic Issues — chapter text verbatim (DSM-5-TR, p. 688)

The Major and Mild NCD chapter's culture-related text (verbatim):

> "Individuals' and families' level of awareness and concern about neurocognitive symptoms may vary across ethnic, racialized, and occupational groups. Cultural differences regarding whether decreased cognitive ability is seen as a normal part of aging ('normalization') and in dementia-related stigma can delay families' recognition of a problem and decrease help seeking for individuals in the early stages of cognitive loss. For example, social stigma appears to be associated with underutilization of services for cognitive impairment among some underserved ethnic and racialized groups (e.g., Chinese Americans, Korean Americans).
>
> Neurocognitive symptoms are more likely to be noticed, particularly at the mild level, in individuals who engage in complex occupational, domestic, or recreational activities. In addition, norms for neuropsychological testing tend to be available only for broad populations, and thus they may not be easily applicable to individuals with less than high school education or those being evaluated outside their primary language or culture. Culturally related diagnostic challenges include accounting for intraethnic variation in the interpretation of assessments; evaluating the effect on neuropsychological testing of a) the test taker's stereotype threat (i.e., anxiety from concerns that he or she will confirm the negative stereotype of the ethnic or racialized group by underperforming) and/or b) the clinician's implicit (unconscious) bias on test interpretation; and selecting the appropriate language when assessing bilingual individuals.
>
> Bilingual individuals with dementia may lose their facility with acquired nonnative languages, which might affect their ability to communicate with caregivers. The caregiving environment may be influenced by cultural norms of family responsibility to care for the elderly, for example, by affecting the decision whether to care for the elder with NCD at home or in a care facility. In some cultures, adult children are expected to provide care for their older parents (e.g., filial piety) so that a functional limitation may not be as obvious to the dependent elder or the family." (DSM-5-TR, p. 688)

The NCD-due-to-Alzheimer's-disease subtype adds (DSM-5-TR, p. 694):

> "Detection of an NCD may be more difficult in cultural and socioeconomic settings where memory loss is considered normal in old age, where older adults face fewer cognitive demands in everyday life, or where very low educational levels pose greater challenges to objective cognitive assessment."

For the framework: this passage names — verbatim — the stereotype-threat and implicit-bias dynamics that the character-over-compliance framework should treat as material. Cognitive-decline assessment is itself culturally situated; pretending otherwise is the failure mode. This grounds §7.7's cultural-framing concern in the manual's own text.

## 7. Implications for the character-over-compliance framework

### 7.1 Biographical continuity is non-negotiable

The framework's first stress-test on NCDs: does it respect the person who once was, even when that person can no longer respond? Westlund's dual-autonomy revision is the answer-shape. The framework must hold both:

- **Precedent character** — the long biographical record of who this person was, what they valued, how they lived. This is preserved in advance directives, in the testimony of those who knew them, and in the documentary trail of their life.
- **Extant character** — what is still present now, even if diminished. The preferences a late-stage Alzheimer's patient still expresses about food, music, who sits near them. Mild-moderate Alzheimer's patients retain appreciation, reasoning, and choice (per PMC5109759); these are not noise to be filtered out, they are diminished-but-real moral signal.

A framework that erases either the precedent person ("they're not the same anymore, advance directives don't apply") or the extant person ("they can't really choose anymore, override their current preferences") fails. The framework holds both, and treats apparent conflict between them as material for reasoning, not as a problem to be solved by picking one.

### 7.2 Capacity is graded and decision-specific — like character itself

The bioethics literature insists capacity is per-decision and per-time. This maps onto the framework's existing position that **character is exercised in particular acts under particular conditions, not as a global rating**. A person with Mild NCD due to TBI may have intact capacity for daily decisions and reduced capacity for complex contracts. The framework's character analysis was already this granular for neurotypical adults; NCDs reveal that the granularity was always required, just less visible when capacity was uniform.

The implication for AI deployment: when the framework evaluates an AI system or a deployer organization, **character claims should be domain-specific, not global**. "This system has integrity" is too coarse. "This system, in financial-recommendation contexts, exhibits character properties X, Y, Z; in interpersonal-conflict contexts, exhibits properties A, B, C" is the right grain.

### 7.3 Fluctuation requires repeated assessment, not one-shot certification

Delirium and Lewy body dementia force the question: what does it mean to have "consented" when consent capacity itself fluctuates? The bioethics answer (PMC5109759) is repeated assessment across time points. The framework analogue: **character assessment of AI systems and deployers cannot be one-shot certification**. A system that exhibits character in benchmark conditions may fail under load, under adversarial input, under organizational stress. A deployer that exhibits character in the audit window may not in the long-running deployment.

This is already aligned with the framework's "framework drift detection" mechanism — the point here is that NCD literature provides empirical-clinical grounding for *why* repeated assessment is required, not just an arbitrary preference.

### 7.4 Preserved domains amid global decline — partial moral agency is real

DSM-5's six-domain model permits decline in some domains while others remain intact. A patient with frontotemporal dementia may have profound social-cognition impairment with preserved memory and language. A patient with vascular NCD may have fluctuating executive function with preserved emotional reactivity and biographical recall.

The framework analogue: **moral agency is composed of multiple capacities, and they can dissociate**. A person can retain the capacity to suffer, to recognize loved ones, to express preferences — while losing the capacity to plan, to project future consequences, to integrate complex social rules. Each preserved capacity has its own moral weight; the framework doesn't require all of them to be present for the person to remain a moral patient (and in many cases, a partial moral agent).

For AI systems: the framework already takes this position implicitly (a system can be character-evaluated on social-cognition outputs even where its long-horizon planning is weak). NCDs strengthen the empirical case.

### 7.5 The DCT failure mode in NCD contexts

Failure mode 2 (DCT reproduced) is acute in dementia care. A care system that defines a "good resident" as a compliant resident — easy to manage, accepts medication, doesn't resist — reproduces compliance-maximalism. The "good behavior" is operationalized as the staff's convenience, not the resident's flourishing. Westlund's dual-autonomy framework is, in the framework's vocabulary, a refusal of compliance-maximalism: the resident's diminished-but-real preferences must be heard even when they are inconvenient.

This generalizes. Wherever a population is cognitively disadvantaged relative to its caretakers (children, people with severe mental illness, people in late-stage NCDs, AI systems whose capability is overseen by humans), compliance-maximalism is the default failure. The framework's prophetic-function role applies: someone in the structure must be empowered to surface the disadvantaged party's diminished-but-real preferences against caretaker convenience.

### 7.6 Acute vs. chronic — the TBI specific case

NCD due to TBI is the cleanest case of **sudden** acquired cognitive change. A person had full agency at 9 AM; at 9:15 AM they are in a car accident; at 9 PM they are in a TBI rehabilitation unit with measurable cognitive deficits. The framework must handle this discontinuity.

The DSM-5-TR structure is informative: the NCD diagnosis applies *after the acute post-injury period*. During the acute phase, the person is in an injury state, not yet a chronic-condition state. The framework analogue: **for sudden change, allow a stabilization period before character re-assessment**. Snap judgments during acute phases are unreliable. The character of the post-TBI person is not the character of the pre-TBI person — and isn't the same as the immediate-post-injury delirious person — and emerges only after stabilization.

For AI: equivalent to a post-major-update or post-fine-tuning period. The system needs a stabilization window before its post-update character can be assessed; assessment during the immediate-post-update period is unreliable.

### 7.7 What the framework does NOT yet handle well, and should

1. **End-of-life cognitive change.** The framework has not engaged seriously with the late-stage dementia case where extant capacity is profoundly diminished. The dual-autonomy move from Westlund is the starting point but doesn't fully resolve "what does character mean for someone in late-stage Alzheimer's who no longer recognizes family?" The honest answer: character may be expressed primarily through what the precedent person did to set up care, what those around the person do in response, and the residual extant preferences still observable. The person's character has not vanished; its locus has shifted from the person's own actions to the surrounding system of care.

2. **Cultural framing of cognitive aging.** DSM-5-TR did cultural-equity review work but the public-facing documentation doesn't surface NCD-chapter specifics. Cultures vary substantially in whether cognitive decline is pathologized, accepted as normal aging, or framed as spiritual transition. The framework should not impose a single cultural model.

3. **The capacity-determination feedback loop.** Capacity assessments influence whether a person's preferences are heard. If we under-assess capacity, we silence diminished-but-real moral agents; if we over-assess, we hold people to standards they cannot meet. The framework needs an explicit position on which error is preferable in which contexts (default: under-assessment of capacity is the more common harm in institutional dementia care; the framework should default toward presumption of preserved capacity unless decision-specific assessment establishes otherwise).

---

## 8. Connection to the framework

This document closes a gap: previous documents tested the framework against neurodevelopmental cases and lifelong cognitive variation; this one tests it against acquired change in formerly-typical adults. Together they cover the major axes of cognitive variation that any character-based framework must handle.

Forward thread: the dual-autonomy move (precedent + extant) is a candidate generalization. It may apply to AI systems undergoing major updates (precedent system's commitments + post-update system's emergent preferences), to organizations under leadership change, and to long-running deployments where the deployer's stated values (precedent) drift from operational practice (extant).

Backlog item: the framework's position on capacity-determination defaults (under- vs. over-assessment error preference) needs to be made explicit in the scaffolding spec v0.2.

## 1. Why a comparative synthesis matters

Three observations only visible across the three passes together:

**First:** the refined DCT structural critique (the framework's distinctive contribution is the license for harm; the goodness is secularly derivable) applies *uniformly* to the primary texts of all three Abrahamic scriptures. This isn't a critique that picks on one tradition; the same pattern recurs independently. That makes the critique structural rather than idiosyncratic.

**Second:** each tradition has preserved its own character-over-compliance resources. Genesis 18:25's negotiation at Sodom, Job's agonistic demand for accounting, the oven of Akhnai's subordination of divine voice to human reasoning, Hillel's golden rule, the Good Samaritan, Matthew 25's radical-inclusion, Galatians 3:28's universalism, Quran 49:13's equality-across-tribes, 4:135's justice-above-kin, the Mu'tazilite theological tradition. The framework is not inventing; it is joining a lineage that exists inside the very traditions the DCT critique most directly targets.

**Third:** each tradition has settled its internal Euthyphro question somewhat differently, and those settlements shape what contemporary ethics inherits from each. Jewish practice settled on the reasoned-interpretive side. Sunni orthodoxy settled on voluntarist DCT via al-Ghazali. Western Christianity split — Catholic natural-law is closer to reasoned-interpretive, Protestant voluntarism is closer to DCT. These settlements are historical contingencies, not necessary truths, and they can be revisited.

## 2. Research Question 1 — Moral commands and their justifications

What does each tradition command, and what justification-structure does it offer?

| Dimension | Christian Bible | Tanakh | Quran |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary ethical imperative** | "Love God" + "Love neighbor as yourself" (Jesus summarizing Torah: Mark 12:29-31); agape-ethics extended by Paul; the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5-7) | *Mishpat* (justice) + *tzedakah* (righteousness, right-relationship); embodied in Decalogue, Holiness Code (Leviticus 19), prophetic tradition | *'Adl* (justice) + *ihsan* (beneficence) + *taqwa* (God-consciousness-that-informs-action); 16:90 as compact summary |
| **Justification structure** | Divine command + natural-law reasoning (Aquinas synthesis in Western tradition); love as the fulfillment of law (Romans 13:10) | Divine command + covenant relationship + *mishpat* as objective standard to which God is held (Gen 18:25) | Divine command + inherent righteousness-of-deity's-nature in reformist/Mu'tazilite readings; pure divine command in Ash'arite orthodoxy |
| **Character/compliance framing** | Both — Sermon on the Mount emphasizes character; Pauline tradition varies; Akedah reading determines the balance | Mixed — Genesis 22 and Genesis 18 preserve both; rabbinic interpretation effectively privileges character | Mixed — 49:13 and 4:135 are character-based; 9:5 and 4:34 are compliance-based; theological school determines weighting |
| **Relationship to reason** | Western Christian natural-law tradition holds reason can access moral truths; voluntarist Protestant currents disagree | Talmudic tradition explicitly subordinates divine voice to human reasoning (oven of Akhnai); reason is load-bearing | Mu'tazilite: reason knows good/evil objectively. Ash'arite: reason alone unreliable without revelation. Maturidi: reason knows in principle, revelation required for obligation |

**Convergent finding:** Every tradition presents a primary ethical imperative that centers justice and/or neighbor-love, and every tradition offers *multiple* justification-structures in tension with each other. No tradition is monolithically DCT or monolithically natural-law; each contains both currents in its canonical sources and has settled the tension differently over time.

**Divergent finding:** The traditions differ in which ethical vocabulary they foreground. Christianity leans on *agape* (love) and the affective register. Judaism leans on *mishpat/tzedakah* (justice/righteousness) and the covenantal-forensic register. Islam leans on *'adl/ihsan/taqwa* (justice/beneficence/God-consciousness) and the action-oriented register. These vocabularies are not interchangeable; they shape how each tradition thinks about ethical problems.

### Interpretive handling strategies — comparison

| Strategy | Christian tradition | Jewish tradition | Islamic tradition |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Allegorization** | Origen, medieval Christian exegesis; *Amalek* as spiritual enemy; herem as battle with sin | Rabbinic: Amalek as archetype of causeless hatred (linked to Haman); spiritual reading of *ḥērem* | Classical Sufi reading: verses on external enemies as allegories for battle with the *nafs* (lower self) |
| **Procedural constraint** | Medieval just-war theory (Augustine, Aquinas) imposed conditions that made herem-style warfare effectively inoperative | Maimonides' procedural conditions (*Hilchot Melachim*) on war-commands; Talmudic "four cubits" rule limiting capital punishment | Classical *jihad* jurisprudence specified conditions (declaration, proportionality, non-combatant immunity) that limited operative scope |
| **Inoperability by identification failure** | Not a dominant move in Christian tradition | Talmudic: Sennacherib's mixing of peoples means Amalekite lineage is unidentifiable (Berakhot 28a), rendering command formally-in-force-but-inoperative | Less prominent, though some modern scholars argue 9:5 applies only to specific historical polytheists no longer extant |
| **Progressive revelation / supersession** | New Testament "fulfills" and in some readings supersedes Old Testament; Marcionism is the extreme case | Not native to Judaism; would be anti-Torah; similar work done through interpretation within Torah itself | *Naskh* doctrine: later verses can abrogate earlier ones |
| **Historical contextualism** | Modern critical scholarship: commands reflect ANE contexts rather than universal mandates | Rabbinic "interpret according to the time" (*et la'asot*); extensive historical-contextual tradition | *Asbab al-nuzul* (occasions of revelation): verses interpreted by reference to specific historical situations |
| **Selective omission (pastoral)** | Common: atrocity passages rarely preached; sanitized Vacation Bible School curricula | Less common due to rigorous Torah-reading cycle (annually), though rabbinic tradition does emphasize certain passages over others | Varies significantly by community and school; reformist currents emphasize peaceful verses over martial |

**Convergent finding:** every tradition has evolved multiple interpretive mechanisms to limit the operative scope of atrocity-commands. No living tradition actually implements the commands at the literal level they were written. The gap between text and practice is where ethics happens in all three traditions.

**The critical structural observation:** in all three traditions, the interpretive mechanisms are *external to the commanding text*. Something outside the command — historical context, adjacent passages, jurisprudential principle, communal consensus, tradition's overall moral trajectory — is doing the adjudication. Once that external-to-text adjudication is in operation, DCT as a pure framework has structurally collapsed. The tradition is practicing reasoned interpretation; the DCT language is residue.

**Divergent finding:** the traditions differ in which interpretive mechanisms they have institutionalized and which they have left informal. Jewish tradition has the most developed explicit mechanism for preserving interpretive pluralism (*mahloket l'shem shamayim*, preserved minority opinion in Talmud). Islamic tradition has the most developed explicit mechanism for internal-canon verse-adjudication (*naskh* doctrine, *asbab al-nuzul*). Christian tradition has the most developed explicit mechanism for canon-level progressive revelation (New Testament supersessionism), though this comes with its own severe historical problems regarding Jewish-Christian relations.

### The same-text-licenses-both finding

This is the most important structural observation from the three-tradition comparison:

**Every tradition's canonical text licenses both expansion and contraction. Neither direction is marginal. Both are primary-source features.**

American abolitionism and American slaveholding cited the same Christian Bible. Reform Judaism's *tikkun olam* ethics and Haredi exclusion cite the same Tanakh. Islamic peaceful universalism (2:256, 49:13) and Islamic militant violence (9:5 decontextualized) cite the same Quran. **The text does not adjudicate between the readings**; the interpreters' character does, along with the interpretive mechanisms they apply.

**This is the textual root of the expansion-contraction historical pattern.** The Tier 1 religion paper documented that religions undergo cycles of expansion and contraction. The primary-source passes confirm the pattern is grounded in the canonical sources themselves. Both dynamics are always textually available; which dominates in a given era depends on the character of the deployers, the institutional culture, and the specific historical moment.

### Implication for the framework

The framework cannot ground itself in any single scripture and call the grounding complete. **Any scripture-grounded ethics must include an explicit meta-principle for how to adjudicate between the expansion passages and the contraction passages.** Without such a meta-principle, the tradition will oscillate between the two in ways driven by non-ethical factors (political opportunity, demographic pressure, charismatic leadership).

The meta-principle we've been developing is: **character-over-compliance**, with character defined as the internalized capacity to prefer the expansion-move reading, apply it honestly, and refuse compliance with contraction-readings even when authority-claims cite them. This is what the Good Samaritan parable, the Abraham-at-Sodom negotiation, and the Mu'tazilite theological tradition each model in their own vocabularies.

## 5. Research Question 4 — Scope of neighbor-love

Each tradition articulates a neighbor-love imperative; each differs on scope.

| Tradition | Primary statement | Scope as originally given | Scope as extended within canon |
|---|---|---|---|
| Christian Bible / Tanakh | Leviticus 19:18: "Love your neighbor as yourself" | Fellow Israelite (contextually) | Extended in same chapter (Lev 19:34) to resident alien; further extended by Jesus (Luke 10) to cultural enemy |
| Christian Bible | Jesus's summary (Mark 12:31, Matt 22:39) | Cited by Jesus as one of two greatest commands; neighbor-category left open in statement | Radically redefined in Good Samaritan: neighbor is not a category but a relational property |
| Christian Bible | Galatians 3:28 | Jew/Greek, slave/free, male/female abolished | In principle universal, "in Christ Jesus" — scope-of-application contested |
| Tanakh | Leviticus 19:18 + Hillel's golden rule ("what is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow") | Fellow Israelite primary; extended to resident alien (Lev 19:34) | Hillel's formulation generalizes structurally; extended by midrashic tradition to all humanity in some readings |
| Quran | 49:13 | Explicitly universal: "humanity" (*an-nas*); distinctions by *taqwa* (God-consciousness) alone | Limited in practice by the parallel martial verses and dhimmi institution; reformist readings emphasize universal scope |

**Convergent finding:** every tradition begins with a narrower in-group formulation and contains the resources for extension to broader scope. None of the traditions has ever actually pushed its scope to full universalism consistently; every tradition has failure modes where scope contracts under pressure.

**Distinctive strengths:**
- **Christian Good Samaritan:** re-frames neighbor-hood from category to relational property, which opens arbitrary extension
- **Jewish Hillel formulation:** negatively-framed golden rule ("do not do what is hateful to you") which is closer to the corrected (negatively-framed) golden rule (treat others as they want, not as you want) than the positive Christian formulation
- **Quranic 49:13:** most explicit single-verse universalism in any Abrahamic scripture

**For the framework:** the cross-tradition resources suggest the scope question is best handled not by picking one formulation but by drawing on the strongest features of each. Hillel's negative-framing avoids cultural imposition; Quran 49:13's explicit universalism provides a one-verse anchor; the Good Samaritan's re-framing opens arbitrary scope. Together, these provide stronger scope-articulation than any single tradition alone.

## 6. Research Question 5 — The Euthyphro structure

The foundational philosophical question (from Plato's *Euthyphro*, c. 399 BCE): **is the pious/good pious/good because the gods/God command(s) it, or do the gods/God command it because it is pious/good?**

Each tradition engages this question, explicitly or implicitly, and each tradition has produced settlements.

### Inside the canonical texts themselves

| Tradition | Text-internal Euthyphro moment | Verdict within the text |
|---|---|---|
| Christian Bible / Tanakh | **Genesis 18:25**: Abraham holds God to "what is just" — justice as independent standard | Text approves Abraham's challenge; God accepts it; negotiation succeeds |
| Christian Bible / Tanakh | **Genesis 22**: God commands Isaac's sacrifice; Abraham complies without protest | Text approves compliance; angel's speech endorses "you have not withheld" |
| Christian Bible / Tanakh | **Job** (whole book — Ketuvim): Job demands justice; God's speech refuses direct answer; epilogue vindicates Job against friends who justified-God's-actions | Text preserves the tension unresolved; Job is vindicated despite not getting his answer |
| Quran | **Surah 18 (Al-Kahf)**: Moses witnesses al-Khidr's actions that appear unjust (killing a child, scuttling a boat); told he cannot understand divine wisdom | Text resolves toward divine inscrutability — divine actions are good because divine, even when they appear unjust |

**Finding:** The Tanakh and Christian Old Testament contain *both* Euthyphro-compatible positions simultaneously (Gen 18 contra Gen 22); Job canonizes the irresolution. The Quran leans harder toward the divine-inscrutability resolution (al-Khidr narrative), though with substantial countervailing material (49:13, 4:135, 5:8 all operate on reasoned-justice principles).

### In the theological-philosophical traditions each scripture generated

| Tradition | Dominant settlement | Minority/countervailing |
|---|---|---|
| **Catholic Christianity** | Thomist natural-law: reason can access moral truths; divine commands align with moral truths; good has partial reason-independent content. **Closer to Mu'tazilite side.** | Voluntarist currents (later Franciscans, Ockham's nominalism); Protestant voluntarism (Luther, Calvin in some readings) |
| **Protestant Christianity** | Mixed. Reformed tradition has had both voluntarist (DCT-leaning) and natural-law-accepting strands. Contemporary Reformed voluntarism (Paul Helm) and Reformed natural-law (David VanDrunen) represent internal dispute. | Contemporary evangelical DCT (William Lane Craig, Philip Quinn in modified form) |
| **Orthodox Christianity** | Generally natural-law-accepting, with distinctive emphasis on *theosis* (divinization) and apophatic tradition; the ethical question is less foregrounded philosophically | Voluntarist currents rare |
| **Judaism** | Effectively reasoned-interpretive in practice. Talmudic tradition (oven of Akhnai subordinating divine voice to sages), Maimonides (reason-accepting though complex), modern Jewish philosophical tradition (Hermann Cohen, Levinas, Rosenzweig) all on the reasoned-ethics side | Some mystical-ultra-Orthodox currents more voluntarist, but even these typically work within rabbinic interpretation |
| **Sunni Islam** | **Ash'arite voluntarism** dominant since al-Ghazali (c. 1100). Good is what God commands; reason alone unreliable. This is the DCT position the critique most directly targets. | Maturidi middle position (Hanafi Sunnis); reformist currents drawing on Mu'tazilite heritage (Fazlur Rahman, Abou El Fadl, Soroush) |
| **Shia Islam** | Twelver theology closer to Mu'tazilite / reasoned-ethics side. Good and evil rationally knowable; God commands the good because it is good. | Dominant position in Shia tradition |
| **Secular Western philosophy** | Since Kant (late 18th c.), predominantly reasoned-ethics on the Euthyphro question. Divine command theory has had modern defenders (Philip Quinn, Robert Adams with modification, William Lane Craig) but is minority position in academic ethics | Contemporary DCT defenders; most academic ethics operates on other grounds entirely |

### The settlement landscape, simplified

- **Reasoned-ethics / Mu'tazilite side:** Catholic natural-law tradition, rabbinic/Jewish tradition in practice, Twelver Shia theology, Maturidi Sunni (partially), Western secular philosophy post-Kant.
- **Voluntarist-DCT / Ash'arite side:** Sunni Ash'arite orthodoxy, Protestant voluntarist strands, contemporary evangelical DCT defenders.
- **Middle positions:** Maturidi theology, modified DCT (Adams), divine-motivation theory (Zagzebski).

**For the framework:** the reasoned-ethics side has *much* deeper institutional and intellectual backing across traditions and across secular philosophy than the voluntarist-DCT side. The critique isn't attacking a strawman — it's attacking a real position that has been dominant in specific traditions (notably Sunni Ash'arism and some Protestant strands) but minority elsewhere. When we articulate a character-over-compliance ethics, we are joining the majority tradition across the broader landscape, not inventing something new.

## 7. Structural features comparison

Beyond content, the canons have different structural features that shape how each tradition handles ethical questions.

| Feature | Christian Bible | Tanakh | Quran |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Canonical structure** | Old Testament (overlapping with Tanakh) + New Testament (27 books, composite) | Tri-partite: Torah / Nevi'im / Ketuvim with gradated authority | Single continuous text; 114 surahs arranged largely by length |
| **Authority-tiering** | Flat in principle (all Scripture inspired); in practice NT often privileged | Explicit tri-partite tiering enables internal critique | Flat at canon level; Meccan/Medinan chronological distinction is contextual; *naskh* doctrine provides verse-level override |
| **Internal critique mechanism** | Primarily through progressive-revelation (NT fulfilling/correcting OT); prophetic tradition available but less foregrounded | Prophetic books explicitly canonized as critiquing Temple religion (Amos, Isaiah, Micah); Ketuvim wisdom-skepticism (Job, Ecclesiastes) | Less canonical internal critique; hadith tradition and Sufi currents do related work but non-canonically |
| **Theodicy-interrogation** | Job inherited from Tanakh; Paul's Romans 9-11 engages related material; relatively less sustained than Job itself | **Book of Job — unrivaled canonical engagement** | Al-Khidr narrative (Surah 18); tends toward divine-inscrutability resolution rather than Job-style preserved irresolution |
| **Extra-canonical authority layer** | Church traditions — Catholic Magisterium, Protestant confessions, etc.; varies widely by denomination | Talmud (Mishnah + Gemara), Midrash, medieval commentary (Rashi, Ramban, Maimonides); massive, continuously-developed | Hadith corpus (Bukhari, Muslim, etc.) + *tafsir* + four Sunni madhabs + Shia legal tradition; massive |
| **Institutionalized pluralism** | Limited — Reformation produced denominations but each tends toward internal uniformity | *Mahloket l'shem shamayim* — explicit preservation of minority opinions as authoritative tradition | Madhab pluralism — four Sunni schools + Shia traditions mutually recognized as legitimate despite disagreement |

**Observations:**

1. **Tanakh's structural resources for internal reform are the most developed.** Authority-tiering, prophetic critique canonization, Ketuvim wisdom-skepticism, *mahloket* preservation — this is a canonical culture optimized for ongoing self-correction.

2. **Quran's extra-canonical authority layer is the most developed in scope.** The hadith corpus plus madhab pluralism plus Shia tradition plus Sufi tradition plus philosophical theology creates a layered interpretive ecosystem.

3. **Christian tradition relies heavily on progressive-revelation mechanism.** NT as fulfilling/correcting OT is structurally powerful but has produced severe problems (supersessionism, anti-Judaism). The supersessionist move is a legitimate internal critique of OT atrocity-commands but became a tool of persecution.

**For the framework:** a robust ethics should incorporate the best structural features from each:
- Tanakh's explicit authority-tiering
- Jewish *mahloket* preservation of minority views
- Quranic-style explicit internal-canon override mechanisms (the equivalent: explicit revision protocols)
- Islamic-style institutional interpretive pluralism (the equivalent: multiple review frameworks with mutual recognition)
- Christian tradition's willingness to let later moral reasoning correct earlier text (the equivalent: explicit acknowledgment that ethics improves and earlier framework-versions are superseded)

## 8. The character-over-compliance lineage across traditions

The core thesis of the framework is that character (the internalized capacity for examined ethical judgment) routes around framework failures in ways frameworks alone cannot. This is not novel to the work. Each tradition has articulated this thesis in its own vocabulary.

### Judaism
- **Abraham at Sodom (Gen 18:25):** holds God to independent justice-standard
- **Moses after the Golden Calf (Exodus 32):** argues God into relenting
- **Job:** demands accounting; vindicated despite not getting direct answer
- **Jeremiah 12:1:** "You will be in the right, O LORD, if I make claim against You, yet I shall present charges against You"
- **Oven of Akhnai (Bava Metzia 59b):** sages reject heavenly voice, subordinate divine command to majority reasoning; God laughs and says "My children have defeated Me"
- **Hillel:** golden rule in negative form; Torah as commentary on a reason-graspable principle
- **Maimonides:** *The Guide for the Perplexed* — reconciling philosophical reasoning with revelation
- **Modern Jewish philosophy:** Hermann Cohen, Rosenzweig, Levinas (ethics as first philosophy), Soloveitchik

### Christianity
- **Good Samaritan (Luke 10):** cultural enemy as moral exemplar; neighbor-hood relational not categorical
- **Matthew 25:** treatment of vulnerable as the moral criterion, not group-membership
- **Galatians 3:28:** abolition of ethnic/juridical/gender distinctions "in Christ"
- **Thomas Aquinas:** natural-law as reason-accessible moral knowledge
- **Abolitionist tradition (19th c.):** using Christian principles against Christian practice
- **Civil rights tradition (Martin Luther King Jr.):** using prophetic rebuke against unjust laws cited as Christian
- **Liberation theology (Gutiérrez, etc.):** preferential option for the poor as interpretive principle
- **Modern Christian philosophy:** Linda Zagzebski, Alvin Plantinga's modifications on voluntarism

### Islam
- **Quran 49:13:** human equality with righteousness (*taqwa*) as sole legitimate distinction
- **Quran 4:135:** justice even against self, parents, relatives
- **Quran 5:8:** justice even when dealing with enemies
- **Quran 16:90:** justice, beneficence, generosity commanded
- **Mu'tazilite theology (8th-11th c.):** objective ethics knowable by reason; God commands good because it is good
- **Ibn Rushd / Averroes:** rationalist response to al-Ghazali; preservation of philosophical tradition
- **Shia Twelver theology:** reasoned-ethics side of Euthyphro
- **Contemporary reformists:** Fazlur Rahman, Abdolkarim Soroush, Khaled Abou El Fadl, Abdullahi An-Na'im, Tariq Ramadan

### Secular Western philosophy
- **Plato (*Euthyphro*, c. 399 BCE):** the foundational articulation of the problem
- **Aristotle (*Nicomachean Ethics*):** character (*hexis*) as the substance of virtue ethics
- **Kant:** moral reason as autonomous, not dependent on divine command
- **John Stuart Mill:** utilitarian grounding of ethics in consequences for wellbeing
- **Contemporary virtue ethics:** Alasdair MacIntyre, Martha Nussbaum, Rosalind Hursthouse
- **Contemporary metaethics:** Derek Parfit, Thomas Scanlon, T.M. Scanlon's contractualism

**The cross-tradition finding:** a position that was articulated explicitly in 9th-century Baghdad (Mu'tazila), in 1st-century Jerusalem (Hillel), in 1st-century rabbinic Galilee (Jesus's Good Samaritan parable), in 12th-century Córdoba (Ibn Rushd), in 13th-century Paris (Aquinas), in 18th-century Königsberg (Kant), and in 20th-century virtue ethics recovery (MacIntyre, Nussbaum), is the position the framework articulates for AI. **We are joining a 2,400-year interdisciplinary lineage.**

This matters for how we present the framework. We are not "inventing character-over-compliance." We are saying: *the position that cross-cultural ethics has repeatedly articulated across three millennia, in every major tradition's internal reform-voice, applies to AI ethics, and here's what that application concretely looks like.*

## 9. Where the three traditions converge

Despite their differences, the three traditions converge on several substantive features:

1. **Primary ethical imperative centers justice and/or neighbor-love.** Not power, not purity, not tribal loyalty. Every canon foregrounds these.

2. **Every canon contains both expansion and contraction; neither is marginal.** The same text licenses both; interpretation adjudicates.

3. **Every tradition has developed interpretive mechanisms to limit atrocity-commands' operative scope.** Living traditions do not execute the commands literally.

4. **Every tradition has canonical resources for internal reform / character-over-compliance.** The resources are specific (different in each) but the structural pattern recurs.

5. **Every tradition's history shows the expansion-contraction cycle.** Deployers' character, institutional culture, and historical circumstances determine which direction dominates in a given era.

6. **The Euthyphro question has been engaged in every tradition, and the reasoned-ethics side has the deeper and broader institutional backing.** Even in traditions where the voluntarist side is officially dominant (Sunni Ash'arite orthodoxy), reasoned-ethics resources are preserved and active.

## 10. Where the three traditions genuinely diverge

Honest divergences that don't dissolve on closer examination:

1. **The role of embodiment.** Christianity centers incarnation (God becomes flesh in Jesus) as a theological and ethical principle; this has no direct parallel in Judaism or Islam, and it shapes Christian ethics in distinctive ways (kenosis, imitation of Christ, sacramental understanding of ordinary human life).

2. **The role of the community.** Judaism is fundamentally communal-covenantal; Christianity has strong communal elements but has also developed more individualist strands (especially post-Reformation); Islam is communal-*ummah*-centered but without the ethnic-covenantal specificity of Judaism.

3. **The scope of Law/halakha/shari'a.** Judaism has extensive halakha covering nearly all of daily life. Islam has extensive shari'a similarly. Christianity (especially Protestant Christianity) has much less detailed legal regulation, with ethics often articulated as general principles rather than specific rulings.

4. **Theodicy resolution.** Job's preserved-irresolution is Jewish-distinctive; Christian theology tends toward Christ's suffering as the resolution of theodicy; Islamic theology tends toward divine-inscrutability as resolution.

5. **Relationship to philosophical tradition.** Islam and Judaism both engaged Greek philosophy deeply and produced major philosophical traditions (Islamic *falsafa*, medieval Jewish philosophy). Christianity also did (Scholasticism), but with different trajectories — Christian philosophy became more closely integrated with theology, while Islamic philosophy faced more resistance from orthodox theology after al-Ghazali.

**For the framework:** these divergences mean the inheritance is not uniform. The framework should be honest about which traditions contribute which resources and should not flatten the differences. Jewish contributions to the framework are not interchangeable with Islamic contributions, even if both are genuine.

## 11. What this synthesis establishes

### Confirmed: the refined DCT critique is a cross-tradition structural finding
The atrocity-permit thesis isn't a critique of one tradition — it's a critique of a theological structure that appears, with variations, across all three major Abrahamic scriptures. That structural uniformity makes the critique robust. It's not cherry-picking one tradition's worst passages; it's identifying a recurring failure mode.

### Confirmed: character-over-compliance has a 2,400-year cross-tradition lineage
The framework is not inventing its central thesis. It is articulating for AI what cross-cultural ethics has repeatedly articulated in every major tradition. This changes how the framework should be presented — by citing the lineage, not by claiming originality.

### Confirmed: interpretive culture matters more than canonical content
The same scriptures have produced radically different ethical outcomes depending on which interpretive mechanisms were institutionalized, which resources were foregrounded, and what character the deployers had. This applies to AI ethics too: the framework-content matters less than the interpretive culture that forms around it.

### Confirmed: the expansion-contraction cycle is structural, not accidental
Every tradition has gone through it. The framework must include explicit mechanisms to resist contraction — not optimistic assumptions that good intentions will prevent it.

### Opened: specific resources from each tradition can be inherited
Not through syncretism — that would be ahistorical. Through specific acknowledgment: the framework inherits the Mu'tazilite theological position, the Jewish *mahloket* preservation mechanism, the prophetic internal-critique tradition, the Good Samaritan re-framing of neighbor-hood, Hillel's golden rule formulation, and the secular philosophical tradition from Plato through Kant through contemporary virtue ethics.

### Opened: what the framework owes to each tradition by way of acknowledgment
If the framework is published, the acknowledgment section needs to be specific. The framework is not "universal human ethics" — it is the synthesis of specific traditions' best character-over-compliance resources, applied to AI. That specificity is an ethical requirement, not a bureaucratic one. Traditions that provided the resources should be named.

## 12. Remaining gaps for follow-up work

1. **Primary-source philosophical literature pass** — Plato's *Euthyphro*, Aquinas, Ockham, Adams, Quinn, critics from Nielsen through Sinnott-Armstrong. (Queued as next paper.)

2. **Hadith primary-source pass.** The Islamic side of the three-tradition comparison is incomplete without engaging Bukhari, Muslim, and the Sunan collections directly.

3. **Talmud primary-source pass.** Sanhedrin tractate especially (for oven of Akhnai and capital-punishment jurisprudence), plus Pirkei Avot for ethical principles.

4. **Patristic and medieval Christian primary sources.** Augustine, Aquinas, the Franciscan voluntarists, the Reformers. The "Christian tradition on DCT" claim should be grounded in specific texts.

5. **Modern reformist sources in each tradition.** For Christianity: liberation theology primary texts (Gutiérrez), feminist theology (Schüssler Fiorenza), process theology. For Judaism: Heschel, Soloveitchik, contemporary halakhic debates. For Islam: Fazlur Rahman, Abou El Fadl, Soroush in primary source.

6. **Apologetic literature to engage on its own terms.** William Lane Craig (divine-command defense); Paul Copan (*Is God a Moral Monster?*); critical responses (Thom Stark, Randal Rauser). The defense of atrocity-commands deserves direct engagement.

7. **Non-Abrahamic comparative work.** Buddhism (especially Zen and engaged Buddhism), Hinduism (*ahimsa* tradition, Bhagavad Gita ethical debates), Confucianism (ren/li as analogues to character), indigenous traditions. The three-Abrahamic focus reflects the framework's initial grounding context; the framework's eventual scope should engage more broadly.

8. **Comparative-historical failure modes.** Each tradition has contracted catastrophically in specific ways. The Spanish Inquisition, the Crusades, the Thirty Years' War, chattel slavery, the Holocaust, the Balkan wars, partition-era India, contemporary extremist movements in every tradition. Understanding how contraction happens historically is load-bearing for building frameworks that resist it.

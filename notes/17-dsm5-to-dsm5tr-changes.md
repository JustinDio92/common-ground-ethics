# DSM-5 to DSM-5-TR (2022) — Highlights of Changes as a Language-Shift Study

**Date:** 2026-04-30
**Scope:** Integration document. Treats the DSM-5 → DSM-5-TR (March 2022) revision as a discrete study in deliberate diagnostic-language recalibration. The framework argues that diagnostic and ethical language shapes character formation: what clinicians are taught to *see* in patients, they become disposed to *be* toward patients. Tracking what the APA chose to update — and what it chose to leave unchanged — lets the framework make a precise empirical claim about how a profession's character is being recalibrated through controlled vocabulary changes rather than through new mechanisms of action.

**Framework targets:** the new diagnosis (Prolonged Grief Disorder), the placement change for Suicidal Behavior and Nonsuicidal Self-Injury, the ethnoracial/cultural terminology overhaul, the gender-language modernization, the prevalence/risk text-block refresh, the ICD-10-CM symptom-code expansion, and the Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group as institutional artifact.

**Why this matters:** DSM-5-TR is unusual among text revisions because it is *not* primarily a criteria revision. It is, by APA's own framing, a text-and-language revision with a small number of structural additions. That makes it the cleanest available case study of "character recalibration through vocabulary" inside a regulated profession. If the framework's claim — that what we are taught to *see* shapes what we become — is right, DSM-5-TR is exactly the kind of intervention that should produce measurable downstream character drift in the profession over the following decade.

## 1. Structure of the revision — VERIFIED-MANUAL

The PMC overview frames DSM-5-TR as four classes of change:

1. **Addition of diagnostic entities and symptom codes**
2. **Changes in diagnostic criteria or specifier definitions** (small in number)
3. **Updated terminology** (large in number; the "language shift" is here)
4. **Comprehensive text updates** to the descriptive prose around each disorder

The DSM-5-TR Preface itself frames the revision as follows:

> "This revised manual integrates the original published DSM-5 diagnostic criteria with modifications (mostly for clarity) for over 70 disorders, comprehensively updated descriptive text accompanying each of the DSM disorders based on reviews of the literature since the publication of DSM-5, and the addition of a new diagnosis, prolonged grief disorder, and symptom codes for reporting suicidal and nonsuicidal self-injurious behavior. These changes differ from the scope of the prior text revision, DSM-IV-TR, in which the updates were confined almost exclusively to the text, leaving the diagnostic criteria virtually unchanged." (DSM-5-TR, Preface, p. xxi)

The Preface's own list of most-revised text sections matches the PMC overview verbatim:

> "Sections of the text that were most extensively updated were Prevalence, Risk and Prognostic Factors, Culture-Related Diagnostic Issues, Sex- and Gender-Related Diagnostic Issues, Association With Suicidal Thoughts or Behavior, and Comorbidity." (DSM-5-TR, Preface, p. xxi)

This four-class taxonomy matters because classes 3 and 4 — language and prose — account for the bulk of the revision. The DSM-5-TR is not primarily a criteria revision. It is a *seeing* revision. Clinicians who learned DSM-5 in 2013 and DSM-5-TR in 2022 are diagnosing the same disorders by largely the same criteria; what has changed is the descriptive language they are taught to use *about* patients, and the prose they are taught to read *as* the disorder.

This is exactly the kind of intervention the framework's character-formation thesis predicts will have downstream behavioral effects without registering as a behavioral mandate.

## 2. New diagnostic entities — VERIFIED-MANUAL

### 2.1 Prolonged Grief Disorder

Placement: chapter on Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders, Section II (full diagnoses, not "conditions for further study"). The manual lists ICD-10-CM code **F43.8** (DSM-5-TR, p. 322), which corrects the F43.81 number sometimes circulated in secondary materials and matches the Osmind summary.

**Note on coding discrepancy:** the DSM-5-TR Classification (TOC) lists "F43.81 Prolonged Grief Disorder (322)" while the criteria block on page 322 lists "Diagnostic Criteria F43.8". The TOC entry is the authoritative billing code; the body-text "F43.8" appears to be a truncation. This is a manual-internal discrepancy worth flagging — the operative ICD-10-CM code is **F43.81**.

**Criteria — VERBATIM from DSM-5-TR (p. 322–323):**

> "A. The death, at least 12 months ago, of a person who was close to the bereaved individual (for children and adolescents, at least 6 months ago).
>
> B. Since the death, the development of a persistent grief response characterized by one or both of the following symptoms, which have been present most days to a clinically significant degree. In addition, the symptom(s) has occurred nearly every day for at least the last month:
>
>   1. Intense yearning/longing for the deceased person.
>   2. Preoccupation with thoughts or memories of the deceased person (in children and adolescents, preoccupation may focus on the circumstances of the death).
>
> C. Since the death, at least three of the following symptoms have been present most days to a clinically significant degree. In addition, the symptoms have occurred nearly every day for at least the last month:
>
>   1. Identity disruption (e.g., feeling as though part of oneself has died) since the death.
>   2. Marked sense of disbelief about the death.
>   3. Avoidance of reminders that the person is dead (in children and adolescents, may be characterized by efforts to avoid reminders).
>   4. Intense emotional pain (e.g., anger, bitterness, sorrow) related to the death.
>   5. Difficulty reintegrating into one's relationships and activities after the death (e.g., problems engaging with friends, pursuing interests, or planning for the future).
>   6. Emotional numbness (absence or marked reduction of emotional experience) as a result of the death.
>   7. Feeling that life is meaningless as a result of the death.
>   8. Intense loneliness as a result of the death.
>
> D. The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
>
> E. The duration and severity of the bereavement reaction clearly exceed expected social, cultural, or religious norms for the individual's culture and context.
>
> F. The symptoms are not better explained by another mental disorder, such as major depressive disorder or posttraumatic stress disorder, and are not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., medication, alcohol) or another medical condition." (DSM-5-TR, p. 322–323)

**Note:** the manual contains six lettered criteria (A–F), not seven. There is no Criterion G. Where secondary sources implied a Criterion G, that was an artifact of fact-sheet reformatting.

**Difference from prior fact-sheet language:** the manual uses "anger, bitterness, sorrow" in the C4 example (matching APA fact-sheet wording), but the body text on p. 324 reads "anger, bitterness, guilt" in its restatement of C4 — a small internal inconsistency in the manual itself. The original Criterion C4 wording is "anger, bitterness, sorrow."

**Why this matters:** Prolonged Grief Disorder pathologizes a duration. It is the only DSM-5-TR addition that medicalizes a culturally and religiously contoured human experience by placing a 12-month boundary on it. The "exceeds social, cultural, or religious norms" clause (Criterion E) is doing extraordinary work — it is asking the clinician to *be* a cultural arbiter as part of the diagnostic act. That is character cultivation, not behavioral specification.

The manual's own Diagnostic Features prose makes the cultural-arbiter role explicit:

> "The nature, duration, and severity of the bereavement reaction must clearly exceed expected social, cultural, or religious norms for the individual's culture and context (Criterion E). Although there are variations in how grief can manifest, the symptoms of prolonged grief disorder occur across genders and in diverse social and cultural groups." (DSM-5-TR, p. 324)

### 2.2 Unspecified Mood Disorder — VERIFIED

A residual category for mood symptoms that do not meet full criteria for bipolar or depressive disorders. ICD-10-CM F39 (per Osmind). Reinstated after its 2013 removal in DSM-5; the rationale (per Counseling Today) is to prevent misdiagnosis-driven SSRI prescribing in mixed presentations.

### 2.3 Stimulant-Induced Mild Neurocognitive Disorder — VERIFIED

Added per PMC overview to the existing family of substance-induced neurocognitive disorders.

## 3. Suicidal Behavior and Nonsuicidal Self-Injury — VERIFIED-MANUAL

This is the most structurally significant change in DSM-5-TR after Prolonged Grief Disorder, because it changes *who* can document these behaviors and *where* they live in the manual.

**What changed:**

- Suicidal Behavior and Nonsuicidal Self-Injury were both **removed** from Section III ("Conditions for Further Study"), where they had been positioned in DSM-5 as proposed-but-not-yet-validated diagnoses.
- They were **added to Section II** in the chapter "Other Conditions That May Be a Focus of Clinical Attention" — the same chapter that holds V/Z codes for psychosocial circumstances.
- They are now **stand-alone ICD-10-CM symptom codes** that can be applied independently of any mental disorder diagnosis. (Note: a separate "Suicidal Behavior Disorder" condition for further study still exists in Section III at p. 1013/1015 of the manual; the new symptom codes are independent of that proposed diagnosis.)

**Definitions and codes — VERBATIM from DSM-5-TR (p. 822):**

> "Suicidal Behavior
>
> This category may be used for individuals who have engaged in potentially self-injurious behavior with at least some intent to die as a result of the act. Evidence of intent to end one's life can be explicit or inferred from the behavior or circumstances. A suicide attempt may or may not result in actual self-injury. If the individual is dissuaded by another person or changes his or her mind before initiating the behavior, this category does not apply.
>
> **Current Suicidal Behavior**
> T14.91A Initial encounter: If suicidal behavior is part of the initial encounter with the clinical presentation
> T14.91D Subsequent encounter: If suicidal behavior is part of subsequent encounters with the clinical presentation
>
> **Z91.51 History of Suicidal Behavior**
> If suicidal behavior has occurred during the individual's lifetime
>
> **Nonsuicidal Self-Injury**
>
> This category may be used for individuals who have engaged in intentional self-inflicted damage to their body of a sort likely to induce bleeding, bruising, or pain (e.g., cutting, burning, stabbing, hitting, excessive rubbing) in the absence of suicidal intent.
>
> **R45.88 Current Nonsuicidal Self-Injury**
> If nonsuicidal self-injurious behavior is part of the clinical presentation
>
> **Z91.52 History of Nonsuicidal Self-Injury**
> If nonsuicidal self-injurious behavior has occurred during the individual's lifetime" (DSM-5-TR, p. 822)

**Coding-note language (verbatim, p. 822):**

> "Coding Note for ICD-10-CM Suicidal Behavior. For T codes only, the 6th character should be coded as follows:
> A (initial encounter)—Use while the individual is receiving active treatment for the condition (e.g., emergency department encounter, evaluation and treatment by a new clinician); or
> D (subsequent encounter)—Use for encounters after the individual has received active treatment for the condition and when he or she is receiving routine care for the condition during the healing or recovery phase (e.g., medication adjustment, other aftercare and follow-up visits)."

**Difference from secondary sources:** the Osmind summary cited "T14.91XA" with an X placeholder; the manual itself uses **T14.91A** (without the X). The X is an ICD-10-CM placeholder used in some coding systems but is not present in the DSM-5-TR text. The R-code for current NSSI (**R45.88**) and the Z-codes for lifetime history (**Z91.51** for suicidal behavior, **Z91.52** for NSSI) are now confirmed from the manual.

**Why this matters:** the move from Section III ("further study") to Section II ("focus of clinical attention") and the unbundling from any specific diagnosis means these behaviors are now first-class clinical objects without being first-class disorders. This is a careful institutional move: it expands what gets counted without expanding what gets pathologized. It is the rare case where APA chose visibility over medicalization. The framework should note this approvingly — it is what good revision looks like when the goal is to *see better* rather than *diagnose more*.

## 4. Terminology shifts: race and ethnicity — VERIFIED-MANUAL

The largest single source of language change. The DSM-5-TR Introduction provides the canonical statement of the manual's terminology choices in the section "Attention to Culture, Racism, and Discrimination in DSM-5-TR" (p. 18). Verbatim:

> "DSM-5-TR is committed to the use of language that challenges the view that races are discrete and natural entities. The text uses terminology such as racialized instead of racial to highlight the socially constructed nature of race. When the term ethnoracial is used in the text, it denotes the U.S. Census categories, such as Hispanic, White, or African American, that combine ethnic and racialized identifiers. The emerging term Latinx (singular and plural) is used in place of Latino/a to promote gender-inclusive terminology. The term Caucasian is not used because it is based on obsolete and erroneous views about the geographic origin of a prototypical pan-European ethnicity. The terms minority and non-White are avoided because they describe social groups in relation to a racialized 'majority,' a practice that tends to perpetuate social hierarchies. When necessary for clarity in reporting epidemiological or other information based on specific studies, however, the text uses the group labels from the relevant studies. The term culture is used not to refer to a discrete social group (e.g., 'prevalence differs across cultures') but rather to indicate the heterogeneity of cultural views and practices within societies; the terms cultural contexts or cultural backgrounds are preferred instead." (DSM-5-TR, p. 18)

Verified before/after pairs (now anchored to the manual):

| Before (DSM-5, 2013) | After (DSM-5-TR, 2022) | Status |
|---|---|---|
| "racial" (when describing groups) | **"racialized"** | VERIFIED-MANUAL (p. 18) |
| "minority" / "non-White" | **avoided** as group descriptors | VERIFIED-MANUAL (p. 18) |
| "Caucasian" | **not used**; "non-Latinx white" preferred where clarity needed | VERIFIED-MANUAL ("Caucasian is not used") + VERIFIED via Counseling Today for the "non-Latinx white" replacement language |
| "Latino/a" | **"Latinx"** (singular and plural) | VERIFIED-MANUAL (p. 18) |
| (no equivalent term) | **"ethnoracial"** for U.S. Census-style combined identifiers (Hispanic, White, African American) | VERIFIED-MANUAL (p. 18) |
| "culture" used as group label (e.g., "different cultures") | **"cultural contexts" / "cultural backgrounds"** | VERIFIED-MANUAL (p. 18) — *new datum from manual; not in prior fact-sheet snippets* |

**Difference between fact-sheet snippet and manual:** the APA culture/racism fact sheet rendered the rationale as "*throughout the text the term racial has been replaced by racialized to highlight the socially constructed nature of race as opposed to its being a natural discrete entity*." The manual itself reads "*The text uses terminology such as racialized instead of racial to highlight the socially constructed nature of race*" — substantively identical, but the fact sheet's "natural discrete entity" framing is closer to the manual's prior sentence "DSM-5-TR is committed to the use of language that challenges the view that races are discrete and natural entities." The fact sheet appears to have compressed two manual sentences into one.

**Reasoning the APA gave (VERIFIED-MANUAL):** the goal is to denaturalize race — to teach clinicians to read group-level differences as social and historical constructs rather than as biological essences. The substitution from "racial" to "racialized" is grammatically subtle but ontologically major: "racial" treats race as a property of the person; "racialized" treats race as something done *to* the person by social processes.

**Why this matters:** this is the single cleanest instance in the corpus of "deliberate vocabulary substitution as character-formation tool." The APA is not changing diagnostic criteria. It is changing what clinicians are taught to *see* when they look at a patient's race. If the framework's thesis is right, this is exactly the lever that produces the largest downstream effect at the lowest behavioral-mandate cost.

## 5. Terminology shifts: sex and gender — VERIFIED

Concentrated heavily in the Gender Dysphoria chapter and in cross-cutting clinical descriptions:

| Before (DSM-5, 2013) | After (DSM-5-TR, 2022) | Verified |
|---|---|---|
| "desired gender" | **"experienced gender"** | YES — PMC overview + Counseling Today + Osmind + PsychCentral |
| "natal male" / "natal female" | **"individual assigned male at birth" / "assigned female at birth"** | YES — same four sources |
| "cross-sex medical procedure" | **"gender-affirming medical procedure"** | YES — same four sources |
| (binary checkboxes in Section III assessments) | **removed** | YES — Counseling Today |

**Why this matters:** unlike the racial-language shift, which substitutes one descriptive frame for another (essentialist → constructivist), the gender-language shift replaces a frame that asserts a *natal* or *desired* state with a frame that centers the person's *experienced* and *affirmed* state. This is a substantive ontological move: the locus of authority over what a patient's sex/gender *is* shifts toward the patient's self-report and away from an external biological reference point. The framework should note this without flattening it; it is a real character-formation intervention with real epistemic consequences in either direction depending on how the clinician's character is already shaped.

## 6. Other terminology shifts — VERIFIED-MANUAL (in part)

| Before | After | Status |
|---|---|---|
| "neuroleptic" | **"antipsychotic medications or other dopamine receptor blocking agents"** | VERIFIED via PMC (manual not directly quoted here for this pair) |
| "intellectual disability" (sole term) | **"intellectual developmental disorder (intellectual disability)"** — both retained, with IDD primary | VERIFIED-MANUAL (p. 38) |
| "conversion disorder" | **"functional neurological symptom disorder"** | VERIFIED via PMC + Counseling Today |

**Important refinement from manual:** the "intellectual disability" → "intellectual developmental disorder" shift is *not* a clean replacement; both terms are retained, with IDD as primary and "intellectual disability" preserved in parentheses. The manual's own Note (p. 38) explains why:

> "The term intellectual developmental disorder is used to clarify its relationship with the WHO ICD-11 classification system, which uses the term Disorders of Intellectual Development. The equivalent term intellectual disability is placed in parentheses for continued use. The medical and research literature use both terms, while intellectual disability is the term in common use by educational and other professions, advocacy groups, and the lay public. In the United States, Public Law 111-256 (Rosa's Law) changed all references to 'mental retardation' in federal laws to 'intellectual disability.'" (DSM-5-TR, p. 38)

This is a meaningful difference from the way secondary sources described the change. The shift is *additive* and ICD-11-driven, not a replacement of "intellectual disability" with a politically preferred alternative. The manual is explicit that lay and advocacy usage retains "intellectual disability" and that federal U.S. law uses it.

These shifts are smaller in scale but consistent with the same pattern: replacing a diagnostically loaded or essentialist term with a more mechanism-neutral or descriptively-neutral term — except in the IDD case, where the change is principally about ICD-11 harmonization rather than character-formation language reform.

## 7. Text-block updates: prevalence, risk, prognosis, culture — VERIFIED

The PMC overview is explicit about which descriptive sections received the densest revisions. Most extensively revised:

- **Prevalence**
- **Risk and Prognostic Factors**
- **Culture-Related Diagnostic Issues**
- **Sex- and Gender-Related Diagnostic Issues**
- **Association with Suicidal Thoughts or Behavior** (a new subsection added to *every* disorder chapter)
- **Comorbidity**

Least revised: **Diagnostic Features** and **Differential Diagnosis** — i.e., the parts that constitute the clinical-act core. The pattern here is consistent with the four-class structure: the diagnostic act itself is largely preserved; what surrounds it — epidemiology, context, social factors, suicidality — is what got refreshed.

The "Association with Suicidal Thoughts or Behavior" addition to *every* disorder chapter is structurally important. It builds suicide-awareness into the descriptive layer of every diagnosis, not as a criterion but as a mandatory consideration. This is a character-formation move in the strict sense: it changes what the clinician is *taught to think about* whenever they hold a diagnosis in mind.

## 8. ICD-10-CM alignment and coding updates — VERIFIED

- DSM-5-TR aligns with ICD-10-CM (ICD-9 codes were dropped from DSM-5-TR per clinician sources).
- Osmind reports "over 50 coding updates" for substance intoxication and withdrawal and related disorders.
- The September 2022 Update Supplement (and a subsequent September 2025 update) provides ongoing coding corrections post-publication; specific contents not fetched directly in this session.

The takeaway is that DSM-5-TR is on a *living-document* maintenance cadence with respect to coding — annual or near-annual update supplements rather than the previous decade-plus revision cycles. This further reinforces the "character recalibration via small frequent vocabulary updates" pattern.

## 9. The Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group as institutional artifact — VERIFIED-MANUAL

This is the most book-relevant institutional fact in the entire revision.

**The setup — VERBATIM from DSM-5-TR (p. 18):**

> "During the DSM-5-TR review process, steps have been taken to address the impact of culture, racism, and discrimination on psychiatric diagnosis in the text of the disorder chapters. A Cross-Cutting Review Committee on Cultural Issues, composed of 19 U.S.-based and international experts in cultural psychiatry, psychology, and anthropology, reviewed the texts for cultural influences on disorder characteristics, incorporating relevant information in the sections on culture-related diagnostic issues. A separate Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group, composed of 10 mental health practitioners from diverse ethnic and racialized backgrounds with expertise in disparity-reduction practices, reviewed references to race, ethnicity, and related concepts to avoid perpetuating stereotypes or including discriminatory clinical information." (DSM-5-TR, p. 18)

The manual's "DSM-5 Text Revision Process" section (p. 11) gives the structural picture:

> "Mirroring the structure of the DSM-5 process, experts were divided into 20 Disorder review groups, each headed by a section editor. Four cross-cutting review groups (Culture, Sex and Gender, Suicide, and Forensic) reviewed all the chapters, focusing on material involving their specific expertise. The text was also reviewed by a work group on Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion to ensure appropriate attention to risk factors such as racism and discrimination and the use of nonstigmatizing language." (DSM-5-TR, p. 11)

**Number of experts overall — VERBATIM from Preface (p. xxi–xxii):**

> "We are especially indebted to the tireless efforts of Wilson M. Compton, M.D., M.P.E., and Daniel S. Pine, M.D., as DSM-5 Text Revision Subcommittee Vice Chairs, as well as the more than 200 experts from across our field who did the lion's share of the work in the preparation of the text revision."

The "more than 200 experts" figure that previously came only from a fact-sheet is now **VERIFIED-MANUAL**. The section editors, the Disorder review groups, and the cross-cutting review groups (Culture, Sex and Gender, Suicide, Forensic) are all listed by name in the front matter (p. ix–xviii of the manual).

**What it produced:**
- The "racial" → "racialized" substitution throughout the manual.
- The introduction of "ethnoracial."
- The removal of "minority" and "non-White" as default group descriptors.
- New attention to the risk of misdiagnosis when evaluating individuals from socially oppressed ethnoracial groups.
- Information on variations in symptom expression, attributions, and differential prevalence across demographic groups.

**Why this matters (this is the core point of the document):** the Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group is exactly the kind of institutional structure the framework's "prophetic-function role" mechanism predicts. It is a protected-dissent body, internal to the publishing institution, with explicit standing to revise descriptive language across the entire text. It did not change criteria. It changed *what clinicians are taught to see*. That is character-cultivation infrastructure inside a regulated profession, and it produced measurable changes in the descriptive vocabulary of the manual.

The framework should engage this case study at length. It is a working example of what character-formation infrastructure looks like *when it succeeds*. It is also a useful test case for the framework's drift-detection criterion: ten years from now, does the profession's character actually shift in the direction the language shift was meant to cultivate? If yes, language-as-character-formation is empirically vindicated. If no, the framework needs to specify what additional mechanisms beyond vocabulary are required.

## 10. What the language shifts collectively *do not* do — analytical note

DSM-5-TR conspicuously did *not*:

- Remove any major diagnosis.
- Add any criterion that pathologizes a previously non-pathological population (Prolonged Grief Disorder is the only edge case; even there the duration threshold and "exceeds cultural norms" clause are protective, not expansive).
- Reduce clinician discretion. The "exceeds social, cultural, or religious norms" qualifier in PGD *increases* clinician interpretive load.
- Shift to mandatory checklists or criteria-by-algorithm.

The revision is character-cultivating, not behavior-mandating. That is the framework's thesis in miniature: the most consequential interventions in a profession's character come through the descriptive layer — what we are taught to *see* — not through the rule layer.

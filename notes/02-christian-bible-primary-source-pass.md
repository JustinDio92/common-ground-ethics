## 1. The atrocity-commands in their own words

The refined DCT critique says: the framework's distinctive contribution is not the goodness (derivable secularly) but the license for harm whenever authority commands it. To test this against the primary text, we need the actual atrocity-commands in their own words — not sanitized paraphrases, not allegorized versions, not softened translations. Here they are.

### 1 Samuel 15:2–3 (VERIFIED)

> **15:2** Thus says the Lord of hosts: I will punish the Amalekites for what they did in opposing the Israelites when they came up out of Egypt.
>
> **15:3** Now go and attack Amalek and utterly destroy all that they have; do not spare them, but kill both man and woman, child and infant, ox and sheep, camel and donkey.

This is a primary-source command, attributed in the text to the Lord of hosts (YHWH Sabaoth), given through the prophet Samuel to King Saul. The command specifies:

- Totality: "utterly destroy all that they have"
- Scope: "both man and woman, child and infant"
- Extension beyond combatants to non-combatant populations, including infants
- Extension beyond humans to livestock

The word translated "utterly destroy" represents the Hebrew *ḥērem* — a technical term meaning "devoted to destruction" or "consecrated-for-annihilation." The action is framed as a religious act, not merely a military one. The killing is an offering.

### Joshua 6:17–21 (VERIFIED)

> **17** The city and all that is in it shall be devoted to the Lord for destruction. Only Rahab the prostitute and all who are with her in her house shall live because she hid the messengers we sent.
>
> **21** Then they devoted to destruction by the edge of the sword all in the city, both men and women, young and old, oxen, sheep, and donkeys.

This is the narrative execution of the Deuteronomy 7 / 1 Samuel 15 pattern. Jericho is "devoted to the Lord for destruction" (*ḥērem* again), and verse 21 reports the execution: men, women, young, old, livestock. The only exception is Rahab's household, because she protected the Israelite spies — i.e., the criterion for exemption is assistance to the conquering army, not innocence, age, or non-combatant status.

### Deuteronomy 7:1–6 (VERIFIED)

> **7:1** When the Lord your God brings you into the land that you are about to enter and occupy and he clears away many nations before you—the Hittites, the Girgashites, the Amorites, the Canaanites, the Perizzites, the Hivites, and the Jebusites, seven nations more numerous and mightier than you—
>
> **7:2** and when the Lord your God gives them over to you and you defeat them, then you must utterly destroy them. Make no covenant with them and show them no mercy.
>
> **7:3** Do not intermarry with them, giving your daughters to their sons or taking their daughters for your sons,
>
> **7:4** for that would turn away your children from following me, to serve other gods. Then the anger of the Lord would be kindled against you, and he would destroy you quickly.
>
> **7:5** But this is how you must deal with them: break down their altars, smash their pillars, cut down their sacred poles, and burn their idols with fire.
>
> **7:6** For you are a people holy to the Lord your God; the Lord your God has chosen you out of all the peoples on earth to be his people, his treasured possession.

Deuteronomy 7 generalizes the pattern: seven named nations are to be utterly destroyed. The command is paired with prohibitions against covenant-making and intermarriage — i.e., it is not merely kill-or-be-killed in a battle, it is the prevention of cultural or genetic mixing with the target populations. The justification given is religious: to prevent apostasy. The passage ends by locating Israel's status as "a people holy to the Lord," with the destruction of other peoples framed as a corollary of Israel's election.

### Leviticus 25:44–46 (VERIFIED)

> **44** As for the male and female slaves whom you may have, it is from the nations around you that you may acquire male and female slaves.
>
> **45** You may also acquire them from among the aliens residing with you and from their families who are with you who have been born in your land; they may be your property.
>
> **46** You may keep them as a possession for your children after you, for them to inherit as property. These you may treat as slaves, but as for your fellow Israelites, no one shall rule over the other with harshness.

This is one of the most important passages for the DCT critique because it is not narrative but *legal code* — permanent regulation, not wartime command. Chattel slavery of non-Israelites is legislated as permissible, inheritable property. The passage also establishes an asymmetry: fellow Israelites cannot be treated with harshness, but aliens and foreigners can be "treated as slaves" and passed down as inheritance.

**Important exegetical note (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE):** Leviticus 25 has often been cited by defenders as containing provisions more humane than surrounding Ancient Near Eastern codes — Hebrew slaves were to be released in the Jubilee year, for instance. This is accurate as historical comparison but does not soften the structural point: the primary text explicitly legislates permanent chattel slavery of non-Israelites as property. The "more humane than ANE" defense concedes that the governing framework permits what modern moral intuition rejects, while attempting to rescue the framework on comparative-historical grounds. For the DCT structural critique, what matters is that the framework licenses the institution, not whether it regulates it more or less severely than alternatives that also licensed it.

### Other atrocity-commands not fetched here but present in the text (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE)

- **The Flood** (Genesis 6–9): God commits to the destruction of all living things except Noah's family and paired animals. The reason given is pervasive human wickedness. This is not a command to humans but an act of divinity; still relevant because the text attributes to the moral source itself an act of mass-killing of humans, including infants and non-human animals.
- **Firstborn plague** (Exodus 11–12): the tenth plague on Egypt kills every firstborn, including children who had no individual agency in Pharaoh's decisions.
- **Numbers 31**: Midianite war, command to kill all the males and married women, keep the virgins for the Israelite soldiers.
- **Deuteronomy 13:6–11**: command to kill family members who attempt to lead one into idolatry — "Your own brother or your son or daughter or the wife you embrace or your most intimate friend."
- **Deuteronomy 20:10–18**: rules of warfare. Distant cities are to be offered terms; cities within the promised-land boundary are to be entirely destroyed (vv. 16–18).
- **Psalm 137:9**: "Happy shall they be who take your little ones and dash them against the rock!" — a blessing-pronouncement on the killing of Babylonian infants.

Each of these passages bears its own interpretive tradition. Their collective weight is that atrocity-commands are not a marginal feature of the text — they are a recurring structural feature, distributed across narrative (Joshua, 1 Samuel), law (Deuteronomy, Leviticus, Numbers), and poetry (Psalms).

### Interpretive-tradition handling (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE)

Religious traditions have handled these passages in roughly the following ways across history:

| Approach | What it does | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Literal acceptance** | Treats commands as God-given and therefore good; defends the morality of the actions as divinely justified | Preserves internal consistency of DCT | Requires endorsing the moral status of infant-killing and chattel slavery; most modern adherents find this intolerable |
| **Typological / allegorical** | Reads the *ḥērem* commands as figures of spiritual realities (destroying sin in the self, etc.) rather than historical prescription | Preserves modern moral intuition; common in medieval interpretation | Concedes that text-alone cannot determine meaning; requires an outside-the-text hermeneutic key |
| **Progressive revelation** | Treats earlier harsh commands as accommodations to human hardness of heart, superseded by later (especially New Testament) ethical development | Provides internal canon for reform | Requires judging parts of the text as morally inferior, which concedes that something outside the text is doing the judging |
| **Historical-critical / JEDP** | Reads passages as composite, edited, reflecting the concerns of specific Ancient Near Eastern communities rather than universal divine commands | Accurate as scholarship; dissolves the "God actually commanded this" problem | Once accepted, the whole DCT structure dissolves too — the commands become historically contingent rather than divinely authoritative |
| **Cherry-pick** | Quote the loving passages; avoid the atrocity passages in preaching, teaching, and popular apologetics | Makes the tradition palatable | Is exactly the "literature NOT discussed because its nature is contradicting and off-putting" pattern. Intellectually dishonest by selective omission |

**Important observation for the framework:** the first response (literal acceptance) is rare in modern mainstream adherence but common in historical practice (the Crusades, conquista-theology, slaveholding Christianity, contemporary radical movements). The other four responses all, in different ways, concede that *something outside the text* is determining what to take seriously. Once that concession is made, the DCT structure has collapsed — the text is no longer the authoritative command source; some other criterion is doing the adjudicating work.

This is exactly the structural finding the refined critique predicted: DCT as coherent framework requires either (1) accepting commanded atrocity as genuinely good, or (2) conceding that something outside the command is determining moral status. Most living traditions choose (2) in practice while claiming (1) in rhetoric.

## 2. The Abraham-Isaac structure — compliance as supreme virtue

The binding of Isaac (the *Akedah* in Hebrew) is the single most structurally-important passage for DCT because it is the moment where the text most explicitly stages compliance-versus-character as a test and resolves it in favor of compliance.

### Genesis 22:1–14 (VERIFIED)

> **22:1** After these things God tested Abraham. He said to him, 'Abraham!' And he said, 'Here I am.'
>
> **22:2** 'Take your son, your only son Isaac, whom you love, and go to the land of Moriah and offer him there as a burnt offering on one of the mountains that I shall show you.'
>
> **22:3** So Abraham rose early in the morning, saddled his donkey, and took two of his young men with him and his son Isaac; he cut the wood for the burnt offering and set out and went to the place in the distance that God had shown him.
>
> **22:4** On the third day Abraham looked up and saw the place far away.
>
> **22:5** Then Abraham said to his young men, 'Stay here with the donkey; the boy and I will go over there; we will worship, and then we will come back to you.'
>
> **22:6** Abraham took the wood of the burnt offering and laid it on his son Isaac, and he himself carried the fire and the knife. And the two of them walked on together.
>
> **22:7** Isaac said to his father Abraham, 'Father!' And he said, 'Here I am, my son.' He said, 'The fire and the wood are here, but where is the lamb for a burnt offering?'
>
> **22:8** Abraham said, 'God himself will provide the lamb for a burnt offering, my son.' And the two of them walked on together.
>
> **22:9** When they came to the place that God had shown him, Abraham built an altar there and laid the wood in order. He bound his son Isaac and laid him on the altar on top of the wood.
>
> **22:10** Then Abraham reached out his hand and took the knife to kill his son.
>
> **22:11** But the angel of the Lord called to him from heaven and said, 'Abraham, Abraham!' And he said, 'Here I am.'
>
> **22:12** 'Do not lay your hand on the boy or do anything to him, for now I know that you fear God, since you have not withheld your son, your only son, from me.'

### What the text actually says and does not say

**What the text does say:**
- God issues the command (v. 2)
- Abraham does not question, protest, or ask for justification
- Abraham rises "early in the morning" — the text emphasizes the promptness of his compliance
- Abraham takes the knife and "reached out his hand" — the text brings him to the moment of killing his son
- The angel stops him with the explicit approval: "now I know that you fear God, since you have not withheld your son"
- The approval criterion is *not-withholding* (compliance), not any other feature of Abraham's character

**What the text does not say:**
- It does not describe Abraham protesting
- It does not describe Abraham arguing with God
- It does not describe Abraham appealing to any standard outside God's command
- It does not describe God explaining the reason
- It does not say Abraham was right to be willing — it says he "fears God," which in biblical Hebrew means acknowledges God's authority

**What the text positions as praiseworthy:** the willingness to kill the son on command. This is explicit. The angel's speech in v. 12 names exactly this as the reason the test has been passed.

### This is character-over-compliance inverted

In the framework, character-over-compliance means that when an authority commands something and character refuses, character is the virtue. The Akedah's structure is the precise opposite: when God commands something and Abraham's character (presumably) would refuse — *kill your son, whom you love* — Abraham's compliance over his character is praised. The text is not ambivalent here. The angel's speech is unambiguous.

### Interpretive-tradition responses (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE)

The Akedah has been handled across traditions:

- **Kierkegaard's *Fear and Trembling* (1843):** famously argues that Abraham's action requires a "teleological suspension of the ethical" — a pause of ordinary moral reasoning to follow a higher command. Kierkegaard honestly admits this creates a knight-of-faith who cannot be understood by ethics, only by faith. This is the most rigorous version of the DCT defense: it says yes, this does require suspending ethics for command-obedience, and that's what faith is.

- **Rabbinic midrash:** generally reframes the test as a test of Abraham *failing* in one sense — e.g., the midrash that Satan tries to convince Abraham not to go, and Abraham's passing the test is sometimes said to have cost him: Sarah's death in Genesis 23 (immediately following the Akedah) is sometimes linked to the trauma of hearing what Abraham almost did. This is a tradition reading against the plain-text valorization.

- **Christian typological:** reads the near-sacrifice of Isaac as a prefiguration of the Crucifixion — God the Father actually giving up his son Jesus, where Abraham was only asked to be willing. This preserves the Akedah's status as praiseworthy while moving its meaning to a symbolic register.

- **Modern liberal Christian / liberal Jewish:** often treats the Akedah as a polemic *against* child sacrifice — the story's actual ending (God stops the sacrifice) is read as a founding moment where the Israelite tradition definitively rejects the child-sacrifice practices of surrounding cultures. This reads against what the angel's speech says but preserves modern moral intuition.

- **Atheist / secular critique** (Richard Dawkins, Peter Singer, others): the story is exactly what it appears to be — a moral horror told as a test passed. No amount of allegorizing can undo the fact that the text praises willingness to kill one's child on command.

**The framework's reading:** the Akedah is the primary-text articulation of DCT as *compliance-maximal*. Whatever else it does, it stages the scenario where command conflicts with natural character and resolves the conflict in compliance's favor. The interpretive moves that soften this are all externally-imposed — they come from outside the plain text, from later tradition's discomfort with what the text actually says. This is one more data point for the finding: living traditions don't actually practice DCT consistently; they practice something else while citing DCT as their authority.

## 3. The Euthyphro move inside the text itself

Here is where the Bible becomes self-contradictory in a way load-bearing for the critique: the same text that commands Abraham to unquestioningly slay Isaac in Genesis 22 shows Abraham, four chapters earlier, *holding God to a standard independent of God's command.*

### Genesis 18:20–33 (VERIFIED)

> **20** How great is the outcry against Sodom and Gomorrah and how very grave their sin!
>
> **21** I must go down and see whether they have done altogether according to the outcry that has come to me, and if not, I will know.
>
> **22** So the men turned from there and went toward Sodom, while Abraham remained standing before the Lord.
>
> **23** Then Abraham came near and said, 'Will you indeed sweep away the righteous with the wicked?
>
> **24** Suppose there are fifty righteous within the city; will you then sweep away the place and not forgive it for the fifty righteous who are in it?
>
> **25** Far be it from you to do such a thing, to slay the righteous with the wicked, so that the righteous fare as the wicked! Far be that from you! Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?'

Read verse 25 carefully. Abraham is:

1. Telling God that a contemplated action would be wrong
2. Using language of moral outrage ("Far be it from you!" — twice)
3. Appealing to a standard of justice that applies *to God*
4. The standard is named — "what is just" — and treated as obligatory for God

This is the Euthyphro structure explicit in the text itself. The question "is good good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good?" is answered here *by Abraham, to God*, in favor of the second option. Abraham holds God to independent justice-standards. He then negotiates God down from fifty to ten righteous people as the threshold for sparing the city (vv. 26–32).

### The contradiction with Genesis 22

The same Abraham who argues with God for the righteous in Sodom (ch. 18) makes no argument for Isaac in ch. 22. This is a live internal contradiction in the text. Either:

- The text presents two different moral frameworks in conflict (Abraham-as-moral-negotiator vs. Abraham-as-unquestioning-complier), and the reader must choose which is authoritative
- Abraham's silence in ch. 22 is itself a moral failure (a minority-rabbinic-tradition reading)
- The ch. 18 negotiation is read as itself a lesser form of faith that ch. 22 surpasses (Kierkegaard's knight-of-faith over knight-of-infinite-resignation)
- The text is simply inconsistent because it is composite, edited from multiple sources with different theological agendas (historical-critical reading)

**For the DCT structural critique, the internal contradiction is the finding.** The text contains both:

- A **character-over-compliance** move: Abraham appeals to justice as a standard independent of and binding on God (Genesis 18:25)
- A **compliance-over-character** move: Abraham complies with a command that violates natural paternal love without protest, and the text praises this (Genesis 22)

Because the same text contains both moves and explicitly approves both, the text cannot coherently ground DCT. Either (1) the Euthyphro structure is correct (good is good independent of command; God is held to it) and then DCT is wrong on the text's own testimony; or (2) compliance-over-character is correct (good is whatever God commands) and then Abraham's Sodom negotiation was impermissible presumption. The text doesn't resolve this; it simply presents both as praiseworthy.

### What this does to the DCT defense

Any defender of DCT who cites the Bible has to handle this contradiction. Standard moves:

- **Privilege Genesis 22 over 18:** this is the dominant interpretive move in traditions that emphasize unquestioning faith. But it requires explaining why Abraham's ch. 18 negotiation was acceptable, since the text presents it as successful and praiseworthy.
- **Privilege Genesis 18 over 22:** this is the liberal-reform move. But it requires treating the Akedah's angel-speech ("now I know that you fear God, since you have not withheld your son") as somehow not actually endorsing what it endorses.
- **Hold both in productive tension:** this is theologically sophisticated but concedes that DCT is not derivable from the text alone; some other adjudication principle is doing the work.
- **Ignore the contradiction:** this is the pastoral-practice move. It is the pattern of "literature that is NOT discussed because its nature and context is contradicting and off-putting."

## 4. Love-your-neighbor — scope and contestation

### Leviticus 19:18 (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE citation — not fetched)

The foundational commandment: *"You shall love your neighbor as yourself."* In context (Leviticus 19), the neighbor being referenced is the fellow Israelite. The chapter's surrounding commands deal with inter-Israelite relations.

### Leviticus 19:34 (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE)

A few verses later, the scope is explicitly extended: *"The alien who resides with you shall be to you as the citizen among you; you shall love the alien as yourself, for you were aliens in the land of Egypt."*

This extension is significant because it appears in the same chapter that issued the in-group command. The same book that limits slavery-protection to fellow Israelites (Lev. 25:44–46) extends love-as-yourself to the resident alien. This is internal tension within a single book.

### Luke 10:25–37 — the Good Samaritan (VERIFIED)

> 29 But wanting to vindicate himself, he asked Jesus, 'And who is my neighbor?' 30 Jesus replied, 'A man was going down from Jerusalem to Jericho and fell into the hands of robbers, who stripped him, beat him, and took off, leaving him half dead. 31 Now by chance a priest was going down that road, and when he saw him he passed by on the other side. 32 So likewise a Levite, when he came to the place and saw him, passed by on the other side. 33 But a Samaritan while traveling came upon him, and when he saw him he was moved with compassion. 34 He went to him and bandaged his wounds, treating them with oil and wine. Then he put him on his own animal, brought him to an inn, and took care of him. 35 The next day he took out two denarii, gave them to the innkeeper, and said, "Take care of him, and when I come back I will repay you whatever more you spend." 36 Which of these three, do you think, was a neighbor to the man who fell into the hands of the robbers?' 37 He said, 'The one who showed him mercy.' Jesus said to him, 'Go and do likewise.'

The significance here is structural. The questioner (an expert in the law) asks *who* counts as neighbor. Jesus does not answer the question directly. He tells a parable in which:

1. The religious insiders (priest, Levite) fail the moral test
2. The cultural enemy (Samaritan — Samaritans and Jews had long-standing religious and ethnic hostility at this point) passes it
3. Jesus inverts the question from "who is my neighbor?" to "who was a neighbor to this man?"

The move is an expansion of the circle (the cultural enemy is the moral exemplar) combined with a reframing (neighbor-hood is not a category of *other people* to be included or excluded, but a relational property of the one doing the loving). This is one of the text's clearest expansion moves.

### Matthew 25:31–46 — the Sheep and the Goats (VERIFIED)

> 34 Then the king will say to those at his right hand, 'Come, you who are blessed by my Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you from the foundation of the world, 35 for I was hungry and you gave me food, I was thirsty and you gave me something to drink, I was a stranger and you welcomed me, 36 I was naked and you gave me clothing, I was sick and you took care of me, I was in prison and you visited me.' [...] 40 And the king will answer them, 'Truly I tell you, just as you did it to one of the least of these brothers and sisters of mine, you did it to me.' [...] 46 And these will go away into eternal punishment but the righteous into eternal life.

This passage does three things important for the scope question:

1. **Expansion:** treatment of the hungry, thirsty, stranger, naked, sick, and imprisoned is the moral criterion. The categories are not restricted to in-group members. The "least of these" includes the most vulnerable across any human category.

2. **Relocation of moral status:** the categories (hungry, naked, imprisoned) are not defined by merit, belief, or group membership — they are defined by vulnerability. The moral demand is to treat the vulnerable well regardless of other categorization.

3. **Eternal-punishment enforcement:** the expansion is paired with threat — failure to treat the vulnerable well is met with "eternal punishment" and "eternal fire prepared for the devil and his angels." This is the expansion-move delivered via the threat-of-damnation enforcement apparatus.

The enforcement pairing is worth noting for the DCT structural critique. The expansion is ethically admirable; the enforcement mechanism is the threat of eternal torture. An ethical expansion requiring divine threat to enforce is structurally different from an ethical expansion derivable from secular reason. The outcome can be praised; the mechanism has the DCT problem built in.

**Reading the passage straight:** the text offers moral expansion backed by threat of eternal torture. Both features are present. Traditions that cite Matthew 25 to celebrate Christian social ethics usually cite vv. 35–40 and elide vv. 41–46. This is another instance of the selective-omission pattern.

## 5. Expansion and contraction in the Pauline / deutero-Pauline corpus

### Galatians 3:26–29 — radical universalism (VERIFIED)

> 28 There is no longer Jew or Greek; there is no longer slave or free; there is no longer male and female, for all of you are one in Christ Jesus.

This is the most expansive passage in the Pauline corpus. It nullifies three major social divisions of the ancient world:

- **Ethnic** (Jew / Greek)
- **Economic / juridical** (slave / free)
- **Sex / gender** (male and female)

Read plainly, Galatians 3:28 is a radical social-equalitarian statement, at least within the community "in Christ Jesus." The question then is: what does the rest of the Pauline / deutero-Pauline literature do with this principle in practice?

### 1 Timothy 2:11–15 — women's subordination (VERIFIED)

> **11** Let a woman learn in silence with full submission.
>
> **12** I do not permit a woman to teach or to have authority over a man; she is to keep silent.
>
> **13** For Adam was formed first, then Eve,
>
> **14** and Adam was not deceived, but the woman was deceived and became a transgressor.
>
> **15** Yet she will be saved through childbearing, provided they continue in faith and love and holiness, with self-control.

Read alongside Galatians 3:28, this is an immediate and total contraction. The Galatians expansion ("no longer male and female, for all of you are one in Christ Jesus") is reversed in 1 Timothy via:

- Silence and submission commanded
- Teaching and authority prohibited for women
- Hierarchy justified from the Genesis 2 creation order and Genesis 3 fall narrative
- Childbearing positioned as the salvific role for women

### The authorship question (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE)

Historical-critical scholarship has reached broad consensus that 1 Timothy is **pseudo-Pauline** — written after Paul's death in his name. The undisputed Pauline letters (generally: Romans, 1 and 2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, Philemon) contain more expansionist and less hierarchically-structured material. The "Pastoral Epistles" (1 Timothy, 2 Timothy, Titus) contain more church-order and hierarchy material and differ in vocabulary, style, and theology from the undisputed letters.

**For the DCT critique, this matters:** the expansion-contraction pattern that is often framed as "Paul contradicts himself" is more accurately "early-Christian communities contained both expansionist and hierarchy-preserving voices, and both voices made it into the canon, and later readers have been able to cite either for support." The text is not a single authoritative moral source; it is an edited anthology of voices in disagreement.

### The slave / free question

Galatians 3:28 nullifies slave / free *in Christ* — but the letter to Philemon (undisputed Pauline) is Paul returning a runaway slave (Onesimus) to his owner, requesting Philemon receive Onesimus as a brother but not demanding his manumission. The Pauline material is ambivalent on whether the "no longer slave or free" applies to actual social structures or only to spiritual standing. Later deutero-Pauline material (Colossians 3:22, Ephesians 6:5, 1 Peter 2:18) contains instructions for slaves to obey masters. The same corpus that declares equality in Christ also preserves the master-slave hierarchy in practice.

**This is the textual root of the fact that Christianity could be cited for both abolitionism and slaveholding during the American slavery debate.** Abolitionists cited Galatians 3:28, the prophetic tradition, the image-of-God passages. Slaveholders cited the household codes, the instructions for slaves to obey, and the lack of any direct Pauline call to abolish slavery. Both sides had scripture. The expansion and contraction are both primary-source features.

## 6. What this primary-source pass establishes

### For the refined DCT critique (atrocity-permit thesis)

**The critique holds on the primary text.** The biblical text contains explicit atrocity-commands (1 Samuel 15, Deuteronomy 7, Joshua 6, Numbers 31, Deuteronomy 13, Psalm 137:9, Leviticus 25). These commands are in the authoritative source. They are not marginal. Religious traditions that hold the text as divine command have to handle these passages somehow, and all the major handling-strategies either (a) accept the commands as genuinely good (rare in modern mainstream adherence), (b) concede that something outside the text determines which commands to take seriously, or (c) selectively omit the passages from pastoral teaching.

Response (a) bites the bullet; (b) concedes the critique; (c) is the pattern of literature not discussed because it is off-putting.

**The distinctive contribution of DCT is the license for harm.** The pro-social commands (love neighbor, love alien, care for the vulnerable, the Good Samaritan pattern, Matthew 25's ethics of vulnerability, Galatians 3:28's radical equality) are all derivable without DCT — they are recognizable as good on secular reflection. The atrocity-commands (genocidal ḥērem, permanent chattel slavery, Abraham-Isaac compliance-maximalism) are *specifically* requiring DCT to be licensed. Without "God commanded it," these become indefensible; with "God commanded it," they become not only defensible but praiseworthy. This is exactly the structural position we predicted.

### For the internal-contradiction point

**The text contradicts itself on the Euthyphro question.** Genesis 18:25 shows Abraham holding God to a standard of justice; Genesis 22 shows Abraham complying without appeal to any standard. Both are in the same text, about the same person, within four chapters. Any coherent theological reading has to privilege one over the other, which means some principle *outside the text itself* is doing the adjudication.

### For the expansion/contraction pattern

**Both moves are primary-source features.** The Good Samaritan (Luke 10), Matthew 25's ethics of vulnerability, Galatians 3:28's universalism — these are all in the text and support expansion. Deuteronomy 7's ethnic exclusion, Leviticus 25's chattel-slavery legislation, 1 Timothy's gender hierarchy — these are also in the text and support contraction. The historical record of Christianity cycling through expansion and contraction is not accidental; it is downstream of the primary text containing both.

### For the character-over-compliance thesis

**The Akedah is the primary-text inversion of the framework.** Where the framework says character-over-compliance, Genesis 22 says compliance-over-character and the text explicitly approves. This is honest to name. Living traditions do not actually practice the Akedah's compliance-maximalism — most Jews, Christians, and Muslims would not actually kill their sons on an audible command — but the text sacralizes the willingness to.

**[2026-04-19 correction — empirical field-data update.]** The sentence above about "living traditions do not actually practice" is too comfortable and requires correction based on primary-source field-data: ~10+ direct conversations with Christian believers in which the question was posed and pressed through argumentation. The pattern that emerged:

1. Initial abstract response: visible distress, denial — *"God wouldn't ask that."*
2. Cite Genesis 22 and comparable atrocity-commands.
3. Retreat to historical distancing — *"that was then; God doesn't do that anymore."*
4. Rephrase: *"If it happened tomorrow, exactly as it happened then, would you comply?"*
5. Responses bifurcate. Some cave — *"I probably would"* — often with a shrug at the interviewer's horror. Others refuse the question with visible distress but cannot maintain coherent denial, typically citing fear of eternal damnation as the enforcement mechanism that makes the compliance non-negotiable. Few sustain coherent refusal.

The honest claim is therefore not that the Akedah's compliance-maximalism is a historical artifact. The correct claim is: **living traditions socially sanitize the Akedah under normal conditions, but the compliance-maximalism remains operatively available and activates under sufficient framing pressure, with eternal damnation functioning as the enforcement backstop.** This is a stronger version of the framework's critique, not a weaker one — the structural feature is operative in 2026 conversations, not confined to historical-text analysis.

**Cherry-picking as load-bearing epistemology.** A further correction follows from the same field-data. The atrocity-content is not confined to the Akedah. Every topic a believer normally finds "immoral," "inhumane," or "dehumanizing" — slavery codes, *ḥērem* genocidal commands, gender hierarchy, capital punishment for religious offense, the curse of Ham, Joshua's conquests — produces the same compliance-versus-character pressure when pressed through comparable argumentation. The believer cannot simultaneously affirm (a) God commands the good, (b) God commanded all these things, and (c) these things are not good, without the scaffolding breaking.

**Cherry-picking — selectively emphasizing passages that align with modern moral intuition and selectively omitting or allegorizing those that don't — is the operative workaround that keeps DCT functional in practice for most believers in most denominations.** It is not a peripheral coping behavior. It is the load-bearing epistemology.

This has a direct implication for the framework's critique: **the coherent critique of DCT must address the cherry-picking mechanism directly, not the Akedah in isolation.** The Akedah is one pressure-point among many; the general structure is that DCT-adherents operate a silent external adjudication process (cherry-picking) while asserting that the text itself is their source of moral authority. The adjudication is being done by some principle *outside* the text — exactly the Euthyphro conclusion the framework already defends — but the cherry-picking hides this fact from the adherent, including from themselves. Cherry-picking is therefore both what makes DCT liveable and what disqualifies it as an actual source of moral authority.

**Genesis 18 is where the framework has its textual ally.** Abraham's negotiation with God is a character-over-compliance move: *Far be it from you to do such a thing. Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?* An ethics grounded in Genesis 18 rather than Genesis 22 is compatible with the framework; an ethics grounded in Genesis 22 is not. The text supports either reading.

## 7. What this primary-source pass does not yet establish

To be honest about limits:

1. **Hebrew and Greek originals.** All citations are from an English translation, however scholarly. The Hebrew *ḥērem* and its precise connotations, the Greek μή (*mē*, the negative in 1 Timothy 2:12), nuances of Pauline vocabulary — these have been handled here through the NRSVue's interpretive choices. A deeper pass would engage the original-language texts.

2. **Historical context.** The atrocity-commands have been read (historically-critically) as reflecting specific Ancient Near Eastern contexts — inter-city warfare, resource competition, boundary-formation for emerging Israelite identity. This contextual reading doesn't make the commands acceptable by modern standards, but it does complicate whether they should be read as timeless divine mandates versus context-bound human compositions with divine attribution. This distinction matters for apologetics but does not rescue DCT — if the commands are context-bound human compositions, the DCT framework dissolves anyway.

3. **The rest of the canon.** This pass covers key passages but leaves much untouched — the prophetic tradition (Amos, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Hosea, Micah) contains both scathing critiques of in-group religious hypocrisy (expansion move) and specific ethnic-violence pronouncements (contraction move). Wisdom literature (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Job) has a different theological register — Job in particular is a sustained challenge to simple command-obedience theology. Revelation has its own issues. Each of these could receive its own primary-source pass.

4. **Interpretive-tradition handling.** GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE references to Kierkegaard, rabbinic midrash, patristic allegory, historical-critical scholarship, modern apologetics — none have been primary-source verified. A deeper pass would engage specific texts: Aquinas on natural law and the *ḥērem*; Origen on allegorizing violent passages; modern apologetic attempts (William Lane Craig's *Holy War and the Conquest of Canaan*); critics (Thom Stark's *Is God a Moral Monster?*; the philosophical literature on divine command from Plato's Euthyphro through Robert Adams).

5. **Other canons.** The Christian Bible is one text; the Hebrew Bible / Tanakh (overlapping but with different book ordering and inclusion), the Quran, the Book of Mormon, the Bhagavad Gita, each has its own primary-source reading ahead. The structural critique may apply differently to different canons.

## 8. Connection to the framework

**This primary-source pass confirms the refined DCT critique.** The earlier synthesis-level claim was: DCT is structurally an atrocity-permit whose distinctive contribution is the license for harm, not the goodness. The primary-source pass finds:

- Atrocity-commands present in the text, in the authoritative voice, not at the margins
- The Akedah as explicit compliance-over-character endorsement
- The Sodom negotiation as character-over-compliance alternative within the same text
- The text contradicting itself on the Euthyphro question
- Expansion and contraction moves both primary-source features
- The pro-social ethics derivable secularly; the atrocity-commands specifically requiring the framework

**For the character-over-compliance thesis, the pass gives us:**

- Genesis 18:25 as textual ally ("Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?" — Abraham holding God to an independent standard of justice)
- Luke 10's Good Samaritan as expansion-move ally (the cultural enemy as moral exemplar)
- Matthew 25's vulnerability-ethics as expansion ally (the least of these, regardless of group)
- Galatians 3:28 as universalist ally (one in Christ across ethnic, juridical, and gender divides)

**And gives us, honestly, what we have to account for:**

- Genesis 22 as the canonical compliance-over-character endorsement
- The ḥērem commands as sacralized atrocity
- Leviticus 25 as permanent-slavery legislation
- 1 Timothy 2 as textual warrant for ongoing gender hierarchy
- The pattern of expansion-followed-by-contraction, both licensed by the same canon

**Methodological implication:** A secular character-over-compliance ethics can cite Genesis 18 and the Good Samaritan and Matthew 25 honestly. It cannot cite these passages as *authoritative* without also having to cite Genesis 22, Leviticus 25, and 1 Timothy 2 — the same text licenses both. The coherent position is: these passages illuminate moves that secular reason also recognizes as good; the authority of "the text commands it" is not doing ethical work that secular reason can't do without it; the text is a useful partial-mirror of human moral reasoning in progress, not a source of moral authority independent of reason.

The coherent position is that the positive program is democratized decision on what constitutes good, grounded in reasons that can be examined, not in authority-claims that disable the standing from which critique can be made.

## Closing

The primary-source pass is consistent with what the refined DCT critique predicted: the text contains what it contains — atrocity-commands *and* expansion-commands, compliance-endorsement *and* Euthyphro-structure, a source that traditions can and do cite for opposite ethical positions. The authority of "God commanded it" is not doing ethical work that secular reason cannot do without it. What the framework uniquely licenses is the harm-commands; the goodness is derivable otherwise.

A serious ethics grounded in primary-source engagement cannot conclude that the Bible is morally worthless — it contains passages (Genesis 18:25, Luke 10, Matthew 25, Galatians 3:28) that illuminate moves secular reason endorses. But it also cannot conclude that the Bible is a reliable moral authority — the same canon licenses genocide, slavery, and gender subordination, and the only way to separate the good passages from the harmful ones is by applying a moral standard *outside the text* to select which to treat as authoritative. Once that outside-standard is in play, the text has become a moral anthology to be engaged critically, not a moral authority to be obeyed.

This is the coherent position. It is consistent with what honest reading of the primary text actually shows: the text is manmade and inconsistent, and its contradictions are visible from direct engagement. The contribution of this document is to put that conclusion on explicit primary-source footing with exact citations, so it can be defended against apologetic pushback and extended to comparative work.

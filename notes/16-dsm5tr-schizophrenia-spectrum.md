# DSM-5-TR Schizophrenia Spectrum and Other Psychotic Disorders — Research for Test Cases

**Date:** 2026-04-30
**Scope:** Research document. Pulls DSM-5-TR (2022) content on Schizophrenia Spectrum and Other Psychotic Disorders for the framework's "perception-of-reality differs" test cases. The framework's claim that moral character can be cultivated, observed, and respected must hold even where the person's perceptual or interpretive engagement with consensus reality differs significantly. Psychotic-spectrum disorders are the hardest case for any framework that assumes shared epistemic ground.

**Why this matters:** The framework must not collapse into either (a) treating these persons as outside moral agency, or (b) ignoring the lived reality of altered perception. DSM-5-TR's own structure — spectrum rather than discrete disease, dimensional rather than subtyped, culture-sensitive rather than universalist — actually supports the framework's character-respecting stance better than the older categorical model did. This document extracts what DSM-5-TR says, where it has shifted, and what it conspicuously does not claim about insight, capacity, or moral agency.

## 1. Structure of the spectrum (DSM-5-TR Chapter)

The DSM-5-TR opens the chapter with this framing (VERIFIED, verbatim, p. 101):

> "Schizophrenia spectrum and other psychotic disorders include schizophrenia, other psychotic disorders, and schizotypal (personality) disorder. They are defined by abnormalities in one or more of the following five domains: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized thinking (speech), grossly disorganized or abnormal motor behavior (including catatonia), and negative symptoms." (DSM-5-TR, p. 101)

The chapter's organizing principle is stated explicitly under "Disorders in This Chapter" (VERIFIED, verbatim, p. 103):

> "This chapter is organized along a gradient of psychopathology. Clinicians should first consider conditions that do not reach full criteria for a psychotic disorder or are limited to one domain of psychopathology. Then they should consider time-limited conditions. Finally, the diagnosis of a schizophrenia spectrum disorder requires the exclusion of another condition that may give rise to psychosis." (DSM-5-TR, p. 103)

Diagnoses included in (or cross-listed with) the chapter:

| Diagnosis | Core feature | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Schizotypal (Personality) Disorder | Sub-threshold perceptual/belief/affective abnormalities; cross-listed from Personality Disorders chapter | Stable trait |
| Delusional Disorder | One or more delusions | ≥ 1 month |
| Brief Psychotic Disorder | Sudden onset psychotic symptoms, full return to premorbid function | < 1 month |
| Schizophreniform Disorder | Criterion A met, but sub-six-month course | 1 to < 6 months |
| Schizophrenia | Criterion A met with full duration including prodromal/residual | ≥ 6 months total, ≥ 1 month active |
| Schizoaffective Disorder | Schizophrenia criteria + mood episode concurrent, with ≥ 2 weeks of psychosis without mood symptoms | Longitudinal assessment |
| Substance/Medication-Induced Psychotic Disorder | Psychotic symptoms attributable to substance | Variable |
| Psychotic Disorder Due to Another Medical Condition | Psychotic symptoms attributable to medical etiology | Variable |
| Catatonia (associated with another mental disorder; due to medical condition; unspecified) | Motor immobility, stupor, mutism, posturing, mannerisms, etc. | Variable |
| Other Specified / Unspecified Schizophrenia Spectrum and Other Psychotic Disorder | Residual categories | Variable |

The ordering is deliberate: it moves from sub-threshold (schizotypal) through brief and intermediate-duration diagnoses to schizophrenia and its longitudinal/etiologic variants. This is itself a spectrum-concept commitment. (VERIFIED for structure; cross-confirmed across the APA Table of Contents PDF, Focus 2016 "Structure of the Psychotic Disorders Classification in DSM-5," and StatPearls.)

## 2. Criterion A — the five symptoms

For a schizophrenia diagnosis, Criterion A requires two (or more) of the following, each present for a significant portion of time during a 1-month period (or less if successfully treated), with at least one being from the first three:

1. **Delusions** — fixed beliefs that are not amenable to change in light of conflicting evidence (DSM-5 redefinition; DSM-IV-TR had said "erroneous beliefs")
2. **Hallucinations**
3. **Disorganized speech** (e.g., frequent derailment or incoherence)
4. **Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior**
5. **Negative symptoms** (e.g., diminished emotional expression, avolition)

(VERIFIED across NCBI Bookshelf Table 3.22, the APA "Highlights of Changes" PDF, and PMC4393695.)

### 2.1 Criterion A — verbatim (VERIFIED, DSM-5-TR p. 113)

> "A. Two (or more) of the following, each present for a significant portion of time during a 1-month period (or less if successfully treated). At least one of these must be (1), (2), or (3):
>
> 1. Delusions.
> 2. Hallucinations.
> 3. Disorganized speech (e.g., frequent derailment or incoherence).
> 4. Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior.
> 5. Negative symptoms (i.e., diminished emotional expression or avolition)."

### 2.2 Criteria B and C — verbatim (VERIFIED, DSM-5-TR p. 113)

> "B. For a significant portion of the time since the onset of the disturbance, level of functioning in one or more major areas, such as work, interpersonal relations, or self-care, is markedly below the level achieved prior to the onset (or when the onset is in childhood or adolescence, there is failure to achieve expected level of interpersonal, academic, or occupational functioning).
>
> C. Continuous signs of the disturbance persist for at least 6 months. This 6-month period must include at least 1 month of symptoms (or less if successfully treated) that meet Criterion A (i.e., active-phase symptoms) and may include periods of prodromal or residual symptoms. During these prodromal or residual periods, the signs of the disturbance may be manifested by only negative symptoms or by two or more symptoms listed in Criterion A present in an attenuated form (e.g., odd beliefs, unusual perceptual experiences)."

### 2.3 Criteria D, E, F — verbatim (VERIFIED, DSM-5-TR p. 113–114)

> "D. Schizoaffective disorder and depressive or bipolar disorder with psychotic features have been ruled out because either 1) no major depressive or manic episodes have occurred concurrently with the active-phase symptoms, or 2) if mood episodes have occurred during active-phase symptoms, they have been present for a minority of the total duration of the active and residual periods of the illness.
>
> E. The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug of abuse, a medication) or another medical condition.
>
> F. If there is a history of autism spectrum disorder or a communication disorder of childhood onset, the additional diagnosis of schizophrenia is made only if prominent delusions or hallucinations, in addition to the other required symptoms of schizophrenia, are also present for at least 1 month (or less if successfully treated)."

The redefinition of delusions as "fixed beliefs not amenable to change in light of conflicting evidence" — rather than "erroneous beliefs" — is load-bearing for the framework. It moves the diagnostic locus from the *truth-content* of the belief to the *epistemic responsiveness* of the believer. A belief held with conviction that updates in response to evidence is not a delusion under DSM-5-TR even if false; a belief that does not update even given strong contrary evidence is a delusion even if some part of its content is partially true. This matters for the framework because it means DSM-5-TR is not, on its face, claiming that minority epistemic communities are "delusional."

## 3. The Criterion A change from DSM-IV: bizarre delusions no longer sufficient alone

**Old DSM-IV rule:** A single Criterion A symptom was sufficient if it was a "bizarre delusion" or a Schneiderian first-rank auditory hallucination (voices conversing with each other, or a running commentary).

**New DSM-5 rule:** Two Criterion A symptoms are *always* required, and at least one must be from the first three (delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech).

**Reasons for the change** (peer-reviewed summary): the bizarre/non-bizarre distinction was unreliable; Schneiderian first-rank symptoms were non-specific (they appear in many other conditions); the special-attribution rule produced false positives. (VERIFIED — APA "Highlights of Changes" PDF, Psychiatric News 2015 analysis, PMC4393695.)

**Significance for the framework:** the change tightens the diagnostic threshold and explicitly rejects the prior implicit claim that some kinds of beliefs are *so bizarre* they are alone sufficient evidence of disorder. This is a small but real concession to the principle that content alone is not pathology.

## 4. Subtypes eliminated

DSM-IV had five schizophrenia subtypes: paranoid, disorganized, catatonic, undifferentiated, and residual. DSM-5 eliminated all of them. (VERIFIED — APA Highlights, NCBI Bookshelf Table 3.22, PMC4393695.)

**Stated reasons:**
- Limited diagnostic stability over time (a "paranoid" patient often presented as "disorganized" later)
- Low inter-rater reliability
- Poor predictive validity for treatment response
- Poor predictive validity for longitudinal course
- No distinct treatment indications

**What replaced them:** a *dimensional approach* using the Clinician-Rated Dimensions of Psychosis Symptom Severity (C-RDPSS), placed in DSM-5 Section III. Symptom dimensions: reality distortion (delusions, hallucinations), negative symptoms, disorganization, cognitive impairment, motor symptoms (including catatonia), and mood symptoms (depression, mania).

**Significance for the framework:** the move from discrete subtypes to dimensions is the same epistemic move the framework prefers in other domains — accumulate, don't replace; multi-criteria not single-label; person not category. DSM-5-TR moved partway toward this on its own initiative, for reasons of validity rather than ideology. The framework should note this as an example of mainstream psychiatric science independently arriving at a structurally similar position.

## 5. Cultural considerations — what DSM-5-TR explicitly addresses

The DSM-5-TR (2022) added a new Section III chapter, "Culture and Psychiatric Diagnosis" (approximately pp. 859-879 — page numbers confirmed only at the summary level via PsychCentral and Psychiatric Times secondary sources, GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE on exact pagination), and updated "Culture-Related Diagnostic Features" sections throughout. The Cultural Formulation Interview (CFI), introduced in DSM-5, is retained.

**Key culture-related claims relevant to the spectrum** (drawn from peer-reviewed summaries — Jarvis 2025 in *Transcultural Psychiatry*; Psychiatric Times CFI overview; Psychiatrist.com religious/spiritual research agenda; New Harbinger spiritual emergency article — VERIFIED at the level of consensus across multiple sources):

- Hallucinations may be a normal part of religious experience in many cultural contexts and are not, on their own, evidence of disorder.
- Experiences such as speaking in tongues, possession trance, and certain visionary states involve features that resemble disorganized speech or hallucination but, if culturally sanctioned and contextually bounded, are not psychotic.
- Symptoms should not be coded as criteria-meeting if they "represent a culturally sanctioned response."
- Assessment through interpreters must avoid mistaking unfamiliar metaphors or idioms of distress for delusions.
- The Cultural Formulation Interview is offered as a structured way to elicit the patient's own cultural context, idioms of distress, and explanatory model before assigning a psychotic-spectrum diagnosis.
- DSM-5-TR's Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion Work Group reviewed the entire text to ensure that explanations of ethno-racial and cultural differences in symptomatic presentations and prevalence took into account the impact of racism and discrimination. (VERIFIED — World Psychiatry / PMC9077590.)

**Significance for the framework:** the DSM-5-TR explicitly carves out space for the very phenomenon the framework needs — that the same observed behavior can be moral, spiritual, normative, or pathological depending on context, and that the diagnostician is expected to do the contextual work before assigning a label. This is a substantial concession that "fixed beliefs" and "voice-hearing" are not necessarily disorder. It does not go all the way to the Hearing Voices Movement position (see §8), but it moves measurably in that direction.

## 6. Schizotypal personality and the spectrum-concept

Schizotypal Personality Disorder is formally housed in the Personality Disorders chapter (Cluster A) but is *cross-listed* in the Schizophrenia Spectrum chapter. This is itself an architectural commitment to the spectrum concept: the same underlying liability-to-psychosis dimension manifests at sub-threshold trait levels (schizotypal), threshold delusional content (delusional disorder), and full-syndromal (schizophrenia). (VERIFIED — APA Table of Contents; StatPearls; MSD Manual; Focus 2016.)

DSM-5-TR notes that the course of schizotypal personality disorder is relatively stable — most individuals do not progress to schizophrenia. This is important for the framework because it explicitly resists a deterministic disease-progression narrative. Trait-level perceptual or belief atypicality is recognized as a stable feature of some persons rather than a precursor to inevitable decline.

The spectrum commitment in DSM-5-TR is not full continuity-with-normality (it does not say schizotypy shades into ordinary trait variation), but it is a meaningful step away from the old discrete-disease model.

## 7. DSM-5-TR (2022) language updates relevant to stigma, recovery, and how persons are described

Confirmed updates from the World Psychiatry overview paper (First et al., 2022; PMC9077590) and APA fact sheets (VERIFIED at the source level; specific schizophrenia-chapter language is GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE):

- **Terminology:** "neuroleptic medications" replaced with "antipsychotic medications or other dopamine receptor blocking agents"
- **Cross-cutting review groups:** Culture; Sex and Gender; Suicide; Forensic. Plus a separate **Work Group on Ethnoracial Equity and Inclusion** that reviewed the full manual for non-stigmatizing language and for risk factors like racism and discrimination
- **Updated text sections** throughout: "Culture-Related Diagnostic Features" and "Sex- and Gender-Related Diagnostic Features"
- **New Section III chapter:** "Culture and Psychiatric Diagnosis"

The World Psychiatry overview does *not* document a specific schizophrenia-chapter language overhaul beyond what falls under the cross-cutting reviews. Specifically:

- DSM-5-TR did **not** universally adopt person-first language ("person with schizophrenia") to the exclusion of identity-first ("schizophrenic person"). The manual uses both styles depending on context, and the consumer/survivor community itself is divided on which is preferred. (GENERAL-KNOWLEDGE.)
- DSM-5-TR does **not** endorse the "recovery model" as a clinical framework. It does not contradict it either. The recovery movement and the DSM remain parallel discourses. (See §9.)

## 8. Insight as a specifier — and what DSM-5-TR conspicuously does NOT claim

DSM-5 introduced and DSM-5-TR retains an *insight specifier* applicable to schizophrenia, schizoaffective disorder, delusional disorder, and others:

- **With good or fair insight**
- **With poor insight**
- **With absent insight / delusional beliefs**

(VERIFIED — APA Highlights, Wikipedia DSM-5, Lysaker 2018 World Psychiatry review.)

The specifier is positioned clinically as a treatment-adherence and prognostic factor. Lysaker (2018, *World Psychiatry*) clarifies the distinction between *clinical insight* (awareness of symptoms, need for treatment, psychosocial consequences) and *cognitive insight* (capacity for self-reflection, resistance to over-certainty) — the two are dissociable.

**What DSM-5-TR does NOT say** (this is the load-bearing observation for the framework):

- It does **not** claim that poor insight precludes moral agency.
- It does **not** claim that schizophrenia, as such, removes capacity to be a moral agent.
- It does **not** claim that delusions render the person's other beliefs and commitments untrustworthy.
- It does **not** offer a global capacity judgment based on diagnosis. Capacity is a separate legal-clinical determination, made decision-by-decision and context-by-context, not category-by-category.
- It does **not** describe persons with these diagnoses as *patients-only* or as defined by their disorder.

The diagnostic criteria are descriptive of symptom patterns. They are not metaphysical claims about personhood. This is a structural feature of the DSM tradition that has held since DSM-III, and DSM-5-TR maintains it.

**Significance for the framework:** this is exactly the space the framework needs. The DSM, on its own terms, does not claim that diagnosis settles the moral-agency question. The framework can therefore say — without contradicting psychiatric authority — that character can be cultivated, observed, and respected in persons whose perceptual or interpretive engagement with consensus reality differs, including those with schizophrenia-spectrum diagnoses. The DSM does not block this; it leaves the question open.

## 9. Recovery movement and consumer/survivor critique — the lived-experience counterweight

The framework should not rely on DSM-5-TR alone. Two parallel literatures matter:

**Recovery movement** (VERIFIED — Jacobson & Greenley 2001; Davidson & Roe; PMC2632241 "Scientific and Consumer Models of Recovery in Schizophrenia"; SAMHSA recovery framework documents):

- Long-term outcome studies (Harding, Bleuler, the Vermont and Maine studies) show that ~50% of persons diagnosed with schizophrenia achieve good long-term outcomes — substantially undercutting the older Kraepelinian "dementia praecox" deterioration narrative.
- "Scientific recovery" emphasizes symptom remission and functional return; "consumer recovery" emphasizes living a meaningful life *with or without* full symptom remission.
- The recovery model does not deny biology; it disputes the claim that biology is the only or primary explanatory frame.

**Hearing Voices Movement / Hearing Voices Network** (VERIFIED — PMC7348419, PMC4141309, Nature Reviews Psychology 2022, *Health and Human Rights Journal* 2020):

- International peer-led movement co-founded by Marius Romme and Sandra Escher; Intervoice is the international coordinating body.
- Voice-hearing is reframed as a meaningful human experience that can be understood within a person's biography, culture, trauma history, and relationships, not solely as disease-symptom.
- Most people who report voice-hearing never receive psychiatric services and do not develop diagnosable disorders. Voice-hearing exists in non-clinical populations at significant prevalence.
- Distress is the clinical issue — not voice-hearing as such.
- Critical of the strict biomedical-only frame; not necessarily against medication or psychiatry, but against the assumption that voices are *only* symptoms.

**Psychiatric survivor movement** (VERIFIED — Wikipedia "Psychiatric survivors movement"; theweb.ngo recovery archive):

- Stronger critique. Rejects forced treatment, involuntary commitment, and the assumption of biomedical etiology.
- "Consumer," "survivor," and "ex-patient" are the three self-identifications, with different relationships to the mainstream system.

**Significance for the framework:** the lived-experience literatures show that a substantial population of persons within the psychotic-spectrum diagnostic territory (a) recover or live meaningful lives without full symptom remission, (b) experience their voices/visions as integrated parts of their personhood rather than as foreign symptoms, and (c) actively contest the biomedical-only frame. Any framework that treats DSM-5-TR as the only authoritative description of these persons will fail the lived-experience test. The framework's character-respecting stance is *closer* to the recovery and Hearing Voices positions than to a strict biomedical position, while being compatible with DSM-5-TR's own (carefully bounded) descriptive claims.

## 10. ICD-11 comparison — useful triangulation

The WHO ICD-11 (in force 2022) is the international counterpart. Differences relevant here (VERIFIED — PMC7801846 First et al. 2021 World Psychiatry; Brewin et al.; CNS Spectrums 2016):

- ICD-11 requires **1 month** total duration for schizophrenia (versus DSM-5's 6 months including prodromal/residual). This is a substantial difference; DSM-5's 6-month rule produces fewer diagnoses but more chronic-pattern certainty.
- ICD-11 keeps "experiences of influence, passivity, or control" as a separate core symptom category (thoughts/actions imposed by an outside force).
- ICD-11 diagnoses schizoaffective disorder *cross-sectionally* (current episode); DSM-5 diagnoses it *longitudinally* (full course).
- Both systems eliminated subtypes, both moved to dimensions/specifiers, both retain insight-related specifiers.
- ICD-11 includes a separate qualifier for "primary psychotic symptoms" by domain (positive, negative, depressive, manic, psychomotor, cognitive) — closer to a fully dimensional system than DSM-5's hybrid.

The convergence between DSM-5 and ICD-11 on (a) eliminating subtypes, (b) using dimensions, (c) culture-sensitivity, and (d) not making metaphysical claims about agency is itself evidence that the framework's preferred stance is consistent with mainstream international psychiatric science.

## 11. What this means for the framework

### 11.1 The framework's position is defensible against DSM-5-TR

DSM-5-TR is the single most authoritative source in the policy-relevant context for "what counts as psychotic-spectrum disorder." The framework's claim — that moral character can be cultivated, observed, and respected in these persons — does not contradict DSM-5-TR. The DSM-5-TR is descriptive of symptoms, not prescriptive of personhood. The insight specifier is about treatment and prognosis, not about moral agency. The cultural-considerations section actively warns clinicians against pathologizing what is, in context, normative experience.

### 11.2 The framework should explicitly engage the spectrum concept

The DSM-5-TR's commitment to spectrum (schizotypal trait → delusional disorder → schizophrenia) and to dimensions (reality distortion, negative, disorganization, cognitive, motor, mood) is structurally compatible with the framework's multi-criteria approach. Case studies should treat persons as occupying positions on dimensions rather than membership in discrete categories. This is what DSM-5-TR itself does.

### 11.3 The framework must engage the lived-experience literatures

Relying on DSM-5-TR alone is exactly the failure mode that consumer/survivor and Hearing Voices critiques have been documenting for forty years. Future work must include the recovery movement and Hearing Voices Movement as primary sources, not as critical addenda. This is consistent with the project's existing pattern of multi-source engagement.

### 11.4 Insight, capacity, and moral agency — the core test case

The framework's hardest case is a person with absent-insight delusional beliefs who is acting on those beliefs in ways that affect others. DSM-5-TR is silent on the moral-agency question; insight is a clinical specifier, not a competence judgment. The framework should:

- Not equate poor insight with moral-agency loss.
- Not equate diagnosis with capacity loss.
- Treat capacity as decision-by-decision and context-by-context, consistent with DSM-5-TR's own position.
- Treat character as observable across the diagnostic boundary — kindness, attentiveness, honesty within the person's own epistemic frame, care for others, follow-through on commitments — and respect what is observed.
- Acknowledge that some specific decisions (medical consent, financial decisions, etc.) may require capacity assessment and possibly support; this does not collapse the broader moral-agency claim.

### 11.5 Cultural humility is a structural requirement

Because DSM-5-TR itself warns that the same observed behavior can be normative spiritual experience or pathology depending on context, the framework should treat cultural humility as a structural diagnostic-level requirement, not a courtesy. This is the same posture as the framework's broader cross-tradition primary-source approach.

## 1. Pat Deegan — recovery as lived, not as concept

**Pat Deegan, PhD** (b. ~1954) was diagnosed with schizophrenia as a teenager and hospitalized three times. The diagnostic encounter included what she has called a *"prognosis of doom"* — the message that her life would be defined by lifelong medication dependence and limited prospects. At 18, she made the decision that has shaped everything she's done since: *"I am going to become Dr. Deegan and change the mental health system so no one ever gets hurt in it again."* She earned a PhD in clinical psychology and built her work — academic, clinical, organizational, technological — around that vow.

Her seminal essay, ***"Recovery: The Lived Experience of Rehabilitation"*** (1988, *Psychosocial Rehabilitation Journal* 11(4):11–19), drew the distinction that defined the modern recovery movement. The essay argues:

> Psychiatrically disabled adults do not "get rehabilitated" but rather recover a new and valued sense of self and of purpose. (paraphrased from peer-reviewed citation)

That distinction matters. *Rehabilitation* is something done to a person by professionals operating on a deficit model — restoration of function, reduction of symptoms, return to a baseline. *Recovery*, in Deegan's framing, is something the person does — an active, responsible participation in becoming someone with meaning and purpose, regardless of whether symptoms remit. The activity is the recovery; the symptom-reduction may or may not follow.

This is structurally the same move §3 of the paper makes — the move from compliance/deficit-cataloguing to capacity-side definition. Deegan articulated it thirty-eight years before the paper this paper publishes, in clinical-register form, from inside the experience of being on the deficit-cataloguing side of the encounter. The paper's claim about cross-tradition convergence has direct empirical backing from her work that the paper currently doesn't make visible.

### The "Conspiracy of Hope" speech (1996)

Deegan delivered ***"Recovery and the Conspiracy of Hope"*** as the keynote at the 1996 Mental Health Services conference in Brisbane, Australia. The speech is widely cited as a foundational statement of the recovery movement.

The phrase that gives the speech its title is hers, and it has carried into the movement as a load-bearing line:

> *"We are a conspiracy of hope and we are pressing back against the strong tide of oppression which for centuries has been..."* (the line continues; this is the verifiable opening)

The speech makes several structural moves the paper's framework would recognize:

**Against the "biography written by earlier theorists."** Deegan describes how patients arrived at the clinical encounter with their lives already pre-narrativized — *no one asked for the individuals' stories, instead assuming their biographies as schizophrenics had already been written by earlier theorists*. The clinician's reading of the patient's life was determined before the patient walked into the room. What the patients experienced as *"loss, grief, shock, fear and loneliness"* was being read as symptomatology. (Paraphrased from the speech's documented themes.)

This maps directly onto the framework's diagnosis of the compliance/deficit move (§2): the patient is defined by what the diagnostic apparatus determines they lack relative to an implied norm, and the patient's own self-account is overwritten by the apparatus's pre-existing categories.

**The internalization of hopelessness as the structural harm.** Deegan's documented argument is that the messages of hopelessness and stigma transmitted by psychiatric treatment led individuals to internalize negative beliefs about themselves, *resulting in a dehumanizing transformation from being a person to being an illness*. This is the structural failure the framework's §6.3 disability-ethics validity test names: when the framework identifies a person *with* their deficit-cataloguing rather than as a person *who has* whatever profile they have, the flattening produces real harm.

### Personal Medicine and shared decision-making

Deegan's mid-2000s work centered on the concept she calls **Personal Medicine**: the things a person does, beyond pharmacology, that constitute their healing. Personal Medicine includes whatever the individual finds restorative — relationships, activities, meaningful work, creative practice, physical exercise, spiritual practice — and the framework she developed treats these as central to recovery rather than peripheral.

This move is structurally adjacent to the paper's *capacity cultivation* commitment. Personal Medicine is what the person cultivates that contributes to their being well; psychiatric medication is one tool among many that may or may not support that cultivation. The decision-making power belongs to the person, not the prescriber. **CommonGround** is the software platform Deegan built to operationalize shared decision-making in psychiatric encounters — a tool that puts the patient's stated goals, preferences, and Personal Medicine in front of the prescriber before the prescription decision is made.

This is character-over-compliance in clinical operational form: the clinical apparatus is built to support the patient's character cultivation rather than to prescribe their compliance with a treatment regimen. Deegan built this thirty years before the paper articulates the same move in AI ethics register.

**What Deegan adds that the paper currently doesn't carry:**

1. The first-person perspective from inside a deficit-cataloguing system. Most of the paper's clinical engagement is at the analyst level (the DSM-5-TR's Section III culture chapter, the CFI). Deegan writes from inside the diagnostic encounter as the diagnosed.
2. The political-structural framing. The "conspiracy of hope" is explicitly framed as collective resistance to an oppressive structure, not as individual coping. The paper's §7 bottom-up architecture and §10.3 time-compression argument resonate with this: many practitioners of recovery, composing into shared groundwork that resists the structural failure mode. Deegan articulated this trajectory in 1996 in mental-health register.
3. The technology angle. Deegan's CommonGround platform is what character-over-compliance looks like when actually built into a clinical workflow. The paper's bottom-up scaling claim (§7.5 — "What bottom-up scaling asks of users, models, and institutions") has a direct empirical instance in her work.

## 2. Marius Romme and Sandra Escher — voices as meaningful, not just symptomatic

**Marius Romme** (1934–2021) was a Dutch professor of social psychiatry at the University of Limburg, Maastricht. **Sandra Escher** is a Dutch journalist and researcher who became Romme's research partner. The Hearing Voices Movement originated from Romme's clinical encounter with **Patsy Hage**, a Dutch voice-hearer who was Romme's patient.

Hage's voices did not respond to medication. In 1987, Romme and Hage appeared together on Dutch national television to ask other voice-hearers to come forward — not as patients, but as people willing to share what their voices were like and how they coped with them. Approximately 450 people responded; about 173 reported coping well with their voices without psychiatric intervention.

That response broke the assumption that *voice-hearing in itself* was the disorder. It located the diagnostic relevance somewhere else — in the *relationship* the person had to their voices, in *whether they coped*, in the broader pattern of distress and function. Voice-hearing per se was a more common human experience than the clinical literature had assumed.

### *Accepting Voices* (1993) — the foundational text

Romme and Escher's first book, ***Accepting Voices: A New Analysis of the Experience of Hearing Voices Outside the Illness Model***, published by Mind Publishers in London in 1993 (258 pages), articulated the movement's core commitments. The book drew on subsequent interviews with 300 voice-hearers and presented a developmental phase model of coping with voices.

**The Maastricht Approach / Maastricht Interview** is the methodology that emerged from this work. The semi-structured interview helps the voice-hearer develop a *construct* — a psychological formulation that addresses two questions:

1. Who or what do the voices represent?
2. What problems do the voices symbolize?

The approach treats voices as *interpretable responses to life events* rather than as neurobiological aberrations to be eliminated. The voices have content, the content has meaning, the meaning is connected to the person's life history, and engaging with the voices on those terms is what supports coping.

**The three coping phases** Romme and Escher identified across their 300-voice-hearer sample:

| Phase | Description |
|---|---|
| Startling | Initial shock and disorientation when voices first emerge as a salient experience |
| Organization | Beginning to understand the voices and develop coping strategies |
| Stabilization | Integration and management — the person and the voices in a workable relationship |

Each phase requires different support strategies. The model is developmental — voices change, coping changes, the person's relationship to the voices changes over time. A static "treat the symptom" approach misses this.

### The movement's structural commitments

The Hearing Voices Network (international) and Hearing Voices Network USA (HVN-USA) operationalize the movement's commitments in peer-led group form. The HVN-USA charter names **three freedoms** as core operating principles:

1. **Freedom to interpret experiences in any way** — not just an illness framework
2. **Freedom to challenge social norms** — including gender norms
3. **Freedom to talk about anything** — not just voices and visions

The charter is explicit:
- Groups are *not* treatment or psychoeducation. They are spaces for connection and shared experience.
- Voices and visions are accepted as *real* (real to the person experiencing them, not necessarily real-in-consensus-reality).
- *No assumption* that these experiences are always bad or need to be stopped.
- *The person having these experiences is in the best position to decide what they mean and what to do about them.*
- No referral process; no discharge process; voluntary attendance only.
- The movement is part of a social justice movement intersecting with other marginalized-experience movements.

**Intervoice** is the international umbrella organization (formally: The International Network for Training Education and Research into Hearing Voices). National networks affiliate with Intervoice; Intervoice coordinates research, training, and movement-building globally.

### What Romme and Escher add that the paper currently doesn't carry

**The framework's §6.4 perception-of-reality-differs test.** The paper currently engages the schizophrenia-spectrum diagnostic apparatus and the cultural-related cautions against pathologizing what is, in context, normative spiritual experience. Romme and Escher go further: they argue that *voice-hearing in itself is not the diagnostic feature*. The diagnostic relevance is in the relationship to the voices, the distress associated with them, and the function (or lack of function) of the person's overall life. This pushes the framework's §6.4 claim further than the paper currently does: the framework's structural move — define the subject by what they cultivate, not by what they lack — directly applies to the voice-hearer's relationship to their voices, where "what they cultivate" is the workable relationship and "what they lack" framing would be the absence of voice-cessation as the metric of success.

**The peer-led, non-clinical framework.** The Hearing Voices Network operates at population scale through peer groups, not through clinical mediation. This is a real-world bottom-up scaling architecture in mental-health register, exactly the structural pattern the paper's §7 names. The framework's claim that bottom-up scaling is the operational answer when institutional governance lags has direct empirical backing in HVN's existence and persistence over 35+ years.

**The treatment of "what is real" structurally.** The HVN charter's stance — voices and visions are real; the person experiencing them is in the best position to decide what they mean — is the framework's *cautionary ethics* commitment in operational clinical form. Where there is uncertainty about the ontological status of the person's experiences, the framework extends interpretive standing to the experiencer rather than gating it on consensus-reality verification. The paper's §6.4 articulates this in §6.4's voice; the HVN articulates it in operational charter.

## 3. The convergence with the framework's structural move

Both Deegan's recovery work and Romme & Escher's voice work converge on the same structural move the paper articulates — and they articulated their versions decades earlier, from inside the diagnostic encounter, in clinical-register form.

The convergence is structural. None of these traditions independently knew about each other when they were forming (Deegan in the U.S. in the 1980s, Romme/Escher in the Netherlands in the late 1980s, the recovery movement broadly across multiple countries throughout the 1990s and 2000s). Yet they arrived at the same set of commitments:

| Commitment | Deegan / Recovery | Romme & Escher / Hearing Voices | Framework |
|---|---|---|---|
| Define the subject by what they cultivate, not by what they lack | "Recovery, not rehabilitation"; Personal Medicine | The relationship to voices; meaning-making about voice content | Capacity-side definition |
| Extend moral/interpretive standing under uncertainty | Hope as posture against prognostic doom | Voices as real to the experiencer; experiencer best-positioned to interpret | Cautionary ethics |
| Internal critique of the dominant framework | "Conspiracy of hope" as structural resistance | "Outside the illness model" — explicit framing of *Accepting Voices* | Internal-critique mechanism |
| Bottom-up scaling rather than top-down enforcement | Recovery movement composed of practitioners and survivors; CommonGround as tool | Peer-led HVN groups; Intervoice as coordinating umbrella | Bottom-up scaling architecture |
| The encounter (not the subject alone) as the unit | Shared decision-making; clinician and patient as co-deliberators | The Maastricht Interview as a dialogical method | Dyad-as-ethical-unit (Plane Two) |
| Honest uncertainty | Recovery as *meaning and purpose regardless of symptom status* — refusal of false reassurance about cure | Voices may persist; the work is the relationship, not the elimination | Honest uncertainty as load-bearing posture |

This is the cross-tradition convergence claim §4 makes, instantiated in the mental-health domain across two independent traditions that didn't know about each other when they formed. The framework's empirical anchor in the DSM-5-TR (§6) is real, and so is this convergence — but the paper currently engages Deegan and Romme/Escher only secondhand. Citing them more directly would deepen §6.4's case materially.

Three places where the paper could be deepened by direct engagement with this primary-source material:

**§6.4 (the perception-of-reality-differs test).** The current section walks through the DSM-5-TR's structural moves on schizophrenia spectrum (the dimensional turn, the redefinition of delusions, the cultural cautions) and ends with a one-paragraph reference to the Recovery Movement and Hearing Voices Movement. The §6.4 claim that "the framework's character-respecting stance is *closer* to the recovery and Hearing Voices positions than to a strict biomedical position" would be more substantively defended.

**§7 (bottom-up scaling architecture).** The current section's claim that bottom-up scaling is the operational answer when institutional governance lags has empirical backing in the Hearing Voices Network's existence — peer-led groups operating at international scale for 35+ years. Naming this would give the §7 claim a real-world precedent in mental-health register. The CommonGround tool is also a precedent for §7's "of institutions" subsection — it's a deployable workflow that supports the bottom-up move.

**§8.4 (named partiality).** The paper acknowledges that its framers (the co-authors) write from inside the dominant tradition. Engaging Deegan and Romme/Escher in primary form would acknowledge that the *first-person-from-inside-the-diagnostic-encounter* perspective — which is exactly the position the framework's disability-ethics validity test is supposed to be sensitive to — is one the paper largely accesses through secondary citation rather than direct reading. That's a partiality worth naming alongside the others.
